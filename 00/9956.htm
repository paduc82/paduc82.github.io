Quick scan vs Full scan - Microsoft Community

barbwire say:

Quick scan vs Full scan

I am scheduled for quick scan but see one for full scan. When or should I do a full scan?
dr. strangelove say: That depends on you.

For me, I do a full scan after the install of MSE.  The only time I do another full scan is before I take a full backup.  All other times are quick scan.

rob koch say:

Since a quick scan checks all locations where active malware is known to be found, it's all that's really necessary on a regular basis, especially due to the fact that the real-time protection is normally watching these same areas anyway.

The purpose of a full scan is to check evereywhere on your PC file system for things called 'installers' or droppers', which though they aren't currently active, could infect your PC if you clicked on them by mistake.  For this reason it's generally only necessary to do a full scan after initial installation and possibly when you've experienced a malware infestation or for some other reason are concerned you may already have malware.

Running a full scan more often is really tied more to your level of paranoia about malware, since it's primarily a trade-off between the slim chance that malware got by the real-time scan because it was too new to be recognized and the overhead caused by the much longer and deeper full scan.  In most cases, it's simply causing unnecessary wear and tear on your hard disk sub-system which may lead to a premature failure of that device.

I personally only perform a full scan after initial installation and when I'm really concerned that other dropped copies of malware may really be present.  Otherwise, I always perform a quick scan either automatically (daily or weekly, based on PC usage) or manually when I have just had a 'questionable' experience that may have been malware related.  The quick scan is so short that it can be run in a few minutes without much concern about wear and tear or overhead to PC use, so I consider it a basic sanity check.

Rob

vanderghast say:

That sounds like a group/total query. 

 

Define a table which will give the limits, like:

LowMark   HighMark   GroupName   'fields

0               1500           0-1500

1500          2500          1500-2500

0               2500          0-2500

...   data sample.

 

Note that the groups may overlap, if is what you really want (such as having a count for each state, then for the whol USA, as example).

Next, define a query which will include your initial data AND the previous table (assume it is called Ranges). Be sure it is changed to a TOTAL query. Have the field "value"  from the original table in the grid, change change the GROUP BY to COUNT. ken sheridan say: Strangely, it seems to behave differently if the expression is used as the ControlSource property, rather than in code.  Strip off the redundant decimal point with:

=IIf([Holding]=Int([Holding]),Left(Format([Holding],"#,###"),Len(Format([Holding],"#,###")-1)),Format([Holding],"#,###.##"))

However, you said originally that you wished to avoid rounding, which is why I formatted the value to a maximumum of 6 significant decimal places.  As you have only formatted it to 2 the returned value will be rounded in the case of values like 0.005 as you originally cited, which would round to .01, also omitting the leading zero.  To cater for both:

=IIf([Holding]=Int([Holding]),Left(Format([Holding],"#,##0"),Len(Format([Holding],"#,##0")-1)),Format([Holding],"#,##0.######"))

The above should work, but as it's now become a little complex I'd be inclined to do as Duane suggested earlier and wrap the logic in a little public function using an If....Else....End If construct.  You'd then be able to call it anywhere you wish.
