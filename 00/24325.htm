1) The font change applies to text after subscripts and superscripts and changes in font size, and basically any change in font properties within the same cell. For instance, ex: F_d = m*a; the font would change starting right before "d".

2) Excel changes the font to whatever properties you set in your "Normal" style font. (In styles, right-click the style and click "Modify" to see the properties.)

3) If you open the file through Excel (rather than directly through the desktop or folder), the font issue does not occur. If you have Excel open already (i.e. a new workbook), the font issue does not occur in other files when you open them directly through the desktop or folder. So definitely a software startup issue. 

This has worked for me and several other people.

Yes you can. All Office 2007 have portable licences. Depending on the history of the activations, you might have to activate by telephone.

dan at it associates say:

The correct answer to your question depends upon the type of license you purchased.

 

If you purchased Office 2007 with an "FPP" product designation, then your Software Licensing Terms permit you to move the license to a new computer.  If you purchased Office 2007 in the form of traditional packaged full retail or upgrade retail discs, you have an FPP license.  If you purchased Office 2007 as a direct purchase and download from Microsoft (either the now-defunct Windows Marketplace or the current Microsoft Store), then you have an FPP license.

 

If you purchased an OEM (original equipment manufacturer) license or an MLK (medialess licensing kit) license, unfortunately, these licenses are permanently tied to the computer on which they were first activated and are not permitted to be moved to any other computer for any reason.  The license lives and dies with that original computer.

dan at it associates say:

Assuming the license is one that permits moving from the old to the new, simply install on the new.

 

Depending upon how long the license was installed on the old computer, you may or may not encounter an error message along the lines of "this product has been installed too many times."  (Based on the info you have provided I do not expect that you will encounter it.)  If you do, rerun the activation wizard but this time, choose the option to activate by telephone, and follow the prompts.

 

Just as an FYI on the old dead computer--assuming it's not operational enough to let you run Windows and uninstall Office from the computer, I would suggest that you remove the hard disk drive before disposing of the computer in order to protect any sensitive personal data that would have collected on the hard disk over the years of use of the computer.
