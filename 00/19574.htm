Truth be told, they didn’t make scheduling a scan more difficult in Windows 8/8.1/10 – they made it completely unnecessary.

The Windows Defender Scheduled Scan task (a Quick Scan) is now run daily by Automatic Maintenance.

The scheduling options have disappeared from the Defender interface because the scheduled scan no longer requires any kind of setup at all – it just runs automatically like the definition updates.

On most systems, the Automatic Maintenance scan runs with near perfect reliability – which is a far cry from the MSE scheduled scan, which just sits there and refuses to run indefinitely if the PC isn’t on and awake at the scheduled time.

In addition, the Automatic Maintenance scan only runs when the PC is idle, so you don’t have to settle for half of the system’s resources while your scheduled scan is running (like you do with MSE at the default CPU setting).

You get the full power of your PC every time you start using it – because Automatic Maintenance immediately halts the scan and waits for you to take a break before restarting it.

So the procedure that we’re discussing here isn’t something that’s required in order to get a “scheduled” scan in Windows 10.

That happens automatically.

What we’re talking about is scheduling a redundant Quick Scan that runs with normal priority and starts precisely at the specified time (or scheduling a Full Scan).

That’s completely optional.

A scan scheduled with the Create Basic Task Wizard provides us with the full Task Scheduler option-set, so I’ve been recommending it as a replacement for the onboard MSE scheduled scan from the get-go.

  • It starts precisely on time
  • It runs at full speed
  • It can wake the computer to run the scan, or run the scan at next startup, for real reliability
  • It can keep the PC awake for the entire duration of an unattended Full Scan
  • It provides the option to shut the PC down at the end of the scan

http://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/protect/forum/mse-protect_start/running-mse-full-scan-as-a-scheduled-task/f627f5ce-0b74-4b1d-be64-d6ceeb4d9b02

In short, the scheduled scan that you set up with the Create Basic Task Wizard fixes everything that’s wrong with the MSE scheduled scan – and it likewise provides for a full-featured Windows Defender scheduled scan.

Most people who claim that the Create Basic Task Wizard is too “difficult” are just repeating the mantra, and have never actually tried it.

The Create Basic Task Wizard is a highly-simplified walkthrough for setting up a scheduled task – designed especially for novice Windows users.

But if you want something over and above the Automatic Maintenance scheduled scan – and you’re overwhelmed at the very thought of setting up a scheduled task, then I think you should probably consider just replacing Windows Defender with one of the free alternatives.

Most of the competitors fare better in lab testing, and some of them have "extras" that might turn out to be useful. I haven’t really had time to investigate the way they handle scheduled scans, but my impression is that most of the third-party AV apps still handle this in the conventional way – with onboard scheduling options rather than with Automatic Maintenance.

So that might be the way to go.

terfar say:

Not really as such as a line can be part of a sentence or several short sentences, so there’s really no logic to define a line.

However, if the insertion point is at the beginning or end of the line, then Shift+Home or Shift+End will select from the insertion point to the beginning or end of the line.

If the insertion point is somewhere else in the line of text, Home, Shift+End will select the line too.

But if you have to take your hand off the keyboard to move the cursor to the line of text with the mouse, you may as well click in the left margin to select it.

sven johannsen say:

Quick points since this might be seen. The current used to charge a device is determined by the device, not the charger. You could charge off a 5v car battery if such a thing existed*. The current capacity of a charger is what it is designed to deliver, at most. If you try to charge a device that wants 1.5 or 2 amps with a 1 amp charger, the charger will in some cases get hot, and the duration of charge will certainly be longer.

 

Some devices are able to determine if they are connected to a PC, .5A max output (500mA, at least on typical ports), and if they are connected to a wall charger. They may have the ability to draw more current on the latter to charge faster, and may get a bit warmer. In no situation should the device draw more current of any charger than the cable that came with it can support.

 

 5volts is important though.

 

*Think about lights in a car for example. Headlights and the little dash lights are both hooked to that big old battery that can supply enough current to crank over the engine when starting. The little lights don't pop, they just draw the current they need.
