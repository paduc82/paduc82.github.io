jason (office engineer) say:

Hello Garav,

There are two links you can share.

One is the normal link that you as the author use to open the sway.  When this link is opened, if the person is signed in as an author of the Sway, they will get the edit view, otherwise they will get the view-only page.

The other link is an edit link and can only be grabbed from the share dropdown.  When someone follows this link, they are given the option to become an author on the Sway or just remain a viewer of the Sway.  Upon making their choice, they are redirected back to the first link.

If you are the owner of a Sway, you will always be an author of it.  However, if you have been invited by the owner to be a co-author, you can always choose to remove yourself as an author of the Sway later (there is a button o the My Sways page to do this).

Let me know if you have further questions about this.

    cataholic say:
    krxx wrote: Ok, but there is just a 200mA difference between N8&808 batteries and the 808 charger is 1,625x powerful...I think that there should be a more complex explanation

    Assuming that it is able to produce the maximum charge current that the battery accepts then the "power" of the charger is not related to the charge time at all.

     

    I do not know the charge algorithms that Nokia uses, but typically when a Li-ion (or Li-Polymer, which is the same chemistry as Li-Ion) battery is charged the charge current is first limited to somewhere around 0.8x (may be a bit more or less) the capacity of the battery, so may be around 1150mA for a 1400mAhr battery. This constant current charge rate is only maintained until the battery cell voltage reaches about 4.2v at which time the charge goes into constant voltage mode. Then as the battery charge continues to increase the charge current decreases (voltage remains constant) from that maximum rate down until the current approachs zero, at which time the battery is considered charged. So a great deal of the charge time is at a current much less than the maximum charge current.

     

    This drop off in current is not linear (it drops off more quickly to start with so a graph of it is a concave curve), but if we were to assume it was linear, for the sake of an easy example, then for much of the charge time the charge rate would be half of the maximum charge rate. So taking the case of the 1150mA charge rate I mentioned above as an example, for much of the charge time (the time after the cell voltage reaches approx. 4.2v) the average of the charge current would be 575mA.

     

    So charge times are much longer than a simple comparison between the charger's maximum current capability and the battery capacity would indicate. I think that you will also find that because of the shape of the curve as charge current decreases during the constant voltage portion of the charge that a 1400mAhr battery will also take noticably longer to charge than the 20% longer that a simple comparison of battery capacities between a 1400mAhr and 1200mAhr battery might infer.

     

    As I say that is the normal type of charge regime and I assume that Nokia uses something similar. Regardless, the charge times you are quoting don't seem abnormal to me.
