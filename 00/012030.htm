chip pearson say:

You need to create a procedure to set focus to the name box and  the assign a keyboard shortcut to that procedure. In a regular code module, paste the following code:

Private Declare Function SetFocus Lib "user32" (ByVal hwnd As Long) As Long
Private Declare Function FindWindow Lib "user32" Alias "FindWindowA" _
    (ByVal lpClassName As String, ByVal lpWindowName As String) As Long
Private Declare Function FindWindowEx Lib "user32" Alias "FindWindowExA" _
    (ByVal hWnd1 As Long, ByVal hWnd2 As Long, _
     ByVal lpsz1 As String, ByVal lpsz2 As String) As Long
 
Sub SetFocusNameBox()
    Dim Res As Long
    Res = SetFocus( _
        FindWindowEx( _
            FindWindowEx( _
                FindWindow("XLMAIN", Application.Caption) _
                    , 0, "EXCEL;", vbNullString) _
                    , 0, "combobox", vbNullString))
End Sub

Then, get out of VBA and return to Excel. On the Developer tab of the ribbon, click the Macros button, select SetFocusNameBox in the list and then click the Options button. There, assign the keyboard shortcut. I use CTRL SHIFT N. If you put the code in the Personal.xlsm workbook, or any other workbook that is always opened when you start Excel, the shortcut will always be available. See alsohttp://www.cpearson.com/Excel/NameBoxShortcut.htm

NOTE: This code has not been tested on the 64-bit version of Office and may not work under 64 bit Excel. It does work with 64-bit Windows and 32-bit Excel.

marco_mi. say:

Hi reinerchamp,

 

Let me help you with your concern. Please do the following steps to minimize or to stop receiving unwanted emails.

 

A. Scheduled Sweep - automatically lets you keep only the latest mail from a sender or have their emails be moved to deleted folder after 30, 60, or 90 days.

1. Open the email you would like to filter.
2. Click Scheduled Clean-up beside sender's name
3. On the pop-up window, choose your preferred option
4. Click OK.

B. Rules - we can create a rule that can block a specific word from the subject of the spam email.

1. Sign in to your Microsoft account. Go to Inbox.
2. Click on Gear icon then More mail options... at the upper right corner of the page.
3. Under the Customizing Outlook section, click on Rules for sorting new messages.
4. Click on the New button.
5. Under Step 1: Which messages do you want this rule to apply to, 
    a. First drop-down menu - choose Subject
    b. Second drop-down menu - choose contains
    c. Third field - input the word you would like to block
6. Under Step 2: What action do you want to apply, click on Delete these messages
7. Click Save button.

C. Sweep - This will allow all received or even future emails coming from the specific sender be deleted.

1. Sign in to your Microsoft account. Go to Inbox.
2. Check the specific sender you would like to filter.
3. On the upper part, click Sweep
4. Choose Delete all from
5. On the pop-up box, just click Delete all.

Note: You can also block future messages from that sender by checking the box beside Also block future messages.

 

 

We also recommend that you follow these guidelines to help reduce junk e-mail:

 

* Don't reply to junk e-mail, even to ask to be removed from the sender's mailing list. The sender may simply use that response to confirm that your e-mail address is valid, and continue to send you junk e-mail. To report the message as junk e-mail, select the message, click Junk.

 

* To report the e-mail message as a suspected phishing scam (Falsely claiming to be a legitimate business to send you to a fake website or scam you into giving out private information.), click Mark as - Phishing scam.

 

* Remove your e-mail address from any web page, newsgroup, or bulletin board available on the Internet.

 

* To remove your e-mail address from newsletters or mailing lists that are on your safe senders list, you can click Unsubscribe. Microsoft notifies the people who created the newsletter to stop sending it to you. 

 

Note: If you receive junk e-mail that looks as if it was sent from your Outlook.com account, someone may have spoofed (Sending messages with fake or stolen account information) your account information.

 

If you have any question or clarification, don't hesitate to reply.

 

Thanks!

jay freedman say:
It is not possible in an InputBox or a MsgBox. It is possible by using a UserForm (http://gregmaxey.mvps.org/word_tip_pages/create_employ_userform.html).

John Walkenbach's article at http://j-walk.com/ss/excel/tips/tip71.htm shows how to make a label on a UserForm act like a hyperlink. Modifying his code to work in Word instead of Excel, it would look like

Private Sub Label1_Click()
    Dim Link As String
    Link = "http://www.google.com"
    On Error GoTo NoCanDo
    ActiveDocument.FollowHyperlink Address:=Link, NewWindow:=True
    Me.Hide
    Exit Sub
NoCanDo:
    MsgBox "Cannot open " & Link
End Sub

You can make the UserForm look like an InputBox or MsgBox, or you can make it more complex -- for example, it could collect more than a single piece of information and populate multiple fields in the document.

Serendipity! I've found a major problem for anyone dual-booting Windows 10 and Linux.

I was occasionally getting FSCK0000.REC and other files sequentially named from there (FSCK0001.REC, FSCK0002.REC, etc.). I knew one or the other OSes was "cleaning" these files, but I didn't know which OS was causing the problem, which one was doing the cleaning, nor did I know what I could do to stop it. Now I have verified the problem every step of the way:
1) run Windows 10 with the default fast startup enabled (This is the way it is unless you've already changed this setting)
2) shut down Windows
3) boot Linux and create files on a shared partition (Fat32 for me)
4) reboot into Windows
5) Voila! The files you created are gone, collected by Windows into the .rec files.

6) Boot back into Linux and check the root of the shared drive. There you'll find the .rec files. Apparently it is Linux that is collecting the orphaned files, but it is Windows 10 and its hibernation-as-shutdown treatment of the disk that is causing the files to be orphaned to begin with.

The way to fix this is to:
1) unmount the shared volume before shutting down Windows (I don't see how to do this from Windows for a non-removable shared drive)
or
2) use "restart" instead of "shut down" from the Windows menu (Windows does a more complete shutdown if you choose "restart" than if you choose "shutdown")
or
3) permanently fix the problem by disabling fast startup. This is the option I will explain below.

I figured this out while searching for how to use exFAT drives with both Windows and Linux. This was a very lucky find as I had searched for my particular problem with the .rec files before, but I had not found a solution. The two issues were unrelated for me.

From this page: http://www.howtogeek.com/236807/how-to-mount-your-windows-10-or-8-system-drive-on-linux/

'If you dont want to worry about this and would just like to disable hybrid boot entirely to make your life a bit easier at the cost of a slower Windows boot process, of course you can do that. ...'

'To do this, boot into Windows, launch the Control Panel, and click Hardware and Sound. Click Change what the power buttons do under Power Options. (or find "Power Options"  and choose "What the buttons do.)'

'At the top of the window that appears, click the Change settings that are currently unavailable link. Scroll down and uncheck the Turn on fast start-up (recommended) option. Click Save changes to save your changes.'

Now, you can shutdown normally and reboot either OS. And shut down is actually faster for me now: 10 seconds as opposed to 20 seconds.

Update: Apparently dropbox, and maybe other cloud services, provides a workaround for this. If I create files in a dropbox-synced folder on the shared drive while in Linux, then shutdown, immediately after Windows boots I get a notification that the files already synced have just been downloaded. It seems the dropbox app doesn't treat them as being deleted when Windows 10 boots; it sees them as having never been on the machine, so it downloads them.

I have not checked to see if fragments of the original creation under linux remain and are collected upon a reboot into Linux, but my guess is that they are. You'd then have two copes of each file created under linux in a dropbox-linked folder: one normal as restored by Windows and one fragment collected by Linux.
