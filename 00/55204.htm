Hi samwaltonjr,

Error 0x8CCC0003 and 0x8CCC0002 seems to be pointing at a non-Hotmail or non-Live email account configured with Windows Live Mail which attempts to send an email with pictures or photo albums.

When Windows Live Mail sends pictures and albums, it automatically generates a photo album in Windows Live SkyDrive which is another service/feature available to those who signed up an account with Windows Live. This becomes a problem for non-Hotmail or non-Live accounts configured with Live Mail because their account can't connect to SkyDrive for the albums to be created.

I'd recommend that you attach the files using Insert > Single Photo in the ribbon menu. You still select multiple photos using that command but it won't be sent as an album, or an album won't be created in Windows Live SkyDrive.

Another way is to attach the photos. It won't display the photos in the email, but it will be sent as attachments to the email.

If you have no bootable XP media (or are not sure what you have) create a bootable XP Recovery Console CD and be sure.

The Windows Setup... will proceed.

Select the installation you want to access (usually  1: C:\WINDOWS)

You may be asked to enter the Administrator password (usually empty).

You should be in the C:\WINDOWS folder.  This is the same as the

chkdsk C: /r

You do not have to adjust the BIOS again to boot on the HDD since the CD will not be present.

Sleep mode interrupted by Media Center IR Receiver

Hauppauge PVR-500 MCE dual-analog TV tuner
2x WD 7200RPM 320GB SATA2 hard drives

USB accessories include:
Microsoft Wireless Comfort Desktop (Wireless Comfort Keyboard 1.0a & Wireless Optical Mouse 2.0 with one wireless receiver)
Microsoft Xbox 360 Wireless controller receiver
WD MyBook 1TB USB2.0 external HDD
Cavalier 320GB USB2.0 external HDD
Microsoft Media Center Remote Receiver (IR receiver and 2 blaster connectors) v1.0a, Model 1040

The issue at hand is regarding how the computer behaves in relation to the specific Microsoft branded Media Center USB Receiver.

Originally I had my dual tuner set up to receive analog signals, and used my Xbox 360 as a Media Center Extender, with the Microsoft Media Center remote, and had the USB remote receiver away in storage for many years (I purchased it back in XP MCE2005 era, for the IR Media Center Keyboard, which I still have). Comcast had changed all analog channels (except for the first 20) over to digital, and many of my TV recordings failed, so I canceled most of them and just lived with the first 20 channels. But recently I had shows that started coming on digital-only channels that I wanted to record & watch. I have a small "DTA" that Comcast brought out to me, and hooked it up to my computer's TV tuner card. However, I also knew I'd need to use the USB IR receiver/blaster to have Media Center be able to control the channels. I hooked all the hardware up, and was able to set up the TV signal and IR cable properly. My shows have been recording on-time and on the right channels with no problems. However, I noticed that ever since plugging in the USB IR receiver, my computer refuses to go to sleep properly. Before connecting the cable, it would sleep after 30 mins to an hour of being idle, and wake when necessary to record shows, and go back to sleep after complete. When launching the extender software on the Xbox 360, the Xbox would send a WOL magic packet that would properly wake the computer from sleep, or hibernation, or even from complete power-off, so that the Xbox could connect to Media Center. Since adding the USB IR Receiver, the computer refuses to sleep properly. The sequence of events are usually as follows:

Computer reaches standby time-out, or is attempted to be placed into sleep-mode manually. After a few moments of writing the memory to hard drive (Hybrid sleep IS enabled on this desktop, for reliability reasons, our power isn't very reliable in winter), the system will power off the hard drives, turn off the fans, and the sound of the whole system entering sleep (parked HDD heads, fans winding down, etc.) can be heard, but only briefly (less than a second) before the whole system powers back up and dumps me back to my login screen within a few seconds. A subsequent attempt at forcing the computer into sleep, or the additional sleep timeout being reached again, results in the computer going into what seems to be a successful sleep mode (HDD drive heads parked, fans off, silence as usual with S3 sleep mode). However, when attempting to wake via Magic Packet, nothing worked, via Xbox or via a Magic Packet from my router (which I use extensively to wake the computer remotely when away from home). Take note, these methods worked to wake up the computer flawlessly BEFORE inserting the Microsoft USB IR Receiver. The only method of waking the computer is via the power button, which at least that DOES work.

Researching online, I read the KB article, KB972664, which documents this exact sequence of events for the initial attempt at sleep mode, where it fails and immediately wakes back up, but does not mention the second sleep attempt, where it goes to sleep and can't be woken up. Taking the steps mentioned there, I checked BIOS settings, and settings within Device Manager, disabling any power management related "Allow this device to wake computer from sleep" settings under any USB devices that would affect this, such as the keyboard, mouse, and USB IR Receiver. However, the issue still persists. I have tried checking my power management settings, and wake-timers are enabled, as is the ability to wake via magic packet, which is the only real time I want to be able to wake the computer outside of using the power button (which absolutely will wake the computer after the second attempt puts the computer into a "forced" sleep). So, any suggestions?
