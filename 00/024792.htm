sven johannsen say:

The 1320 is a Windows Phone based device, not a Symbian based device. It does not work with Symbian apps/ PC interface software. It works with Windows Phone apps and Windows Phone connectivity software. There is a big difference in the philosophy of mobile devices, not just WP, these days. Where they used to be a 'partner' to a desktop, where all the information resided, and sync'd back and forth via cable, they now typically expect the information to be based in the 'cloud' and the phone and PC interact with the data independently. It actually keeps your data more up to date, and frees you from having to connect to your PC, and only being in sync if you are at your PC.

The information in the cloud for WP is normally stored in a Microsoft account associated with a Hotmail.com, or Outlook.com address. Contact and calendar information can be entered conveniently into the web interface from a PC, and will sync to Windows Phones, or even Android and iPhones, pointed at the account. It can be done from an Outlook interface on a PC as well. If you have information already in another application (Ovi-Suite) it may be possible to export the information to a file format that Outlook.com can import, possibly a comma separated value text file.

Map updates done over WiFi are for maps that are resident on the phone, to allow for off-line (no radio connection) navigation. (You can get a $99 Lumia 520, download some maps, and have a fully functioned GPS navigation capability without ever having a phone account, or data service) You can use maps, off the air, over the data network whenever you wish. Off-line maps tend to be large and most would not wish them downloaded/updated over a metered connection.

P.S. Nokia makes phones using several OSs, WP, ASHA and even Android with the NokiaX. Doesn't mean that that all Nokia phones all use the same apps and software. They aren't alone in this. Samsung and HTC make phones with different OSs that aren't compatible with each other as well.

steve rindsberg say:

Try launching another (lightweight) program like Notepad and leave it up while you work with PPT.  When the crash occurs, can you Alt+Tab to the other program or is the computer totally locked up?

If totally locked up, it's very possible that you have a hardware problem that gets aggravated when PPT stresses the computer's hardware (which it tends to do more than many programs).  You mention that you hear the fan rev up just before this happens ... it might be that the fan's not starting early enough and something's overheating and causing the lockup.

Your power settings may give you some measure of control over how soon the fan kicks in.  That's worth looking at.  Also check the vents for obstructions (but don't use canned air to blow any accumulated crud out ... that can fry the fan). 

If the vents are on the bottom, try propping the computer up to give better airflow. 

And generally, keep an eye on the temperature of the computer, try to determine whether the problem arises more often when it's very warm.
