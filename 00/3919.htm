It's not a bug, it's an informational notice that content in the presentation can't be displayed by your version.

Since there is nothing specific in the message, to find out what content is causing it, you'll have to take an iterative approach:

  1. In PowerPoint Online, break the presentation into 2 halves, then open each in PowerPoint for iOS to see which gives a message.
  2. For the part that raises an message, break that in half again and test each half.
  3. Keep going until you have 1 slide that creates the message.
  4. Delete elements one at a time, opening the slide each time in PowerPoint for iOS.
  5. When the message stops appearing, the last element you deleted was the one that couldn't be displayed.

Task   :   Received Date    :   Completed Date

I can capture if the tasks were wholly with the date range with =countifs(Received Date,"<="&A1,Completed Date,">="&B1)

But I can't quite figure the logic out for capturing the tasks that are started:

1. before Received Date and finished before Completed date; and

2. after Received Date and finished after Completed Date

Is there a neat way of doing this or is it just a case of messy AND and OR's? 

Thanks again 

Susan

joeu2004 say:

Your terminology is confusing, specifically "Receive Date" and "Completed Date".  I think you mean Start Period (A1) and End Period (B1) respectively.

Wholly between Start and End Periods:

=COUNTIFS(recvDates, ">="&A1, cmpltDates, "<="&B1)

Received during Start and End Period and not finished (I assume cmpltDate is empty or the null string if not finished):

=COUNTIFS(recvDates, ">="&A1, cmpltDates, "")

Received before Start Period and finished before End Period:

=COUNTIFS(recvDates, "<"&A1, cmpltDates, "<"&B1

Received after Start Period and finished after End Period:

=COUNITFS(recvDates, ">"&A1, cmpltDates, ">"&B1)

Note:  We cannot use AND and OR with COUNTIFS, "messy" or not.

Yes.  But because you neglected to provide actual numbers, I cannot be specific.

First, some Excel calculations differ from VBA calculations because Excel converts pairwise operations to 64-bit binary floating-point, whereas VBA tries to use the 80-bit binary floating-point results of Intel-compatible CPUs.  In other words, sometimes VBA calculations have greater precision.

Second, sometimes Excel does indeed recognize infinitesimal arithmetic results and arbitrarily replaces them with zero.  (Sigh.)  This is the dubious "close to zero" heuristic that is poorly described in http://support.microsoft.com/kb/78113.  It is "dubious" because it is implemented inconsistently in Excel.  And thankfully, it is not implemented at all in VBA.

IMHO, you should not rely on that heuristic.  I take extra steps to avoid it when I suspect it might be a factor.

Third, you do not say which "sin" functions you use.  Theoretically, the VBA Sin function results might differ from the Excel SIN function results for some values.  That might also be true even for WorksheetFunction.Sin.

I don't know that they do differ.  But they are physically different implementations -- perhaps even different algorithms.  So, their arithmetic properties might differ in infinitesimal ways, which seems to be your concern.

First, bear in mind that computer arithmetic is not the same as mathematical arithmetic.  Mathematical arithmetic has infinite precision.  Computer arithmetic has finite precision.

Second, 5E-15 is very close to zero in computer arithmetic.

Finally, I would round the iterative calculation of t.  This has nothing to do with Excel v. VBA or with rules of engineering math precision.   But it could impact the outcome in both Excel and VBA due to the finite precision of computer arithmetic.

I'm just going to add this little adage regarding accuracy of measurement and calculation:  what you have in the real world (usually) is a case of measure it with a micrometer, mark it with chalk, cut it with an axe in reverse.  Typically your measurements are much rougher than the precision of the computer.  Consider determining the distance between two lat/long pairs from a large scale map:  the ink-dot you mark each point with on the map may be several hundred feet in diameter (assuming a round dot), and then there's the question/issue of parallax when you view the line between the two, or the accuracy of horizontal/vertical measurement back to obtain the coordinates.  So you take two values that may include hundreds of feet of error and toss them into a computer and declare that the two points are 3,214.004 meters from one another.  Your actual measurement may only be accurate to a meter or two at best while you are 'claiming' accuracy to millimeters.  It is this kind of error that results in safety margins built into some things such as airways (those highways in the sky), something I worked with for years; literally miles wide to allow for errors in the initial design phase plus equipment and human error in actual use.

Instead, Excel arbitrarily limits numeric input to the first 15 significant digits, treating additional digits to the right as zero.  And it formats only up to 15 significant digits.

FYI, VBA correctly converts 17-digit data entry.  But it formats type Double only up to 15 significant digits.  (Surprise!)

lcaretto wrote:
For each time the values of x and y are computed from the equations x = V0 cos(q) t and y = v0 sin(q) t - gt^2/2.

In Intel-compatible CPUs, values are stored in memory using 64-bit binary floating-point representation.  But arithmetic is performed using 80-bit binary floating-point representation, and the results are stored in 80-bit registers.

In other words, arithmetic is performed with greater precision than can be stored in memory.

In Excel, you write (I added the "redundant" -0):
x:  =$B$1*COS($B$3)*B11
y:  =$B$1*SIN($B$3)*B11-$B$4*B11^2/2-0

Excel converts the 80-bit result of each pairwise operation to 64-bit.

In VBA, you write (I made some prudent name changes):
x:  xVal = v0 * Cos(theta * degreesToRadians) * myTime
y:  yVal = v0 * Sin(theta * degreesToRadians) * myTime - g * myTime ^ 2 / 2

[ERRATA] Changed the function names from myX and myY to xVal and yVal, following my own advice to avoid Excel column names.  Klunk!

Sin(theta * degreesToRadians) returns a 64-bit result.  Otherwise, VBA tries to use the intermediate 80-bit results from each pairwise operation.

So VBA arithmetic typically has greater precision than Excel.  That can cause differences between Excel and VBA results.

If you want VBA arithmetic results to be the same as Excel, you need to mimic Excel's conversion of pairwise operations to 64-bit.

Moreover, VBA Cos and Sin might be implemented differently than Excel COS and SIN.  The VBA implementation might have greater precision.  And the implementations might differ in many other significant ways.

Alternatively, use WorksheetFunction.Cos and WorksheetFunction.Sin.  Although they, too, are physically different from the Excel functions, they might more closely mimic the Excel functions, since that is their design goal.

However, in the final analysis, it appears that either the VBA and Excel results are the same, or any differences do not have a significant effect for your particular example.

(I did make any effort to compare the results of VBA Cos and Sin and Excel COS and SIN.)



Some final thoughts....

I wouldn't be surprised if this has been overwhelming.  Forgive me if it is.  It's a lot to digest.  But sometimes simple questions require complex explanations.

IMHO, you should not expect non-integer arithmetic to have exact results.  And you should not expect Excel and VBA non-integer arithmetic to have exactly the same results.  Generally, it is not worth the trouble to try to make them agree.

As I have noted above, most VBA arithmetic is performed with higher precision.  So differences should be expected.

Assuming that algorithms are fundamentally correct, differences should not be interpreted as errors.  Neither Excel nor VBA is more correct, even if sometimes one or the other produces the exact results that you expected.

Whew!  I'm exhausted from writing all this.  I'm sure you feel the same about reading it.

The instructions that Doug linked to are for Word 2003 and earlier, but don't apply to Word 2007 or 2010. Because POSTNET bar codes  are no longer useful, the bar code check box in the Envelopes dialog was removed.
