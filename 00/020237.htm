FWIW, I've found by coincidence what was the apparent reason for my problem: it seems the E71 cannot deal with using different certificates for the same host.

This is a common rounding anomaly that arises because of the way that Excel represents time and numbers (64-bit binary floating-point).  I will explain the details below.  But the simple answer is:  the formula in C should be rounded to the correct hh:mm representation.  You can do that one of two ways:

=--TEXT(B2-A2+(A2>B2),"[h]:mm")
or
=ROUND((B2-A2+(A2>B2))*1440,0)/1440

I prefer the first method.  The double negative (--) converts the text result to numeric time.

FYI, 1440 is the number of minutes in a day.  This assumes your times do not include unformatted seconds.  [ERRATA]

Your other formulas are fine as is.

Details....

The constant time 2:00 is represented internally exactly by the number 0.0833333333333333,2870740406406184774823486804962158203125.

But "22:00"-"20:00" results in the number 0.0833333333333332,593184650249895639717578887939453125.

And "20:00"-"18:00" results in the number 0.0833333333333333,7034076748750521801412105560302734375.

(Excel formats only the first 15 significant digits, making the results appear to be 0.0833333333333333 and 0.0833333333333334 respectively.  That might lead you to the incorrect conclusion that "22:00"-"20:00" results in the correct number, making the results in column D even more mysterious.)

As you can see, neither is exactly the same as the number that represents 2:00.

Your formula in column C results in the numbers 1.99999999999999,82236431605997495353221893310546875 for "22:00"-"20:00" and 2.00000000000000,088817841970012523233890533447265625 for "20:00"-"18:00".

As you can see, the number for "22:00"-"20:00" is indeed less than 2.  So your formula in column D correctly rounds down to 1.75, the next lowest multiple of 0.25.

The number in column C for "20:00"-"18:00" is over 2.  So you do not encounter a problem with the formula in column D.  But that is only by coincidence.

It is almost impossible to predict when you will get bitten by these binary floating-point arithmetic anomalies.  So it is always prudent to round results when you expect a certain degree of precision.

However, with Excel time representation, it is imprudent, IMHO, to choose an arbitrary number of decimal digits to round to.  We can always find examples and situations where any arbitrary choice does not work consistently.

jllatham say:

Appears to be caused by the inability of a digital computer to exactly represent analog numbers.  This is a well known and well documented problem.  It usually rears its ugly head when dealing with currency, but this time it is showing up in the time calculations.  One reasonably "plain english" explanation is provided at:
http://www.cpearson.com/excel/rounding.htm

If you take the =B2-A2+IF(A2>B2,1) formula and put it into another cell and format it as general and then show all the decimal places it comes out to 0.0833333333333333  but the one on row 3 is 0.0833333333333334  and then this carries on out into the other formulas.

One fix - not saying it is the fix:

In C2:  =ROUND(B2-A2,5)+IF(A2>B2,1)

In D2: =ROUND((C2-INT(C2))*24,2)

In E2: no change and the result in both E2 and E3 is 2
