For COUNTIF on a filtered list -

I will take the example of =COUNTIF(F3:F6,">0.5") and remaining ones, you can do on your own following the same logic -

=SUMPRODUCT(SUBTOTAL(3,OFFSET(F3,ROW(F3:F6)-ROW(F3),))*(F3:F6>0.5))

palmspringsjo say:

REMOVING A PROBLEMATIC MICROSOFT UPDATE. . .

(Note that my experiences for which this process worked to resolve the problem involved:  Windows 7 XP Pro, Office 2010, updates might be labeled for either Office 2010 or Outlook 2010, and might be labeled "update" or "security update). . . .

1)  Have an idea in mind of an approximate date that you received the update from Mirosoft.

2)  FIND IT:  Start >> Windows Search by folders.  Go to C/Windows/Software Distribution.  In the right-side window, you'll see a Notepad text document icon labeled "Reporting Events".  Click on that.  My list is by date, with the most recent at the bottom.  Search around those and look for a KB____________ number for an Outlook 2010 or Office 2010 update on or about the date you have in mind (like the day or day prior to when your problem began).  Jot down that KB______ number, and note what type of update it was (e.g., Update or Security Update.....for Office 2010 or Outlook 2010).  Close all that.

3)  DELETE IT:  Start >> Control Panel >> Add or Remove Programs.   If it doesn't default to this, click on the "Change or Remove Programs"  button to get to the correct screen.  Then start looking around for a match to that KB_____ number and its type of update.  (On my system, I have to scroll down to an orange flower-looking icon section labeled "Microsoft Office Professional Plus 2010", and the types of updates that I'm looking for are in that section.)  When you find the correct one, hit the Remove button.  Wait until it's finished doing its thing, then when it asks you if you want to reboot, say YES and let it do that.

4)  MAKE SURE THAT YOU DON'T ALLOW THAT UPDATE TO DOWNLOAD AGAIN:  First of all, you might want to reset your system so that you get notified when updates are available -- so they don't just automatically download -- this will allow you to turn-off a particular KB_____ update and not allow the system to ever mention it to you again.    If you have done this, then after you have deleted the pesky update, at some point after you reboot, your virus protector (e.g., Symantic) will notify you that it wants you to allow it to download that exact same update.  To say NO to that, just un-check the little box, and hit Close.  It will then give you the option to check a box for "Don't Notify me about this update again" -- check it, then close the whole thing.  Your're done -- yay!

paulvv say:

Calculating investment return with known cashflows over actual period (not annualised)

I am trying to calculate the actual return generated on an investment portfolio for a given period with cash flows known.


I know that XIRR gives an annualised return but what formula would I use to calculate the actual return for a period of say 9 months.


1/1/2013     $25,000  starting balance

1/4/2013     $10,000  additional contribution

1/6/2013     $5,000    withdrawal

1/8/2013     $10,000  additional contribution

30/9/2013    $75,000  end balance


joeu2004 say: Paul wrote:

I am trying to calculate the actual return generated on an investment portfolio for a given period with cash flows known.  I know that XIRR gives an annualised return but what formula would I use to calculate the actual return for a period of say 9 months.


There are many ways to calculate investment return.  The two most common are the so-called time-weighted and money-weighted returns.  (Both are misnomers, IMHO, since neither involves "weighting" in the conventional sense.)

IRR is a money-weighted return.  It is useful for comparing the timing of investments and withdrawals.

Excel XIRR returns a compounded annual rate.  If you want the equivalent compounded 9-month rate, you would use the following paradigm (see the screenshot below):

(1+XIRR(F2:F6,A2:A6))^(9/12) - 1

But be sure the cash flows are properly signed, typically negative for investments and positive for withdrawals and final balance.  In your example, the signed cash flows would be -25000, -10000, 5000, -10000, 75000.

I suspect you want the time-weighted return.  That is what most international investment firms report, conforming to GIPS.  It is useful for comparing the performance of dynamic investments with indexes and similar funds that do not have external cash flows.

TWR is a simple return.  It is not easy to define or calculate.  And you do not provide sufficient information.  In particular, we need to know the value of the investment before or after (be consistent) contributions and withdrawals.

The following paradigm is one way to calculate TWR.  Note that the signs of the cash flows are the opposite of what I use for XIRR.  It does not have to be that way.  And of course, I made up the intermediate balances.



The formula in D2 is =B3/(B2+C2)-1.  The formula in D8 is =PRODUCT(1+D3:D6)-1.  Note that formula in D8 is array-entered:  press ctrl+shift+Enter instead of just Enter.

[EDIT] We can avoid the formulas in D3:D6 by using the following array-entered formula in D8:  =PRODUCT(B3:B6/(B2:B5+C2:C5))-1.

Normally, non-annualized simple rates are quoted for periods less than 12 months.  But if you want to annualize the TWR, use the following paradigm:  =D8*12/9.

PS:  Note that the TWR paradigm returns the result you might expect (200%) when there are no external cash flows (C3:C5 are zero).

financial engineer say: Assuming that you have the correct signs ( + and - ) for your cash flows, you may at first want to wrap the XIRR function within NOMINAL function and then to find the actual return for 9 months

=NOMINAL( XIRR( B1:B5, A1:A5 ) , 12 ) * 0.75

Nominal function will return a nominal ( annual ) rate, 3/4th of such a value is the 9 months return on your investment keeping in view that 1/12th of the value returned by nominal is the periodic monthly rate.
