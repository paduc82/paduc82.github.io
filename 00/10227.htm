julie fka profjulie say: It's a complicated question and hard to explain to a 5 year old, but I'll try to make it as simple as I can and my answer may not be technically accurate, since I am not a VOIP expert. 

I have used Skype for years and I have recently ported a landline over to an entirely VOIP service, called Lingo.  And this is what I know about it:

VOIP (Voice Over IP) is a way to send and receive phone calls entirely through an internet connection.  To use it, you'd plug a VOIP box into a broadband or DSL router (to get access to your high speed internet connection) and then you'd plug a phone into the VOIP box.

Once the service is all connected up, anyone can call the phone from any landline or cellphone (and even a compatible VOIP line) and the phone will ring in your home or you can call any landline or cellphone from the VOIP phone. (I'm actually using a two-line phone for this - one line is my home phone number, which is a regular landline, and the other line is for a charity organization I direct - this phone number was a regular landline, but to cut costs I ported it over to Lingo and now it is entirely handled via VOIP).

VOIP can replace your landline if you choose a service that can port your existing phone number.
VOIP service providers generally provide discounted international calling (I do not know of any purely VOIP services that provide FREE international calling).

Skype is not an entirely pure VOIP service.  Skype's free service allows you to call any other Skype user anywhere in the world, similar to the way you can instant message via Yahoo messenger or Windows Live messenger (only instead of messaging you are talking to the person - Skype also provides free messenger services).  To use the Skype free voice services, all you'd need is a headset and microphone to plug into your computer and an internet connection.

Skype also offers some paid services that allow you to have a phone number and would allow you to call from an internet-capable phone that is plugged into your broadband or DSL router (thus bypassing your computer).  If you subscribe to the Skype phone number plan, you could place calls from the phone to any landline or cellphone and you could receive calls that are placed from any landline or cellphone.  The internet-capable phone would ring when a call is received similar to the way a landline rings. 

Skype also provides discounted calling to international numbers. 

If you subscribed to the Skype phone number, calls received to and placed from that number would use a combination of landline/cellular and voip technology to transmit and deliver the phone call.

How to assign ringtones and sound settings - Microsoft Community

jorge e. - wp7 support say:

How to assign ringtones and sound settings

How to assign ringtones and sound settings

jorge e. - wp7 support say:
Change your ring settings

You can access your ring settings by tapping Settings > Sound.

Change the ringer sound

1.      Go to the Apps > Settings > Sound screen.

2.      Turn the Vibrate setting on to have your phone vibrate as well as play a sound when signaling a call or new message.

 

Note: If you have Ringer, Voicemail, or Messages set to No sound, then your phone won’t vibrate even when this setting is on.

  Set the default sound for an incoming call

1.      Go to the Apps > Settings > Sound screen.

2.      Tap Ringer and choose a sound from the Sound selector.
Note: If you’ve assigned a specific sound to a contact, that sound will play rather
than the default sound you choose in Sound settings.

 

Set a sound for Voicemail

1.      Go to the Apps > Settings > Sound screen.

2.      Tap Voicemail and choose a sound from the Sound selector.

 

Set the ringtone for Messages

1.      Go to the Apps > Settings > Sound screen.

2.       To set the ringtone for a new text or MMS message, tap Messages and choose a sound from the Sound selector.

 

Add ringtones to events

1.      Go to the Apps > Settings > Sound screen.

2.      Tap a sound category (Ringer, Voicemail, Messages) to open the Sound selector. Scroll down to see all the sounds available. You can listen to each sound by tapping the play icon.

3.      Tap a sound to set it.

 

Assign ringtones to contacts
The sound set on the contact card overrides the global Ringer setting you choose on the Sound settings screen.

1.   Go to the Favorites Home screen and tap All contacts. (Alternatively you can go to the Apps screen and tap Contacts.)

2.   Tap New to open a new contact card. If you’ve set up an Exchange email account, a submenu will open and you’ll have the option to create a Phone contact or an Exchange contact.

3.   To assign a specific sound (ringtone) to this contact, tap Sound, then choose a sound from the selector that appears.

4.   When finished, tap Save. Your contact is added to the All Contacts screen.

5.   To have your contact appear on the Favorites screen, tap the contact to open their card, then tap Favorite.

 

Add new ringtones to your phone

You cannot import your own ringtones but you can purchase ringtones from www.mediastore.verizonwireless.com on your computer.


The ringtones you purchase will be delivered to your phone via MMS. To assign these ringtones, go to Apps > Settings > Sound > Ringer on your phone.

dhookom say:

The primary issue is that you/someone is storing a duration of time in a point in time format. I would always store time durations as the number of minutes or seconds or hours.

In Access date/time values are actually stored as numbers with the whole number part being the number of days since Dec 30. 1899. The decimal part of the number is the part of a day. Noon is 0.5 and 6:00 PM is 0.75.

You can use format properties in controls on forms and reports to display as times.

If this was my application, I would change the values to durations in seconds.

graham mandeno say:

Are you linking the Excel spreadsheet with a view to importing the data into Access, or are you using the data directly from the linked table?

Either way, you can convert the date/time value to a number of seconds by multiplying it by 86400 (the number of seconds in a day).  

Int([Fieldname] * 86400)

If importing, you can do this in the append query that inserts the new data into your table.  Otherwise, you can do it in a select query as the data is being processed.

If you don't need to process the data at all, but just display it, say, in a report, then you can use an expression to format the date/time value on the fly:

(Int([FieldName])*24 + Hour([FieldName])) & Format([FieldName], ":nn:ss")

The first part of the expression takes the number of whole days, multiplies it by 24, and adds the number of hours from the date/time.  The second part formats the minutes and seconds and appends it to the result.

ken sheridan say: There is a further complication here.  Access and Excel use a different origin in the implementation of date/time data.  For instance your first value, #1/1/1900 4:21:00 PM#, in Access when expressed as a duration in the format hh:nn:ss  is 64:21:00, not 40:21:00.  

You can see this if you apply Graham's expression (corrected slightly) to the literal value:

? Int(#1/1/1900 4:21:00 PM#)*24 + Hour(#1/1/1900 4:21:00 PM#) & Format(#1/1/1900 4:21:00 PM#, ":nn:ss")
64:21:00

or using the following function, which is one of a bunch I've thrown together over the years:

Public Function TimeToString(dtmTime As Date, _
    Optional blnShowdays As Boolean = False) As String
    
    Dim lngDays As Long
    Dim strDays As String
    Dim strHours As String

    ' get whole days
    lngDays = Int(dtmTime)
    strDays = CStr(lngDays)
    ' get hours
    strHours = Format(dtmTime, "hh")
    
    If blnShowdays Then
        TimeToString = lngDays & ":" & strHours & Format(dtmTime, ":nn:ss")
    Else
        TimeToString = Format((Val(strDays) * 24) + Val(strHours), "00") & _
            Format(dtmTime, ":nn:ss")
    End If

End Function

? TimeToString(#1/1/1900 16:21:00#)
64:21:00

Shorter durations are represented correctly, however, e.g.

? TimeToString(#12/30/1899 4:50:54 AM#)
04:50:54

Looking at your sample data I think anything less than 24 hours is being represented correctly, but values over 24 hours are offset by one day, so if this is consistent throughout the data it could be corrected with:

TimeToString(IIf(TimeField <1,TimeField,TimeField-1))

You can see how this works in the debug window:

TimeField = #1/1/1900 4:21:00 PM#
? TimeToString(IIf(TimeField <1,TimeField,TimeField-1))
40:21:00
TimeField = #12/30/1899 4:50:54 AM#
? TimeToString(IIf(TimeField <1,TimeField,TimeField-1))
04:50:54

To see the values corrected in this way in Access you'd call the function as above either in computed columns in a query or computed controls in a form or report.  In the linked table's raw datasheet view the values would be represented as at present, but that is immaterial as data should never be exposed to a user in raw datasheet view.

If you wish to do time arithmetic on the data then you'd need to conditionally adjust each value by -1 if it is 1 or above before using the value in an arithmetic expression.
