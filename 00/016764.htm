joeu2004 say: Cathy0728 wrote:
PMT and FV functions - does this use simple interest or compound the interest?

Your question is unclear, in part because the functions can be used for different purposes.

If you are talking about savings or investments, the functions compute compounded growth.  For example, if you invest $10,000 for 2 months at 1% per month, the simple interest formula is 10000+10000*1%*2 = $10,200.  The compound interest formula is 10000*(1+1%)^2 = $10,201, which is what FV(1%,2,0,-10000) returns.

If you are talking about loans, the functions still use compounded growth in theory.  But it usually does not matter since the payment should be large enough to cover interest for each period; that is, interest is paid off each period, so it does not compound anyway.

On the other hand, if you are asking whether the "rate" parameter to the functions is a simple interest rate or compound interest rate, the answer is:  it can be either one.  How you interpret the "rate" parameter depends on your jurisdiction.

In the US, the periodic interest rate for a loan is stated as a simple interest rate.  And the periodic interest rate is the annual rate divided by the number of periods per year.  For example, if the annual rate is 12%, the monthly rate is 12%/12 = 1%.

(The APR for loans is also a simple interest rate in the US.  This is often confused with the APY for savings and investments, which also used to be called APR in the past, and some people still might misuse the term APR in that way.)

However, for savings and investments, US annual rates are usually stated as an APY, a compounded rate (aka effective rate).  In that case, the monthly interest rate (e.g) can be computed by either RATE(12,0,-1,1+APY) or (1+APY)^(1/12)-1, where APY is written as 12% or 0.12 for example.

Other jurisdictions might have other rules for stating the annual rate and determining the periodic rate -- Canada in particular.  (Perhaps the UK as well.  I find conflicting information for the UK.)

If you have a specific question, provide details, and we can provide more specific answers.  In other words, provide an example of a loan or investment (state which one), including principal, annual rate, term, payment period if any, and jurisdiction.

First, I think you misunderstand what PMT() does.  It computes the periodic payment, assuming a fixed payment each period.  I'm not sure what you are trying to compute with your formula.

Second, stating a US loan annual rate as "compounded monthly" makes no sense.  You might need to state what jurisdiction the loan is in:  US, Canada, UK, other (specify)?

Finally, it is unclear what your spreadsheet is doing.  I assume you are setting up a month-by-month amortization schedule -- 36 lines, one for each payment.

The first problem to solve is:  what is the monthly interest rate?

Normally, it is simply 9.5%/12.  But your statement "9.5% compounded monthly" makes this unclear.  If the annual rate 9.5% was computed by compounding the monthly rate (very unlikely for a US loan!), the monthly interest rate would be =RATE(12,0,-1,1+9.5%).

I will assume that 9.5%/12 is the right answer.  It is the right answer for the US.  It is not the right answer for Canada; but neither is the RATE expression above.  I am not sure about the UK; I have seen conflicting information on various web sites.  And I have no idea about other jurisdictions.

The second problem to solve is:  what is the regular monthly payment?

It is roughly =PMT(9.5%/12,36,-14000).  Note:  In real life, the payment must be "rounded" at least to pennies in the US.  Exactly how it is "rounded" is up to the loan institution.  In any case, "rounding" the PMT result might require a small adjustment of the last payment; again, that depends on the policy of the loan institution.

So, suppose that A1  contains the number of payments (36), A2 contains the monthly interest rate (=9.5%/12), A3 contains the regular monthly period (=PMT(A2,A1,-A4)), and A4 contains the loan amount (14000).

Then in A5, you can compute the first-month interest amount using =A4*$A$2.  In B5, you can compute the first-month principal amount using =$A$3-A5.  In C5, you can compute the remaining balance using =A4*(1+$A$2)-$A$3.

For the remaining 35 months, copy A5:C5 down through A40:C40.

Note:  C40, the final balance, might not be exactly zero.  That is not unexpected.  In real life, we would round the final balance, at least to pennies in the US.  That is, C40 should be:  =ROUND(A39*(1+$A$2)-$A$3,2).

(There are good reasons why I do not round the other formulas.)

joeu2004 say: Brad wrote:
Without going into a long explanation - payment of a loan can be written as a sum of future payments discounted back to time zero - uses compound interest.

This is a common misunderstanding.  In simple terms, it is a classic example of the fallacy "All Greeks are men, therefore all men are Greeks".

Yes, compound interest is an example of geometric growth.  But not all problems that exhibit properties of geometric growth are examples of compound interest.

A normal reduction loan based on the annuity model (there are other models) is an example of geometric decay, just as geometric depreciation is.

But that does not mean that loan interest compounds -- in fact, it does not.  And it does not mean that the annualized interest rate is a compounded rate -- in fact, it is not by law in the US.

We start with a periodic interest rate r.

In the US, the periodic rate is annualized by computing 12*r -- a simple interest rate.  Therefore, if the annual rate for a US loan is stated as 12%, the periodic rate is 1% -- 12%/12.  This is explained in the Truth In Lending Act (aka Regulation Z) Appendix J.

In Canada, the periodic rate is said to be annualized by using "semiannual compounding".  That is a misnomer.  In fact, it is annualized by compounding the periodic rate monthly semiannually (every 6 months).  That is, the annualized interest rate is ((1+r)^6-1)*2.  So if the annual rate for a Canadian loan is stated as 12%, the periodic rate is (1+12%/2)^(1/6)-1 or simply RATE(6,0,-1,1+12%/2).

In the UK, the periodic rate might be annualized by compounding monthly; that is, (1+r)^12-1.  So, if the annual rate for a UK loan is stated as 12%, the periodic rate might be (1+12%)^(1/12)-1 or simply RATE(12,0,-1,1+12%).  However, take that with a grain of salt.  I have seen conflicting information about how the UK annualized interest rate is derived.

But the point is:  those are simply methods of annualizing a periodic rate.  Conversely, they are methods of deriving the periodic rate r from the annualized rate.  But we can do that only if we know how the periodic rate had been annualized in the first place.

In either case, that has nothing to do with the mechanics of a normal reduction loan based on the annuity model.  For loans (unlike savings), interest does not compound.

In any period, the interest amount for the period is computed by the simple interest formula B*r, where B is the outstanding balance and r is the periodic rate.

If you use PMT(r,n,B0) to compute the fixed periodic payment P, you will find that P > B0*r, where n is the number of regular payments and B0 is the initial loan amount.  In other words, the payment is more than enough to cover the interest charged for the period.

Since the remainder of the payment is used to reduce B0, we know that P > B*r for all periods.  Ergo, interest does not compound.   (Loans where that is not true are called negative amortization.)

The important take-away is:  the fact that the theory of (annuity-style) loans and compound interest savings are based on the same computational method -- geometric growth/decay -- tells us nothing about how to derive the periodic interest rate from the annualized interest rate.

Given an annualized interest rate, we can derive the periodic rate only if we know how it was annualized in the first place.

Moreover, knowing the annualized interest rate tells nothing a priori about how interest would compound if, for example, no payments were made.

For example, consider a US loan of $100,000 for 60 months at 12% APR [1].  And assume that no payments are made for the first 12 months.

If this were a savings account, the total interest would be $12,000 -- 100000*12%.  We know this independent of the compounding frequency because, in the US, the savings account "APR" (actually called APY) takes the compounding frequency into account.

But for a US loan, the total interest would be $12,682.50 -- 100000*(1+1%)^12 [2].  We know this only because we know that, in the US, the periodic rate is 12%/12, not (1+12%)^(1/12)-1 or simply RATE(12,0,-1,1+12%), about 0.9489%.

-----
Endnotes

[1] Note that often, and in the US in particular, the term "APR" for loans can refer to two somewhat different things.  Generally, APR includes certain other finance charges in addition to the interest rate.  In that case, we cannot derive the periodic interest rate from the APR without knowing the exact amount of the other finance charges, a fact that we rarely know.  But in this context, I am using the term APR to mean just the interest rate.

[2] Whether or not unpaid interest compounds actually depends on the terms of the loan contract.  Sometimes, unpaid interest simply accumulates, and the lender charges a penalty for each missed payment in lieu of compounding.

If you create the following macro in the template

Sub AutoNew()

Dim i as Long, m As Long
m = InputBox("Enter the month number.")
Dim start As Date
With ActiveDocument.Tables(1)
    start = CDate("1/" & m & "/" & Format(Date, "yyyy"))
    For i = 0 To 31
        If Format(DateAdd("d", i, start), "m") = m Then
            .Cell(4 + i, 1).Range.Text = Format(DateAdd("d", i, start), "m/d/yyyy")
        Else
            .Cell(4 + i, 1).Range.Text = ""
        End If
    Next i
End With

End Sub

when you create a new document from the template, it will ask you to enter the number of the month (7 for July) and then populate the cells in the first column with the required dates.

If the table is not the first table in the document, replace the 1 in With ActiveDocument.Tables(1) with the relevant number.

Doug, being in Australia, used the day/month/year format for the line

start = CDate("1/" & m & "/" & Format(Date, "yyyy"))

I suspect you have the US default date format chosen in Windows, month/day/year. That caused the macro to set all of the cells to the empty string "". The quick fix for that is to change the line to 

start = CDate(m & "/1/" & Format(Date, "yyyy"))

If this were being published for general use by anyone, the macro would have to ask Windows for the current date format.

When you ask to fill multiple tables at once, I'm not sure whether you want all the tables to be filled with the same month, or to be for different months. The following version of the macro asks for the month number for each table; if you want them all to be the same month, move the line

m = InputBox("Enter the month number for Table " & tblnum, "Table " & tblnum)

to just before the "For tblnum" line.

Sub AutoNew()
  Dim i As Long, m As Long
  Dim tblnum As Long
  Dim tbl As Table
  Dim start As Date
  For tblnum = 1 To ActiveDocument.Tables.Count
    Set tbl = ActiveDocument.Tables(tblnum)
    m = InputBox("Enter the month number for Table " & tblnum, "Table " & tblnum)
    start = CDate(m & "/1/" & Format(Date, "yyyy"))
    For i = 0 To 30
        If Format(DateAdd("d", i, start), "m") = m Then

        Else

End Sub
