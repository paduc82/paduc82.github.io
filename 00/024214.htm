suzanne s. barnhill say:

The likelihood is that your graphic designer knows nothing about how Word works. I've worked with graphic designers to create letterhead templates, so I speak from experience. Very likely the "letterhead" is just stuck at the top of the document instead of being properly inserted in the First Page Header. If you can select the letterhead graphic when you are in the document body, then that's what's happening.

If that's the case:

  1. Open the template file, select the letterhead graphic, and Cut it to the Clipboard.
  2. Double-click in the header area to activate the header.
  3. Check the box for "Different First Page" on the Header & Footer Tools | Design tab.
  4. Your header will now be the First Page Header. Paste the letterhead graphic into the First Page Header.
  5. If your designer has designed the letterhead as a page-width graphic, you'll need to set the wrapping to Square and the position as Top and Left Relative to Page. You may well also need to restore a sensible top margin and header margin.
  6. For more on working with letterhead, see http://wordfaqs.mvps.org/LetterheadRibbon.htm, especially http://wordfaqs.mvps.org/LetterheadRibbon.htm#MultiPage.

Jamie Davis, VP, Education, Product Management, and Quality Control | Safe Systems

Office 2013 is Microsoft’s first big push into integrating Office applications and Office files into “the cloud.”  As such, there are a few options you might want to be aware of to ensure you maintain the same file management settings you’ve been using in the past.

By default, Office 2013 applications ask if you want to save any document to the Onedrive as the first option. Onedrive is Microsoft’s cloud storage solution, and opens up new opportunities for collaboration, sharing documents, and accessing needed files from anywhere. Saving files to the cloud BY DEFAULT also opens up a new can of worms for security-minded businesses; by allowing your employees to do this on each machine now means that, at a minimum, you have to perform additional vendor management. If you’re forced to perform that vendor management after your employees are already saving documents to the Onedrive, is the tail wagging the dog? The best thing to do is to have a proactive discussion about cloud storage and put a policy in place stating the financial institution’s stance.

If your institution has decided not to use Onedrive or cloud storage options in general, then the first place to address this is at the firewall. Speak with your firewall vendor about what options you have to block access to Onedrive and other cloud storage options, such as Dropbox.

Remember: Office 2013 actually offers each user the option to save to the Onedrive by default; however, if you prefer to eliminate this option, then there are a couple of ways to disable Onedrive. At the most basic level, you can disable it on a machine-by-machine basis when you install Office. You can also remove this functionality across a large set of devices via the Group Policy Editor.

Remove Onedrive as default Save option in Word:

When choosing to save a document in Office 2013, it does not open up the “Save as” dialog box but instead opens a screen where you can decide whether you want to save the file on the local hard drive or on your Onedrive. This may provide the user with more options than your security policies allow. To correct this, open Word and follow the changes below. Making this change in Word should adjust the behavior of all Office 2013 products (Excel, Power Point, Outlook, etc). Here are the steps:

  1. 1.    Start at the “Save As” screen.
  2. 2.    Chose “Options” on the bottom left.
  3. 3.    Check the option “Don’t show the Backstage when opening or saving files”
  4. 4.    Remove the check from the option “Show additional places for saving…” (sometimes listed as “Always show Sign in to Onedrive”).
  5. 5.    Place a check on the option “Save to computer by default”.

While this is a relatively quick and painless process on an individual machine, it can become a hassle if you plan to implement Office 2013 across a large number of workstations. In these cases you may wish to use group policy to enforce a configuration. If you have used group policy before, the settings are pretty easy; however, if you have never used group policy before, then please have your IT provider perform these changes. Making changes to group policy without an understanding of how it works could lead to no change taking place or could even cause a disruptive network event.

Disabling Onedrive by using the Group Policy Editor

To begin, open the Group Policy Editor on your domain controller. If you do not see the settings below, then you will need to download the latest ADM files from Microsoft for Office 2013.

  1. 1.    The settings can be found under User Configuration > Administrative Templates > Microsoft Office 2013 > Miscellaneous.
  2. “Block signing into Office” option in the list of settings.
  3. 3.    In the Properties window, click “Enable” and select the option “None Allowed.”

Using Onedrive or Office 2013 and Onedrive are legitimate choices.  But it should just be a choice you choose, not adopted by default.  With more applications taking advantage of cloud based technologies, this is just one of many applications that will presume access to cloud based storage or products is acceptable for all.  Before upgrading any software, read the update or release notes and when possible, install it on your machine first.  And lastly, keep these two points in mind when installing software going forward:  1) It’s always easier to add features later than to remove them after the fact.  2) It’s easier to make decisions about what is acceptable and what isn’t if the institution has well defined policies.

Completely Disable Onedrive Everywhere

If you want to get rid of Onedrive entirely, you can use Group Policy to remove it. This will get rid of it from everywhere on the system though, including the Metro apps.

Launch Group Policy Editor by pressing the Windows key and R simultaneously, typing gpedit.msc and hitting Enter.

Beneath the Local Computer Policy section, navigate to Computer Configuration\Administrative Templates\Windows Components\Onedrive and then double click the entry labeled Prevent the usage of Onedrive for file storage to the right.

Select Enabled and then click OK.

The change will take immediate effect and you’ll see that the Onedrive icon disappears from Explorer straight away.

Remove from the Explorer Side Panel

If you just want to remove Onedrive from the side panel, you can use the registry editor to remove the library that provides that feature.

Press the Windows key and R at the same type, type regedit and press Enter. You then need to navigate to HKEY_CLASSES_ROOT\CLSID\ {8E74D236-7F35-4720-B138-1FED0B85EA75}\ShellFolder.

Before you can proceed, you will have to take ownership of this registry key, and when this is done you can make the necessary changes.

Double click the Attribute key and change its value to 0.

Should you ever feel the need to reverse the changes and reinstate Onedrive, you can do so by either disabling the setting in Group Policy Editor, or editing the registry key again, this time changing its value back to the default of f080004d.

Ever since my account was moved to the new Outlook Mail interface (on Exchange server), I've had a similar problem ... new emails received on my account's outlook server were mysteriously being marked as read.

In my case, this was being caused by the fact that (in addition to using a browser on my PC to sign on to outlook.com to review my mail) I use an email app on my phone. The email app on my phone uses POP (as opposed to IMAP).

With the new Outlook Mail interface, whenever the POP- based email app (on my phone) fetches new mail from my account on the outlook Exchange server, the mail that was fetched is marked read on the outlook Exchange server. There are no settings in my email app (on phone) to prevent the Exchange server from marking all fetched mail as being read. I could probably fix this problem by changing my phone's mail app to use IMAP, but I prefer to use POP.

Note that the old outlook.com interface did not do this (i.e., with the old interface, mail fetched by my phone's email app was NOT marked as being read on the server). This behavior (fetched mail being marked as read) only started happening when my account was moved to the new Outlook Mail interface (Exchange servers).

So, if you have an email app on your phone (or use a POP-based email client elsewhere), that might be the reason you are seeing new email being marked as read on the server.
