hal hostetler - mvp-outlook say:
.OST files are tied to the Outlook profile that created them, so they cannot be used for backup purposes.  In your scenarios, the only method really available is to export the .OST to a .PST each backup interval and back that up.  Since the export is not incremental, a full export will need to be taken each time.

Hal, I am aware of that option, but I'm sure you would agree that it is not a practical method of ensuring the backup of mission critical business data.

karl timmermans [outlook mvp 2012-2018] say:

Hal, I am aware of that option, but I'm sure you would agree that it is not a practical method of ensuring the backup of mission critical business data.

That unfortunately is the only option when it comes to anything related to .OST files short of opting to purchase an expensive 3rd party OST2PST converter utility should it ever be required to retrieve the data contained in the OST file.

Also, depending on how your IMAP accounts have been configured, they will also contain "This Computer Only" contact/calendar folders. The loss of contact/calendar data can be mitigated by creating a PST file in the profile and designating it as the "default of this presumes that no other PST file has already been designated as the default or the data file for another email account (Outlook.com <EAS> or Exchange) has not already been designated as the default.

roady [mvp] say:

...it is not a practical method of ensuring the backup of mission critical business data.

What is the Microsoft "Best Practice" for Backing Up IMAP?

In a business scenario, the "Best Practice" is to backup the IMAP server or have an SLA with the provider of your IMAP accounts to ensure uptime and backups.

As Karl Timmermanns already mentioned, you can configure Outlook 2013 in such a way, that other data that is usually stored within "This Computer Only" folder of the IMAP ost-file (like Contacts, Calendar, Tasks, etc...) are stored within pst-files. Those pst-files can be included in your regular client-to-server backup solution.

roady [mvp] say:

The recoverability of the ost-file and not needing to rely on 3rd party tools for it is a long outstanding request and discussion but it is not directly the point of this discussion.

The whole purpose of IMAP (and (Hosted) Exchange for that matter) is that the mail client syncs with the mail server and thus create a centralized managed storage location. Why don't you want to manage your backups from this centralized storage location? When you want to use a 3rd party to host your mail, you should pick one that fits your requirements and thus the uptime, redundancy and recoverability of the infrastructure from your chosen hosting partner comes into question then too. When they don't fit your requirements, you don't choose them.

You mention both Google and Microsoft apparently as an example of a "bad partner" since you can't negotiate your SLA even though they both already have that in their standard SLA and an excellent track record.

As much as I personally would also like to be able to open ost-files in an easy and native way, I must also acknowledge that it would only be needed in the extreme case of a total loss of the mail environment, its backups and all client side configurations (connected ost-files can be exported to pst-files, orphaned ost-files cannot). A serious administrator of a centralized or hosted environment should never (EVER!) need to rely on client side stored data to recover his/her environment (and also note that someone can have Outlook configured to only cache the last 6 months in the ost-file). With that in mind, recovering data from ost-files can be seen as a "last resort attempt" to still salvage some data from an apparently poorly maintained and/or fragile environment.

That you are actually even considering to use pst-files and POP3 account and thus resort to an unmanaged and decentralized (client-side) storage location for your email environment makes me believe that you are an administrator for a relatively small environment (<15) with relatively capable users to maintain their own computers. In those cases it might sometimes make more financial sense to go this way and accept a certain risk.

The choice is yours, but you asked for the best practices to backup data from IMAP accounts. The answer in relation to Outlook is; Don't rely on client side caches.

roady [mvp] say:

When it comes to single-item-restores, the recommendation is no longer to rely on backups for this but on server-level retention settings as Hal Hostetler also pointed out. This is only needed in the cases where the user first deletes the message and then also deletes that message again from the Deleted Items folder. I don't know about Google, but Microsoft allows for recovery of those items for 30 days and it can be performed by the user. Business critical information can even be put on "Legal Hold" or other retention solutions to keep it longer or even indefinitely.

While I can understand the data loss in your scenario and how restoring the data from an ost-file could be helpful, it is a corner case scenario and still a "last resort attempt" rather than a "best practice" as multiple things have gone wrong here.

First of all, you already had a synching issue before having a crashing issue with Outlook so the data was never stored on the server (and data doesn't get deleted from a server when Outlook crashes). Second, reinstalling Outlook doesn't delete its mail configuration so the data was still there after the reinstall. Third, you removed the mail account from the mail profile and only at that stage did you orphan the ost-file and made it unrecoverable by conventional means.

Had you discovered the synching issue before the crashing issue, you would have recovered the data and fixed the issue beforehand. If there hadn't been a synching issue, you could have recreated the account all you want (as an additional measure to reinstalling) without any data loss. Had you known about the synching issue, you wouldn't have deleted the account as well if all it actually took was reinstalling Outlook as you stated. It's the original synching issue followed by the complete local reset of the affected client which caused the data loss in your case.

I'm not trying to point any fingers here, nor am I stating that being able to recover from an ost-file is worthless in all cases (I'm actually a strong advocate of being able to recover it especially when it comes to the "This computer only" folders within IMAP ost-files as you might have noticed in the article I referenced in my earlier post). All I'm saying is that for a "Best practice" you should not rely on a local cache when it comes to being able to recover your IMAP data.
