That's really a shocking thing to say, particularly for someone with 'MVP' after their name.

Hardware manufacturers choose whether or not to support changes to an operating system. Microsoft doesn't choose whether or not to support hardware.

In the first place, Microsoft can't possibly know about the hundreds of thousands of hardware devices in use throughout the world. But let's say they did.

Microsoft can't possibly write drivers for all those hundreds of thousands of hardware devices every time Windows changes, because Microsoft didn't build those devices and doesn't have access to the manufacturers' proprietary knowledge of how they were built and how they work. But let's say they did.

Microsoft has no right to benefit from or modify that proprietary knowledge because  it's the manufacturer's legally protected intellectual property.

Microsoft publishes details of upcoming changes to Windows so hardware manufacturers can update and test their drivers. It's the hardware manufacturers who decide whether they'll keep on supporting their devices, and which models will and won't any longer be supported.

And this is nothing new - it's always been that way. What's different today is that only 6 months, more or less, pass between new versions of Windows, whereas in the past, years would go by.

The quicker cadence is a challenge to hardware manufacturers (and software developers) but Microsoft didn't choose that cadence. If they don't keep up with the pace of technology, their competitors (notably Google and Apple) will eat their lunch. In some ways they already have.

I've lost track of how many updates to ChromeOS my Chromebook has received this year; it's been at least 3 or 4. Of course, Chromebook manufacturers have to build them to Google's specifications, so updating ChromeOS is much easier than updating Windows.

Updating drivers, testing them, documenting them, and bringing support staff up to speed costs money. When a hardware manufacturer decides that a particular model is no longer bringing in money, it's likely to lose support.
