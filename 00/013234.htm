joeu2004 say:

InTroubleNow wrote:

CUMIPMT formula calculates by month

InTroubleNow wrote:

The start_period, end_period, = The starting month and ending month, not the starting day and ending day.

You seem to be laboring under the misimpression that CUMIPMT periods are necessarily monthly. That is incorrect.

Nper (2nd parameter) specifies the number of periods, whether they are days, weeks, months, quarters, years or whatever.  A "period" is the time between

StartPeriod and endPeriod (4th and 5th parameters) are the period numbers 1 through nper, in the same units as nper.

It is important that "rate" (1st parameter) is also in the same units as nper.

Typically, it is the annual rate divided by the number of such units per year.  If the annual rate is 12%:  12%/365 for days, 12%/52 for weeks, 12%/12 for months, 12%/4 for quarters, and simply 12% for years.

However, some regions calculate sub-annual rates differently, notably for EU and Canadian mortgages.

Jim wrote:

So, 12% loan, for 365 days for $100,000, interest paid for the first 20 days is...
  =CUMIPMT(0.0003287,365, 100000, 1, 20, 0)

Jim's example might have been clearer (and certainly more accurate) if the first parameter were written 12%/365.

Nevertheless, I disagree with that calculation because it presumes a daily payment, part of which reduces principal and part of which reduces interest to varying degrees on a daily basis.  I don't know of any loan that works that way.

Well, some bank-to-bank loans do work that way. I'm talking about banks that borrow millions or billions of dollars from each other for 1 to 3 days or so.

But for us mere mortals, interest between loan payments is typically calculated based on simple interest.  It is rarely based on compound interest. And in either case, it is based on the actual or average daily balance, not a fictitious balance that is infinitesimally reduced daily based on the actuarial method.

InTroubleNow wrote:

I need something to calculate by a variable number of days that have passed, like 20 days or 31 days since last payment      

I believe the following demonstrates the calculation that you require. If not, please provide the details of your own example.

The amount of interest is the daily rate (B2/365) times the days between payments (A8-A7) times the previous outstanding balance (E7).

No Excel financial function can calculate the accumulated interest payment over multiple months, because the length of the payment period varies.

And no Excel financial function calculates simple (or compound) interest based on these assumptions.
