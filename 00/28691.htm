jim gordon mvp say:
Word for Mac is not a direct port of Word for Windows. If it were, you would have all the features of Word for Windows, and it would look just as bad. You wouldn't have standard toolbars. You wouldn't have Notebook Layout View. You wouldn't have Publishing Layout View. You wouldn't have Arrange Objects and any other Mac-only features. Word for Mac has its own VBA, its own VBA compiler, supports AppleScript and Automator. None of this is in Word for Windows.

Certainly the Mac and Windows Word teams share code and features, but the two platforms are developed and coded independently. Some features are made on the Mac first (well, probably most of them) and then ported to Windows. Keep in mind Word, Excel and PowerPoint all have a 5 year head start on the Mac.

Some features require both Apple and Microsoft to work together to bring to fruition. RTL support is one of them. If Mac OS supports RTL, that doesn't mean Word's coding can simply use it. As was pointed out, there are language pack and other coding that needs to be done to make RTL work. While there are thousands of programmers working on Office for Windows, even though Microsoft has the largest Mac programming base of any company, there simply aren't enough Mac programmers to make every feature come to life as quickly as even Microsoft would like to see it happen. When it comes to programming in multiple languages, things get even more complicated and it's even harder to find programmers with the necessary skills.

If you know a good Mac proficient programmer who is multi-lingual in RTL languages, by all means refer the person to Microsoft's Macintosh business unit. Such a person would be rare, indeed. I suspect there is more than one opening and that the welcome mat has been rolled out.

suzanne s. barnhill say:

You don't need section breaks for this, but if you do insert section breaks, make sure that the page numbering in every section is set to "Continue from previous section." You cannot type the page number; you have to insert a PAGE field. The easiest way to do that is to press Alt+Shift+P where you want the page number. Here's how to do the rest:

  1. Set the top margin to 1" for the entire document.
  2. If you will have a page number on the first page, then all you need to do to get a 2" top margin is to add 72 points (1") Spacing Before to the first paragraph on the page.
  3. If your first page will not have a page number, then check the box for Different First Page in the Options group on the Header & Footer Tools | Design tab that appears when you access the header. This will give you a separate First Page Header/Footer that you can leave empty (you'll insert the PAGE field only in the primary Header that starts on page 2). You could also, if you choose, add 96 points Spacing After to the empty paragraph in the First Page Header, which will put your first paragraph in the document body at 2" without requiring Spacing Before (see http://wordfaqs.mvps.org/DifferentMargins.htm).
  4. Unfortunately, Word 2013 does not honor Spacing Before at the top of a page except at the beginning of a document; if it did, you could insert a manual page break before the Bibliography and add Spacing Before to the title paragraph on that page. To avoid requiring a section break (after which the Spacing Before will be retained), you could just insert a manual page break followed by an empty paragraph before the bibliography title. Then add enough Spacing Before to the title to put it at 2". If you have Vertical Page Position displayed on your status bar, it will be easy to see when you have got this right.
