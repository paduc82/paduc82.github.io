How to share your Office licenses around to the family.

Article posted: Tuesday, 04 February 2014

An Office 365 Home Premium subscription has always come with 5 licenses for Office desktop software (Office for Windows or Mac).

Originally you could only access those installs by login to the purchasers Microsoft account. That wasn’t a great idea because it meant giving your password to another member of the family to let them install Office.

Now the owner/purchaser of Office 365 Home Premium can nominate up to 4 other people (household/family members according to the license) who can install Office desktop software.

Click on Add user and enter the email address of the person.

Tip:     ask them what email address they have, if any, linked to an existing Microsoft/Hotmail/Outlook.com account. That’s the most convenient address to use for linking to the subscription because no new account has to be created. The extra 20GB of OneDrive (formerly Skydrive) space is added to their existing OneDrive allowance.

When you click on ‘Send Invite’ this is what the person receives.

The ‘Be Careful’ note at the top of the above image came from Outlook.com! Apparently Microsoft’s own Outlook.com hosting system thinks a legitimate invitation from another Microsoft server is potential fraud. It shows a degree of independence I suppose ….

The invitation email expires after one month. At least we assume it’s one month (4 March) and not longer (3 April). When will Microsoft realize that most of the world doesn’t use mm/dd/yyyy date formatting?

When the invitation is accepted by clicking on the link, the new user is taken to their own My Account page. If there’s no Microsoft account for that email address, they’ll be prompted to make an account first.

Each person linked to the Office 365 subscription has access to all remaining/available Office installations. The subscription owner can’t control how many installations each person takes.

Click on Install to get a copy of the latest Office for Windows or Office for Mac.

Office for mobile is also available.

Each person linked to a Home Premium subscription gets an extra 20GB of SkyDrive/OneDrive storage using the same email address/login.

    Preventing a Section Break from Resetting Page Numbering in TOC - Microsoft Community

    confuseditinerant say:

    Preventing a Section Break from Resetting Page Numbering in TOC

    Hello,

    I'm writing a report and inserted a section break because I needed several pages to have landscape orientation to fit tables I need to include in the report. Unfortunately, The TOC restarts page numbering after this section break, which is a problem. How did I prevent the section break from restarting the TOC numbering? If there is something I should be using other than section break in order to change the orientation of several mid-report pages, that information would be helpful too. I'm hoping I can have my cake and eat it too.

    Thank you!

    confuseditinerant say:

    I searched for an hour and a half on Google before posting my question here, and, of course, as soon as I post it on the forums, I figure out the answer. I'll leave the question on here in case anyone else every runs into this problem and needs to know how to solve it.

    I had been inserting the TOC directly without inserting page numbers. Insert page numbers, and you will see that adding a section does reset page numbering. However, after you've added page numbers, right click the page numbers in the new section you added and choose “Edit Page Numbers." There is an option that says "Continue Page Numbers from Previous Section." If you click this option, the page numbers for the new section you've added will be a continuation of the page numbering from the previous section. Go back to the TOC you've added, right click it, and select "Update Field." This will fix the issue. Good luck!

    john korchok say:

    Master documents have been a source of problems for a long time. They work best when you use them as a temporary file, creating a master document just to print the subdocuments, then getting rid of it. When a solution for a customer requires a master document, I usually provide a macro that creates the master, prints the file, then discards the master.

    When you try to maintain a master document for a long time as a permanent document, you'll get bitten by file corruption sooner or later.

    bob jones aka: cybertaz say:

    The debate over Master/Sub Documents is never-ending, so I don't advocate one way or the other with regard to using the feature :-)

    What I will suggest [strongly] is that if you do choose to use it, never edit an expanded sub-document in the Master. Use the Master only for global operations such as viewing, reorganizing, printing, generating TOC, Index, cross-referencing from one sub to another, etc.
