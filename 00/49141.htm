In my view, RAID 1 is a mistake for almost everyone.  RAID 1 (mirroring) is *not* a backup solution. RAID 1 uses two or more drives, each a duplicate of the others, to provide redundancy, not backup. It's used in situations (almost always within corporations, not in homes) where any downtown can't be tolerated, because the way it works is that if one drive fails the other takes over seamlessly.

Although some people thing of RAID 1 as a backup technique, that is *not* what it is, since it's subject to simultaneous loss of the original and the mirror to many of the most common dangers threatening your data--severe power glitches, nearby lightning strikes, virus attacks, theft of the computer, etc. Most companies that use RAID 1 also have a strong external backup plan in place.

"Why RAID is (usually) a Terrible Idea" http://www.pugetsystems.com/articles?&id=29



On the other hand, if you also make external backups, my obhections to RAID largely go away.

hairyfool say:

Make sure the backup is up to date, break the mirror and download the drive diagnostics for your particular make of drive.

 

Run the diagnostics on each individual drive to test them as trying to do it with the RAID active may desynchronise the drives and the mirror might be obscuring problems with one of the drives.

 

In itself a mirror should have no appreciable performance hit and has saved me having to rebuild and restore from backup a couple of times Since switching to SSD I don't use the mirror primarily because only a few RAID controllers support SSD properly.

 

As Ken says mirrors are not a backup but disk space is relatively cheap and can save significant down time if you have to order a replacement drive and then go through the restore process.
