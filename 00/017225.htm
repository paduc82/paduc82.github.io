rob koch say:

No, this is something that can happen. most typically when performing a full scan, since this will scan all locations within the filing system for potential malware and is thus more prone to false positive detections than the much shorter quick scan that only scans locations that malware is known to commonly operate.

If you read that message very carefully, you'll notice that it states "preliminary scan results show that malicious or potentially unwanted software

The first statement is referring to preliminary detection of individual components that may be associated with malware or potentially unwanted software, but have yet to be confirmed, which is why this indicates "might exist" rather than providing assurance that this is true.

The second statement is not, however, referring to these same potential items, but rather to items that have truly been detected as malware or PUPs at final completion of the scanning process.

The subtle difference here is what's causing the confusion, since unless you understand how Microsoft performs this entire process these statements can appear to be discussing the same items.

What actually occurs is that during the initial scan process the scanning engines detect individual file or registry items that are known to be associated with malware or PUPs, but might not be.  However, during the later portions of the process the application itself uses the Microsoft Active Protection Service (MAPS) to communicate with Microsoft's servers and verify these discrete items and determine whether they truly are malicious or might instead be a false positive detection.

The other possibility in this case is that Microsoft doesn't consider these specific items malware or more malicious PUPs and so won't result in a final detection based on their own official criteria, even though some other 3rd-party security products might choose to call them malicious and so list them in their detections.  This most commonly affects such potentially unwanted software as adware or other similarly annoying, but not necessarily malicious software and thus often confuses consumer PC users who don't see the difference.

How Microsoft identifies malware and potentially unwanted applications | Microsoft Docs

It's possible this latter issue is happening in your case, so even though the Microsoft engines are initially detecting the tiny components that are sometimes known to accompany malware, in the end these aren't being detected since Microsoft doesn't consider them malicious.

If you want to determine whether this is what's happing in your case, you could try a reputable 3rd-party scanner that's known to be quite aggressive against PUPs such as Malwarebytes Anti-Malware, which decided a few years ago to detect more of these less malicious items, while leaving the decision of whether to remove them up to the PC owner himself.

Winning the battle against PUPs on your computer and in court - Malwarebytes Labs

sumanth54 say: Hi cjlogan...

When you save a document in a Word 97-2003 format, it will be saved as a .doc document.
The problem here is that when you try to open these documents, the documents open in Microsoft Works. The documents are still Word 97-2003 documents but they are opening in the Works program.
This is because the default program set to open .doc files on your computer is Microsoft Works.

We will have to change the default program for .doc to Microsoft Office Word 2007.

Follow the steps below:

1. Click Start and click on Default programs.
2. Click "Associate a file type or protocol with a program".
3. Locate .doc and double click on it.
4. Click on Browse.
5. Browse to C:\Program files\Microsoft Office\Office 12 (C:\Program Files(X86)\Microsoft Office\Office 12)
6. Select Winword or winword.exe and click open.
7. Click ok.
