bernie deitrick say:

Dr. Otter,

This is a manual solution: You can also use a macro - but that depends on how skilled you are.

I have assumed that your first blank column is H, and the data of interest is in columns F and G. IF that is not the case, then you need to adjust these instructions.

First off, make a copy of your sheet, and do this on the copy until you are sure you have done it correctly.

In H1, enter the formula

=F1

And copy to the right for 5 columns.

Then in H2, use the formula

=IF(F2="",F3,"")

and copy to I2.

In J2, use the formula

=IF(AND(F2="",F4<>""),F4,"")

and copy to K2.

In L2, use the formula

=IF(AND(F2="",F4<>"",F5<>""),F5,"")

and copy to M2.

Then copy H2:M2 down to match your rows of data. Finally, copy H:M and paste special values, and then delete columns F and G. Then select column B, use Ctrl-G   choose the "Special..." button,   select "Blanks" press "OK", right click the selection and choose Delete....   Entire row OK , and you are done.

If you ever have 4 rows of values for one ID, you need to use an extra 2 columns of formulas.

You can also use a macro - but that depends on how skilled you are.

rob koch say:

Microsoft has gone after some of these tech support scammers in the past and I assume they still might try and do something about the more persistent offenders.  However, this is basically like playing Whack-a-Mole, since as soon as they catch one another 5 or 10 pop up in their place.

This is why I suggested spending your own effort at protecting your parents yourself using built-in tools, since that's a far more effective way than trying to catch one-in-a-million offenders after the fact.

The main thing that Microsoft did when these modal dialog (announcement popup window) screens became common with Windows 10, was to add a checkbox that makes it possible to block the repeating popup titled "Prevent this page from creating additional dialogs".  By checking this and then either clicking the OK button or pressing the Enter key if it's not present, future display of that specific dialog by that website is inhibited.

Another security protection you might wish to look into is anti-exploit technology such as Microsoft's EMET or Malwarebytes Anti-Exploit, since the latter is more consumer friendly.  These block some of the techniques used by malware to exploit any new vulnerabilities found in Windows or applications such as the IE web browser or Adobe Flash, reducing the potential that these can successfully install or operate.

If Internet Explorer is being used rather than the Edge browser, then checking Enable Enhanced Protected Mode in the Security section of the Advanced tab in Internet Options will increase the protection within the IE browser, though this may cause issues with some browser add-ons, especially some older 32-bit versions.  However, the trade-off is that some of the less reputable add-ons like toolbars and other browser related malware are disabled from running or even being installed.

All such security modifications have their limitations and trade-offs, but when the PC is being used by less technically capable individuals, some of these additional protections may be valuable.  I personally prefer to accept a slightly more limited experience for the reduced chance that malware may gain a foothold on my PC.

Rob
