rohn007 say:

Just to confirm, you have a Office 365 Home subscription.

Starting with Office 2013/365 MS changed the fundamentals of Office licensing.  Prior to that the license/product key was associated to the hardware/computer.  Starting in 2013 that was changed, the license or subscription was associated with an email account.  Since your husband made the initial purchase and activation of the subscription his email is the account holder. The subscription can be setup to automatically renew. So as long as the credit card was still active, the renewal would happen.

I'm guessing that he "shared" the subscription with you. That gives you the right to install and activate Office on up to 5 computers at the same time.  The owner of the 365 Home subscription also has the right to "share" that subscription with up to 5 other "family" users.

In Office 365, each user has control of their own 5 installations.  You should be able to just install/activate on the new computer.  If you have already used up your 5 activation "counts", you can "deactivate" it on one computer (presumably your old computer) to free up the count on the new computer.  You do this by logging in to the MyAccount site.

This page has specific instructions for deactivating an installation:

https://support.office.com/en-us/article/deactivate-office-365-if-you-see-a-limit-reached-error-767e3560-96ed-4b1c-806d-2fe01c529b1b

Short form, on the new computer, log in to the MYACCOUNT site, under "Devices" find the old computer, click on "deactivate" next to its ID

To install on the new computer this page has specific instructions

https://support.office.com/en-us/article/Download-and-install-or-reinstall-Office-365-or-Office-2016-on-a-PC-or-Mac-4414eaaf-0478-48be-9c42-23adc4716658  

again in short form, while still logged in from the "deactivate" process, you can click on the Install button to install (and activate) on the new computer.

MS does not have a useful process for "transferring" a license to another user, even in the event of death.

They do have a process of assigning an "Alias" to an account, but that requires the original password and account to be active.

If you know when the subscription was renewed, I strongly suggest that you let it expire. But since you cannot access the account to turn off the autorenew option you will have to cancel the subscription. A few days before the next renewal contact MS Support. Explain the situation and ask them to cancel the subscription.  If they cannot, then you will have to contact the credit card company and ask them to reject the charge when it comes in.  Explain to credit card company that you will be making a charge to the same card later, for a new subscription. Or use a different credit card number. 

After the subscription expires or is cancelled you may have a "grace period" before the license goes into "limited function mode", so don't activate the new subscription until you have to.  You can go to an electronics store and buy a 'product key card' so you have it on hand when the current license finally expires.

If you are the only person using the subscription, ie it is not being shared with any other users, then rather than 365 Home, you might as well save some money and get 365 Personal.
