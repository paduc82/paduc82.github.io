chriso747 say:

Maybe you've haven't had the opportunity to fly and therefore get the error message (which will be useful in noting which is the faulting module), in any case, your specs are more than adequate to run FSX at least at the default aircraft/scenery level and higher.   And as you mentioned, that you have used FSX on your previous computer without any problems.   I assume that your TV monitor hasn't been a cause for errors in the past.

Aside from the FSX program, I would run chkdsk for any errors, clean up the drive and defragment.

In FSX (for testing purposes):  remove any added on scenery or aircraft (except installed Afcad scenery files or airline skins).

In your FSX Settings:   reduce all your settings under each of the five tabs to default.  Then adjust the following in each tab:

Graphics:  Target Frame Rate (30);  Filtering & Global Texture Resolution (your choice);  only tick Adv. Animations.

Aircraft:  Untick Aircraft casts shadows on itself.

Scenery:  LOD (your choice);  Mesh complexity (80-100);  Mesh Resolution (38 m);  Texture Resolution (1 m);  Water Effects (High 1.x or low 2.x);  Scenery Complexity (as low as you can go for now);  Autogen Density (Normal or Sparse for now); Special Effects (your choice).

Weather:  Cloud Draw Distance (Low);  Cloud Detail (Detailed Clouds, Medium to High)

Traffic:  Airline traffic (<40%);  GA traffic (<10%);  Airport Vehicle Density (Medium to High);  Road Vehicles (10 -20%).

Then go to your FSX Configuration File (fsx.cfg) located here:  

Your Drive:\Users\yourName\AppData\Roaming\Microsoft\FSX

(you will find two fsx.cfg files:  one .cfg and the other .cfg.txt).   Delete the .cfg file:  FSX will rebuild a new one when you start FSX.   However, you will lose any specially mapped controls, etc., so you may want to note all that down).

Start FSX to rebuild a new cfg file and shut FSX down again.   Go back into your fsx.cfg file in the above location and do the following:

Make sure that your Display Device has only one entry, unless you are using Directx 10, then you will have two entries.

Under Graphics, add the line "HIGHMEMFIX=1" (without quotes).  Aces team forgot to include this entry.

Under Display, WideViewAspect=True (since you're using a TV).

There are other tweaks, but generally they cause more problems than they are worth.

Also, your Nvidia card can be configured, either through their Control Panel or via Nvidia Inspector (can be downloaded for more settings), but not for now.

So that's it for setting up FSX to give you a flight at minimal to average settings.  If you still get a mid-flight crash, then there must be another conflict with your hardware, which will take a lot more time to isolate.   There are also guides published for setting up FSX and dealing with CTDs, etc., but we'll see how you go with the above for now.

In the short paragraph under "Copy files to the new user profile," you'll see the admonition that you "must have at least three user accounts on the computer to complete these steps, including the new account you just created."  You should ALWAYS have an extra account with "administrator" rights that you rarely use (so it has a low probability of becoming corrupted).  The standard advice is that for maximum safety, your everyday account should be a "Standard user" account, but not everyone follows that advice.  No matter whether your everyday account is a "Standard user" or an "administrator," you should have a spare "administrator" account.

If you don't have an uncorrupted "administrator" account, you'll have to activate the built-in account named "Administrator."  By default, Windows 7 keeps this account disabled.  Without "administrator" rights, however, it's a bit tricky to activate the "Administrator" account.  Do you have a Windows 7 "system repair disc"?  Do you have a Windows 7 installation DVD?  If you don't have either of these, all is still not lost.  If you're in this position, post back for further instructions.

lemp say:

P.S.:

A "workgroup" is what Microsoft calls a peer-to-peer local area network.  This is what virtually all home users of Windows computers have.  The alternative is a "domain network," in which there is a central "domain server" computer, usually managed by a network administrator.

"HomeGroup" is a feature that Microsoft introduced with Windows 7.  It is a simplified method of creating a local area network.  A "HomeGroup" and a "workgroup" are very similar but not identical (which is why the same collection of Windows 7 computers may be identified as belonging both to a workgroup and a HomeGroup).  See http://www.computernetworkingnotes.com/networking-basic/differences-between-workgroup-and-homegroup.html and/or http://www.windowspasswordsrecovery.com/win8-tips/what-is-the-difference-between-a-domain-a-workgroup-and-a-homegroup.html
