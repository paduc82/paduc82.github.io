a. user say:

I'm not sure what you have in mind by a 'local backup.' A restore point is not a form of backup and never was.

In general, there are two types of backup:

1- A file and folder backup - which I'll simply call a 'backup' - is a copy of individual files and folders. This is the best solution for backing up your personal data, like your documents, pictures, music, etc.

2- A backup image - which I'll call an 'image' - is copy of everything on a partition. That is, everything from beginning to end and including the metadata that is not included in the Windows file system but is needed to make the partition bootable. An image doesn't copy files and folders; it copies 0's and 1's. An image is the only way to create a backup of Windows, including all the software you have installed on Windows.

If, like most people, you store your personal data on your C: drive, then an image of your C: drive also includes your personal data. While this is handy, it has a dark side: Let's say you tried out an experiment with your computer, and the experiment didn't work out so good. Of course, that never happens to me <cough, cough.> But now your monitor looks likes it's on a 'bad trip' (if you're old enough to remember the 60's.)

No problem, you say, I'll just restore a backup image and everything will be good. And everything is good because restoring a backup brings your computer back in time to moment when the image was made. But now your personal files have also gone back in time.

The ideal solution, then, is to move your personal folders (Desktop, Documents, Downloads, Music, Pictures and Videos) to a separate partition. Now you can image your C: drive and either image or backup your data drive separately.

Windows 10 doesn't have a 'real' backup utility. It's got File History, which does backup individual files quite effectively, but it's really inconvenient if you want to backup all your personal files at one time. For that you'll want third-party backup software.

Windows also has its own image utility but, as Mike P correctly explained, it's on the way out. In any case, it was never as capable or convenient as third-party image software at the best of times.

So, to sum up: Decide what you want to do - backup or image. Or both ... why not both? That's what I do and it's easy and automatic once you learn how. But whatever you decide to do, you're looking at third-party software.

The only time motion sickness gets me is in VR but that's pretty normal.

To reduce the effects check the in game settings, reduce head bob/sway if that's an option, reduce the sensitivity of your controller when turning etc, disable motion blur too, all those things can make matters worse. It's a case of trying to keep the display as steady as possible.

If all else fails then simply avoid games that make you feel sick.

Another school of thought is to play until you start to feel sick then stop, when you feel better play again and keep on doing that until your brain adjusts to the motion and gets used to it.

In Windows 10, your device is always kept up to date with the latest features and fixes. Updates and drivers are installed automatically, with no need to select which updates are needed or not needed. In rare cases, a specific driver or update might temporarily cause issues with your device.
