An unidentified program wants access to your computer

0FF1CE)-

Note This text contains a zero and a one for the letters "O" and "I."

  1. For Windows XP and Windows Server 2003, click Start, click Run, type %appdata%\microsoft\templates, and then click OK.

albert d. kallal say:

You have two choices here.

 

We assume that you are ‚Äúrelating‚Äù the order to the part ‚Äúid‚Äù. This thus gives you use of the cost, and thus you don‚Äôt have to store the cost in the order. (Your relation and query thus pulls the cost from that parts table). No problem so far.

 

However, of course if the part price changes, then all past orders will now show an updated and different price.

 

So, you can either:

 

If a part price changes, you add the part again to the parts table. You likely should include a date of this change, or even perhaps an ‚Äúactive‚Äù flag. That way, when the user selects a part, then the older same parts don‚Äôt show in the selection.

 

The above is often more work, but it does then give you a nice history of the part prices. And it allows you to select or add two parts of the same type to one order, each with a different price.

 

However, if your design has worked reasonable well for some time, then another approach is that when a user selects a part, you PULL the price into a column on your order details table.

 

The above then means that if you change the price of a part in the parts table, then ONLY new orders you create will reflect the updated part price. This is certainly  the least amount of work (likely 1 or 2 lines of extra code in the combo box that you using to select the part (so in after update, you pull (copy) the part price into a price column in your order details table).

 

The above thus will not keep a ‚Äúhistory‚Äù of the past part prices, but it certainly is an easy way to have new orders and past orders reflect the price of the part at the time of the order creating time.

 

So, either you add the part again with a new price (and likely a date) into the parts table (or inventory or whatever it is you have now). Or you when you select a part, always copy the price value into a column in your order details.

 

Of course another approach is to make a child table of parts with the pricing information. This would have the starting date of the price, and the price, and perhaps an active column. This of course is the most normalized approach, but can increase complexity of the queries you have to create.

 

Thus, a child table of prices to the parts table means that you would not re-enter the part again, and thus is likely the most normalized design, and would eliminate duplicate parts and prices due to such a normalized data design.

 

So, above is the 3 basic approaches I can think of right now.
