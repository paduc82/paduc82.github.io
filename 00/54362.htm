cquarry say:

Something else to think about is that the Xbox is much more sensitive to wireless issues than your laptops are. If you want to diagnose wireless issues on the Xbox, start by asking yourself a few questions:

How far away from the Xbox is your router? The ideal range for the console is between 5 and 50 feet for most routers.

What is the wireless signal going through? If you draw a straight line between your router and your Xbox, anything that line goes through (or near) can cause interference. The big issues normally come from metal or glass objects like refrigerators, mirrors, or aquariums. 

Are there any other wireless devices that might cause issues near the Xbox or the router? Things like cordless phones, microwaves, baby monitors, and wireless Turtle Beach headsets can all cause issues with wireless interference if they are too close.

Are the router wireless settings  configured to optimize the wireless connection to the Xbox? If you log into your router (we can cover how to do that later if needed) there are some settings that, if not configured properly, can cause your console to have a sub-par connection.

vibroplex say:

The 360s has its antennas in flat panels on the sides, and we want to get those to an angle where it gets a stronger signal. Rotate the Xbox 30 degrees, first in one direction then the other. Also try standing it upright if it's down flat, or vice versa.

If your wireless router has antennae, try rotating them from straight up-and-down to one o'clock, two o'clock, three o'clock positions, and revolve them, also one 30-degree step at a time, until you get a stronger signal. You need three bars for a reliable connection.

If the operating system is not activated, there is a watermark showing the edition of Windows (although it does not show to activate) on desktop, personalization features (in PC settings [simply Settings in Windows 10], it is located under Personalize in Windows 8, under PC & devices > Lock screen in Windows 8.1, and under Personalization in Windows 10) are disabled. Is the drive letter c: also the XP drive ?

Lets assume it is. (You can list the contents of the current drive and folder with command 'dir' )

You should be able to list contents of the root '\' folder and the '\windows' folder.

 

On the Repair Console type:

1. c: <enter> and then cd \   (now we are at the root of drive c:) 

2. attrib -r -s -h ntldr       (this will set the file in a deleteable state)

3. del ntldr  (deletes the file)

Change attributes for ntdetect.com and delete it.

Now try to copy again both files from the CD to drive c:

 

You can use the commands 'fixboot c:' and fixmbr to repair the system partition boot sector and the Master Partition Table if you still cannot boot after copying the files ntldr and ntdetect.com

"Description of the Windows XP Recovery Console for advanced users"
