a. user say:

In all versions of Windows since the advent of user accounts, the way to prevent other users from accessing your personal files is to store them in the default locations for user files (Documents, Music, Pictures, Video) and then to password-protect your user account with a lengthy, complex password.

The recommended (by Microsoft) way to setup user accounts is to have one user account with Administrator permissions and all other accounts to have Standard (formely Limited) permissions. As long as you do not share the Administrator account password with other users, no other user account can access your personal files. (Administrator accounts can gain access to Standard accounts, but not vice-versa.)

mastermaster-21 say:

It is not possible to store my photos and videos on those folders because they are so many GB so i have to store them in other hard disks separate from the disk that windows is installed.

My account is administrator and the others are standard but they can acess all disk drives and modify all my files.

How can i stop this happening.?

a. user say:

A Standard user cannot change any other user's files - cannot even open them. That's how Windows works 'out of the box' unless someone has been fiddling with the access permissions.

The access permissions apply to internal drives. If you are storing your pictures on external drives, the access permissions won't apply.

I'm not sure if you're aware of this: You don't have to store your personal files on the same partition as your software. In fact, it's better to store them on a separate partition and a separate physical disk is even better, but it has to be an internal disk for the access controls to apply.

You can move any or all of your default user folders (Desktop, Documents, Downloads, Music, Pictures, Videos) to a separate internal hard disk. In fact, that's what I've gone. I've got hundreds of gigabytes of pictures and videos stored on a separate 1 TB hard drive. And because it's an internal hard drive, access permissions are enforced.

a. user say:

The best way to allow other users to see your photos but not to change them is to store the photos in the cloud (e.g., Google Photos, OneDrive, Dropbox, etc.) rather than on your hard drive.

To accomplish the same objective with your internal hard drive you would need to manually alter the access permissions, which is a risky maneuver with complications that are difficult to predict. On the other hand, the cloud services were designed with secure sharing as a feature. And you have an online backup of your photos.
