ActiveDocument.ActiveWindow.View.ShowHiddenText = False
With ActiveDocument.Bookmarks("Transaction").Range.Font
    If .Hidden = True Then

    Else

    End If

into the position shown below

jay freedman say:

Making a template is the better way, but now you need to know how to use the template.

Do not "open" the template unless you intend to make changes in the template. Instead, use the File > New > My Templates command and select the template in the resulting dialog. That creates a separate document

The document will initially have the name Document1 (or some higher number). When you click Save the first time for the document, you'll automatically be shown the Save As dialog, and you should save it with a name that indicates the letter's purpose. You won't even get the chance to overwrite the template accidentally.

In the template, insert a CREATEDATE field (from the Insert > Quick Parts > Field dialog). When you see the field in the template, it will show the date when you first saved the template. Each time you create a document based on the template, the field there will show the date of creation of that specific document, not that of the template.

To extend what others here have stated, an organization your size really should have centralized management to both monitor and respond to security or other management issues that may arise.  Without it, you are depending on the awareness and willingness of your employees to inform you when attacks occur, if they are even truly aware themselves.

I've personally managed networks in both business and engineering education ranging in size from less than 100 to over 500 systems with 3500 users.  Though the larger installation was inherently more challenging, the smaller business quickly grew to 300 staff across several offices in as many states nationwide, which if it hadn't included centralized management I established early would have become a nightmare.

To understand part of the difference that this makes, the following blog article discusses the recent improvements to Windows 10 Anniversary Update Defender to help deal with Ransomware and other emerging threats.  Though the Advanced Threat Protection mentioned near the end is currently tuned for larger businesses, the entire article hints at the aggressive stance that Microsoft is taking towards malware with Windows 10 and Defender in general.

Defending against ransomware with Windows 10 Anniversary Update

I'd personally consider a business your size as on the edge between small and medium business, which includes the possibility for volume license agreements that can make things like centralized management more cost effective.  Whether you attempt to do this immediately or not, I'd begin the process to determine when it might make sense to plan for this migration.  Nothing can slow or stop the growth of a company in its tracks like a significant cyber breach of corporate or customer data in today's unforgiving business environment.

As for the use of Malwarebytes, the question there is whether your users are typically allowed to download or install 3rd-party software on their systems, since this and other threats more commonly associated with consumer users is what the MBAM product is best designed to deal with.  A properly managed business network with policies in place to limit what users can do to their systems should avoid most of these types of malware quite easily, which is why this is so important.

Note that even the Malwarebytes Business Solution product is designed with centralized management  for this very reason.  It's also common that the preferences of your employees aren't as well aligned to those of the business as you might think, since the most dangerous risks to an organization often come from within.  Snowden and others we've seen in the news recently are perfect examples of this unrecognized risk and I've dealt with this personally in both the education and business environments which I managed.
