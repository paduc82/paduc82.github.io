You can turn off hibernation if it is on and you don't use it..

When you hibernate your computer, Windows saves the contents of the system's memory to the hiberfil.sys file. As a result, the size of the hiberfil.sys file will always equal the amount of physical memory in your system. If you don't use the hibernate feature and want to recapture the space that Windows uses for the hiberfil.sys file, perform the following steps:

- Start the Control Panel Power Options applet (go to Start, Settings, Control Panel, and click Power Options).
- Select the Hibernate tab, clear the "Enable hibernation" check box, then click OK; although you might think otherwise, selecting Never under the "System hibernates" option on the Power Schemes tab doesn't delete the hiberfil.sys file.
- Windows will remove the "System hibernates" option from the Power Schemes tab and delete the hiberfil.sys file.

lemp say:
The BSSID is the MAC address of your router.  Most home wireless routers will have two BSSIDs, one for the wired section and the other for the wireless access point.

Typically, you find the BSSID using the router's configuration utility.  On my old Linksys WRT54G, the information is on the "Status" tab and then on the "Local Network" and "Wireless" sub-tabs.

The first 3 "octets' of the MAC address (in your example 00:12:34 or 02:12:34) typically show the identify of the manufacturer of your device.  If you have a Linksys router, then your two BSSIDs should both begin 00:14:BF. 

Are you possibly using some tool that is showing the MAC addresses in the reverse order?  Again using my Linksys WRT54G as an example, the MAC address for the wireless access point differs from the MAC address of the Ethernet adapter by only 2 in the right-most two digits.

That is:

Local Network MAC --  00:14:BF:3A:52:C4
Wireless MAC           --  00:14:BF:3A:52:C6

You can put any MAC address into the tool here to find out the device manufacturer --> http://www.techzoom.net/tools/check-mac.en

How are you determining the BSSID of "the other network" near you?

What tool are you using to troubleshoot your network that gives you the information for the other network?
