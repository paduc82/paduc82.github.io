When the end user selects Department B in Department Level 1, the Department Level 2 should be listed as Department B1 and Department B2 for choice, because Department B has two sub-departments.

I have tried the Combo Box, but I have not succeeded to do that, anyone can help me? Thanks in advance!
ken sheridan say: This 'missing link' scenario is quite a common one with many types of data, e.g. small countries might not have any regional structure so there is a gap in the mapping from town to country.  The way this is handled is to supply a value such as N/A to complete the chain.  With your data you would have tables like this:

Level1Departments
....Level1DepartmentID (PK)
....Level1Department

Level2Departments
....Level2DepartmentID (PK)
....Level2Department
....Level1DepartmentID (FK)

Employees
....EmployeeID
....Level2DepartmentID (FK)
....FirstName
....LastName
....etc

PK = primary key; FK = foreign key.

Note that Employees references Level2Departments only, not Level1Departments.  Each row in Employees maps to Level1Departments via Level2Departments.  So Level1Departments has rows:

Level1DepartmentID    Level1Department

1                                        DepartmentA
2                                        DepartmentB
3                                        DepartmentC

Level2Departments has rows:

Level2DepartmentID    Level2Department    Level1DepartmentID

1                                         N/A                                1
2                                         DepartmentB1            2
3                                         DepartmentB2            2
4                                         N/A                                3

Employees has:

EmployeeID    Level2DepartmentID    FirstName    LastName

1                        1                                       Jack                 White
2                        3                                       Jone                 Black

The combo box in a form for entering rows into Employees would be set up as follows:

Name:                   cboDepartment
ControlSource:    Level2DepartmentID

RowSource:    SELECT Level2DepartmentID, Level2Department, Level1Department
FROM Level1Departments INNER JOIN Level2Departments
ON Level1Departments.Level1DepartmentID = Level2Departments.Level1DepartmentID
ORDER BY Level1Department, Level2Department;

BoundColumn:      1
ColumnCount:       3
ColumnWidths:     0cm;3cm;3cm
ListWidth:               6cm

If your units of measurement are imperial rather than metric Access will automatically convert them  The important thing is that the first dimension is zero to hide the first column.  Experiment with the second two to get the best fit.  The ListWidth should be the sum of the two column widths.

You should now be able to select a department and sub-department from the combo box's list.  For departments without a sub-department the row with N/A in the second column (which will be the only row for this department) is selected.

When you select an item from the list the combo box will show the sub-department.  To show the department add an unbound text box to the form and set its ControlSource property to:

=cboDepartment.Column(2)

The Column property is zero-based, so Column(2) is the third column, Level1Department.  Set the Locked property of this control to True (Yes) and its Enabled property to False (No) to make it read only.

You'll find a file which demonstrates this, and other methods of dealing with linear hierarchies, as ComboDemo2k.zip in my public databases folder at:

https://skydrive.live.com/?cid=44CC60D7FEA42912&id=44CC60D7FEA42912!169

You might have to copy and paste the text of the above (not copy the link location) into your browser; for some reason it sometimes doesn't seem to work as a hyperlink.

The form which illustrates the above method is the one 'using multi-column combo box and text box' opened with the fourth button down on the opening form.  It doesn't in fact include any N/A levels as the type of data used has no gaps in the hierarchies, and it has three levels rather than two, but it is otherwise just the same.

sonatacare say: It is simple and useful, it solved my problem.

I made two queries.

Query 1

SELECT Level_1 AS Department, Count([Level_1]) AS EmpCount

FROM tblAssignment INNER JOIN tblDepartment ON tblAssignment.DeptID   = tblDepartment.DeptID 

GROUP BY Level_1


Query 2

SELECT  [Level_1] & " " & [Level_2] AS Department, Count([Level_2]) AS EmpCount

FROM tblAssignment INNER JOIN tblDepartment ON tblAssignment.DeptID   = tblDepartment.DeptID 

GROUP BY [Level_1] & " " & [Level_2];

In Word 2013, the Merriam-Webster dictionary is one of the "Apps for Office" on the Insert tab. In Word 2016 the name of the feature has changed to "Office Add-ins" but it's the same thing in the same place.

To remove it, click the "My Apps" or "My Add-ins" button to open this panel:

Click the "Manage My Apps" or "Manage My Add-ins" link at the upper right as shown by the arrow. That opens your browser, where you'll be asked to log in to your Microsoft account. You'll be shown a list of the apps/add-ins that you've signed up for. Click the "Hide" link to the right of the Merriam-Webster dictionary. You may log out and close your browser. Back in Word, click the Refresh link at the top right of the apps/add-ins panel.

A longer description of this is at Refresh the Office Add-ins list.
