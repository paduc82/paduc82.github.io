john w. vinson say:

Well, one problem I see is that you're referring to "fields" on a Form. Forms don't have Fields: they have Controls, which may be bound to Fields in the form's Recordsource table, or may be calculated on the fly.

Calculated fields - such as your first three - should not be stored in any Table; they should instead be calculated on the fly. For instance you can display the sum of fields A, B and C in a textbox on a Form by simply setting that textbox's Control Source property to

=[A] + [B] + [C]

You don't need the Exp3, which probably came from trying to do the calculation in a Query.

To look up data from some other table, as it appears that you're doing in the fourth expression, you can use the DLookUp function:

=DLookUp("SomeFieldName", "SomeTableName", "optional query criteria")

I would also (VERY STRONGLY!!) suggest that you avoid using special characters such as period., slash, or even blank in the names of fields or controls. Access can and will get confused; in particular, a period is a delimiter used for constructs like tablename.fieldname or formname.controlname. Having a field or a control named [AmtLtrSCDMV] is just asking for trouble - on a Form, each control will usually be bound to a single field in the Form's Recordsource query such as [trSCDMV].

That said... if you have any number of calculated controls on a Form, you can put a line

Me.Recalc

in the VBA of a button's Click event (or any other appropriate event, such as the AfterUpdate event of a control which goes into the calculation) to recalculate all the calculated controls on the form.

scottgem say:

First, I suggest you do some research on Normalization. Over 40 years ago, the design requirements for a relational database were first postulated. The rules of normalization have existed virtually unchanged since then. They have stood the test of time and represent the most efficient way to store relational data.

So you are saying it is not possible that a car would ever be towed more than once or that a car owner would never have a car towed more than once? I can understand that it may be rare, but not impossible.

So yes, I do think you need to normalize your data. And why do you think a normalized structure means you can't do everything on one form? The way I envision it, the user would enter the vehicle's VIN. Access would then check to see if that VIN was ever towed before. If it wasn't then you main form would be opened to blank record with the VIN filled in. Otherwise it would bring up the record of that VIN. You would have separate subforms for the vehicle owner and lienholder and another one in Continuous form mode for the fees. If there is a new owner or lienholder, the user deactivates the old one and either selects or enters new ones. Then the user enters the fees, again only entering the applicable fees. This would make invoicing easier and eliminate a lot of blank fields. It also eliminates the complex expression to sum the fees. You could also categorize the fee types so you can get subtotals very easily. Also, if you ever add a new fee, you just need to add it to your fee types.. Otherwise you need to modify your table, form and reports.

Like I said, there are very good reasons, the principles of normalization have existed, virtually unchanged for over 40 years. I urge you to look more closely at this.
