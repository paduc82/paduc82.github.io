jeffhubb_ say:

I found a trick that works in 2010, so I'd imagine this also works in 2007.  On the page, type"file:relativepath" and it should automatically create a hyperlink to the file.  Do include quotes around the link or else files with spaces in their name won't work.  Remember to use ..\ to go up one folder (notebooks and section groups are folders).  In Vista and Windows 7 if you hold the Shift key and right-click a file in Windows Explorer, a hidden command "Copy as path" will appear: select this and Paste it into OneNote to get you started.  Then prepend file: to this path (inside the quotes) and fix the relative path with..\ as needed.

For example, suppose you want to link to "D:\Pictures\image.jpg" and your notebook is "D:\Notebooks\Lessons".  Your relative link will be "file:..\..\Pictures\image.jpg".  The first..\ gets out of Lessons, the second gets out of Notebooks.  If your Lessons notebook has a section group named "CSE 101" and you want your link in here, you'll need a third..\ to also get out of the section group.  That means these links will break if you move pages into section groups of different hierarchical levels.

Do not use the hyperlink dialog!  Do not use Ctrl+K!  As soon as you use the hyperlink dialog, OneNote will try to be "smart" and convert it to a UNC server or http path, which of course won't work.  That means you can't assign friendly names to these links - you're stuck with the filepath, but hey, it works.

jay freedman say:
The font change is caused by a setting in the AutoFormat As You Type options. To turn it off, click the Office button and click Word Options. On the left side of the Options dialog, click Proofing, and then click the AutoCorrect Options button. In the next dialog, click the AutoFormat As You Type tab (note: not the AutoFormat tab).

In this page, uncheck at least the option "Built-in Heading styles". This option, when turned on, causes Word to assume that a single line with a paragraph mark at the end is a heading, and it applies a built-in Heading style to the paragraph.

In fact, all of the entries in the "Apply as you type" section are troublemakers, guessing wrong more often than right, and you should leave all of them unchecked. Some of the others in this page are also more trouble than they're worth unless you know what they do and are prepared for them; see http://wordfaqs.mvps.org/TameAutoFormat.htm for more.

mosseyman say:

Thank you Jay! You are a true star! I was having the same problem in Word for Mac 2011 and it was driving me crazy, but now it is sorted. It is the same issue in both Word versions, but the path one has to take to reach the settings in Word for Mac 2011 is slightly different. For anyone using this version of Word, this is what you need to do:

  1. With a Word document open as your current active application, click Word at the left of the toolbar at the top of the Mac screen and select Preferences. This will open the Word Preferences dialogue box.
  2. Under the heading Authoring and Proofing Tools click on Autocorrect.
  3. In the Autocorrect dialogue box select the AutoFormat as You Type button.
  4. Under the Apply as you type heading there is a check box titled Headings. Un-tick this.

GMAIL'S RECOMMENDED IMAP CLIENT SETTINGS

Sending:

Do NOT save sent messages on the server. If your client is sending mail
through Gmail's SMTP2 server, your sent messages will be automatically
copied to the [Gmail]/Sent Mail folder.

DO save draft messages on the server. If you want your drafts in your mail
client to sync correctly with Gmail's web interface, set your client to save
drafts to the [Gmail]/Drafts folder.

Deleting:

Do NOT save deleted messages on the server. Messages that are deleted from
an IMAP folder (except for those in [Gmail]/Spam or [Gmail]/Trash) only have
that label removed and still exist in All Mail. Hence, your client doesn't
need to store an extra copy of a deleted message.

Do NOT save deleted messages to your [Gmail]/Trash folder because this will
delete a message in all folders.

Do NOT save deleted messages to your [Gmail]/All Mail folder as some clients
will try to empty this folder and ultimately fail. This can lead to delayed
mail access or excessive battery consumption on a mobile device.

Junk mail and spam:

Do NOT enable your client's junk mail filters. Gmail's spam filters also
work in your IMAP client, and we recommend turning off any additional
anti-spam or junk mail filters within your client. Your client's filter will
attempt to download and classify all of your existing messages, which may
slow down your client until the process is complete.

Hello,

In PowerPoint 2010 and later, you can insert an embedded video (the default behavior) or link to a video file stored on your PC. Linking a video file would keep your presentation file smaller, but links can break. We recommend that you store the presentation and the linked videos in the same folder. Here's how to embed or insert a link to a video stored on your PC:

  1. In Normal View, click the slide you where you want the link to the video to be in.
  2. On the Insert tab, click the arrow under Video, and then click Video on my PC.
  3. In the Insert Video box, click the file you want to link to, click the down arrow next to the Insert button, and then select Insert or Link to File.

After inserting the video, you may set the video to play automatically or when clicked. By default, the video plays as part of the click sequence. That means that when you click anywhere on the slide to advance to the next step, the video plays. This Start option is called In Click Sequence. If you want to play it automatically as soon as it appears on-screen, you may follow the steps below:

  1. Select the video frame.
  2. On the PowerPoint 2016 ribbon, on the Video Tools Playback tab, open the Start list and choose the option you prefer:
    • In Click Sequence
      • This is the default behavior. When you click anywhere, the video plays.
    • Automatically
      • The video starts automatically. If there are animation steps before it, the animation would play first before the video.
    • When Clicked On
      • The video starts only when it has been clicked.

If you have further questions about inserting a video to PowerPoint, feel free to ask us.
