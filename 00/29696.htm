skat0r vista say:

Hello,

By default, certain files are hidden.  If some of these files are edited, moved, renamed, or deleted, you could damage your operating system. They are hidden for a reason.

Q. Hidden files or folders cannot be seen by any user?

A. You can see the hidden files if you wish. Here's how to view them.

  1. Go the start menu and type folder options.
  2. Hit enter or click on the result.
  3. Go to the View tab.
  4. Under Hidden files and folders, select Show hidden files, folders, and drives.
  5. Optional: If you want, you can uncheck Hide protected operating system files (Recommended).  I would keep that checked if I were you.
  6. At the bottom, hit Apply or OK.

Q. Can only be seen by the main user

A. If the user follows the instructions above, they can see it.  So the answer is no, anybody can see hidden files and folders.

Q. Are use by the operating systems to do its tasks

A. Most of them are.  E.g. If you delete one of your hidden personal documents, the file is not critical to the OS.  If you however delete system32 (don't delete it!), Windows will stop working immediately.

Q. Are not usually edited directly

A. Personal files (pictures, documents, videos, etc.) can be edited.  Operating System files cannot be edited.

Hopefully that clears any misunderstandings.  Please let me know if you have any other questions.

milesrf say:

You might try disconnecting your computer from the internet for a day or two and see if that stops the spam.

 

Adding another antivirus program without first uninstalling your current antivirus program is NOT recommended - antivirus problems tend to see many parts of other antivirus programs as similar enough to viruses that they should treat them as viruses.

 

However, there's another class of programs known as antispyware programs that you may want to try.  Windows Defender is one of them, so you may want to try it first.  Programs in this class seldom interfere with each other, so you can safely have more than one installed.

 

Three other possibilities to check:

 

1.  A spammer might have found your login information for your email account, and started sending the spam using your account but without using your computer at all.  In this case, changing the password on your email account is often enough, so try that.  Make sure that your new password is near the maximum length the email server allows, to make it very slow for computer programs to run through all the possible guesses of what it could be.  Also, try to make it include all the classes or characters allowed (often lower case letters, UPPER CASE LETTERS, numerals, and special characters) to slow down such a program even more.  In this case, you'll probably also need to contact the people running that server to make sure they don't disable your account.

 

2.  A spammer might have found just your email address and started using it on the spam without sending it to the same email server you use.  In this case, you'll need the headers of a few of the spam messages to do much.  Most of the header lines aren't normally shown when you look at the email; the extra steps needed to show them depend heavily on which email program you want to show them.

 

3.  If your computer allows remote logins, the spammer may have guessed your username and password for your computer.  In that case, a password change, but on your computer instead, is likely to be needed.  You may also want to look up remote logins and how to disable them in the help file for your computer.  Some of the email programs have a Sent folder, so you may want to check for spam messages there.  Also, the spammer might have created an extra account on your computer, so find the full list of accounts, and check the Sent folder for every account.on that list.
