jim gordon mvp say:

Copy equations from Word

By Bob Mathews, WIRIS

Technical Level: Beginner

Summary

This article explains, as the title states, how to copy equations from Word. More specifically, it describes how to copy them such that pasting them to the intended destination is successful. Techniques described here are valid for Word 2007 and later for Windows and Word 2011 and later for Mac.

Details

It should "just work"

Copy & paste – doesn't it "just work"? Well yes and no. The reason it's necessary to describe copying equations from Word is because Word gives you the choice of 2 clipboard formats. You can

  • Copy MathML to the clipboard as plain text, or
  • Copy linear format to the clipboard as plain text.

When you install Office, the default clipboard format for equations in Word is to copy them as plain text. If you remain within Word, it does in fact "just work", regardless of which format choice you make. Even copying from Word to paste into PowerPoint normally doesn't require any special consideration (except for Office 2007 on Windows and Office 2011 on Mac). If you stay within the same version of Office, your choice of clipboard format won't matter.

If linear format works, why choose MathML?

That's the question, and the answer is simple: don't. IF the default "copy linear format" works, you needn't worry about choosing MathML. "If it ain't broke", you know you don't need to fix it, but when would you ever MathML. "If it ain't broke", you know you don't need to fix it, but when would you ever need to choose MathML? Not at all an exhaustive list, but here are some cases where you'll need MathML rather than linear format:

  • Pasting the equation into a computation engine, such as Maple or Mathematica.
  • Pasting into another equation editor, such as MathType.
  • Pasting into another word processor, such as Apple Pages.
  • Pasting into some web pages.

Basically, when pasting into any non-Office application, you should try MathML. If the application understands MathML, that's absolutely the better choice. There's a good chance that such an application won't understand either format, MathML or linear. If that's the case, the best solution may be to copy and paste as linear format, because at least it will be mostly human-readable. For example, compare the MathML for a simple expression – the square root of 2 – with that expression's linear format:

<mml:math xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:m="http://schemas.openxmlformats.org/officeDocument/2006/math"><mml:msqrt><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:msqrt></mml:math> √2
MathML linear format
Where do I go to make the selection?

The place to choose between MathML and linear format is in the Equation Options dialog (or on Mac, simply Equations). Where you find this dialog depends on your version of Word.

  • Windows. First insert an equation. After inserting an equation, look for the Equation Tools/Design tab. Now click the dialog launcher – for the Equation Tools/Design tab. Now click the dialog launcher –  – in the Tools section of that tab (Word 2016, Conversions section).
  • Mac in the Tools section of that tab (Word 2016, Conversions section).
  • Mac
    • Word 2011. InWord Preferences, chooseEquations.
    • Word 2016. In the Format menu, choose Equation Options.

Once you find the dialog, the choices are identical regardless of your operating system of version of Word:

doug robbins - mvp office apps & services (word) say:

If you run a macro containing the following code, (on a copy of the document), it will insert the name of the author of the comment and the text of the comment directly into the document.

Dim acomment As Comment
With ActiveDocument
    For Each acomment In .Comments
        acomment.Reference.InsertBefore "[" & acomment.Author & " - " & acomment.Range.Text & "]"
    Next acomment
End With

Due to the insertion of that information into the text itself, it will of course alter the layout\pagination of the document.
