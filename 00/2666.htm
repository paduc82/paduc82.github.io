If you have not changed the location where Word stores its templates you can find it by typing
C:\Users\<YOUR_USER_NAME>\AppData\Roaming\Microsoft\Templates
in the Address bar of Windows or Internet Explorer (not Firefox etc).

Ensure that your old normal.dot from Word 2003 is in this folder.

Start Word, and it will create a new Normal template based on the old 2003 template.

From File > Options > Advanced > Save ensure that prompt to save the normal template when quitting Word is checked and ideally Always create a backup should also be checked.

Close Word and at the prompt allow it to save the normal template.

Remove normal.dot from the user templates folder to a safe location leaving in place normal.dotm.

Ensure that there are no files called 'normal' in the Word Startup folder.

Restart Word and your autotext entries should be present. You can evaluate them using the building blocks organizer.

shane devenshire say:

Which one?

The Mapping Workbooks described in the early part of this thread are Excel workbooks made by Microsoft which give most of the 2003 commands in each of the 5 major apps and then the 2007 equivalent.  It can be a little difficult to navigate at first.

The Interactive guides, again by Microsoft, are programs you can download to your computer.  When you click the hyperlinks above you will be taken to the download site.  Follow the instructions to download any and all that you want.  When its on your computer, just double click the file to run these programs.  You don't open them inside of Excel or Word or....  When they open up you will be asked to press Start.  Then you will see, for example, the Excel 2003 screen, where you do a command the way you did it in 2003.  Then, most of the time, the 2003 screen will fade out and the 2007 screen will appear and you can watch the 2007 version of the command get executed.  Sometime you may get a screen tip insteads the 2007 interface.  Click anywhere on the screen and you're back to the 2003 interface where you can check out another command.  My opinion is that once you see how this works you will find it much easier that the Mapping Workbooks.

I have been using 2007 since 2005 (Beta) and I still use the interactive guides from time to time.

hansv mvp say:

For monthly payments on the 19th of the month:

=DATE(YEAR(TODAY()-19),MONTH(TODAY()-19)+1,19)

For quarterly payments on the 19th of January, April, July and October:

=DATE(YEAR(TODAY()-19),MONTH(TODAY()-19)+3-MOD(MONTH(TODAY()-19)-1,3),19)

For semi-annual payments on the 19th of January and July:

=DATE(YEAR(TODAY()-19),MONTH(TODAY()-19)+6-MOD(MONTH(TODAY()-19)-1,6),19)

hansv mvp say:

You are correct that the 19 stands for the day of the month, and that 3 stands for the interval: 3 for quarterly payments, 6 for semi-annual payments.

For monthly payments, use interval 1 instead of 3 or 6:

=DATE(YEAR(TODAY()-19),MONTH(TODAY()-19)+1-MOD(MONTH(TODAY()-19)-1,1),19)

But since MOD(MONTH(TODAY()-19)-1,1) = 0 regardless of today's month, we can omit this part, and the formula becomes shorter:

=DATE(YEAR(TODAY()-19),MONTH(TODAY()-19)+1,19)

which is the formula that I posted. So in fact, it uses the same layout as the other formulas, but omitting the part that always evaluates to 0.

The -1 after the month is because we start at the first month (January), If you want to start in February, use -2, and if you want to start in March, use -3, etc.

So for example, for quarterly payments on the 13th of February, May, August and November, use

=DATE(YEAR(TODAY()-13),MONTH(TODAY()-13)+3-MOD(MONTH(TODAY()-13)-2,3),13)

13 = payment day

3 = interval (quarterly = every 3 months)

2 = start month (February)

And for semi-annual payments on the 7th of April and October, use

=DATE(YEAR(TODAY()-7),MONTH(TODAY()-7)+6-MOD(MONTH(TODAY()-7)-4,6),7)

7 = payment day

6 = interval (semi-annual = every 6 months)

4 = start month (April)

For monthly payments on the 5th of each month:

=DATE(YEAR(TODAY()-5),MONTH(TODAY()-5)+1,5)

5 = payment day

1 = interval

start month is irrelevant here.
