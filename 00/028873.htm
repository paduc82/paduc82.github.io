jllatham say:

I'm going to take a first stab at it. First, I agree with Tom Ogilvy that the codes in A0A-Y1Z99 are going to be a bit problematic.

For this to work, you need to re-arrange and expand your table.  Assuming columns A, B and C are used for the table, we will be examining the "From Zipcode" column; A.  It MUST  be in ascending order, with no gaps and it needs to include entries that are outside of it at both ends. Part of being in ascending order is to have numeric type entries ahead of entries that begin with letters of the alphabet.  I created a table that looks like this:

From    To       Sales Team
     0     500     NONE
  501     544     Team 2
  545    7010    NONE
7011    7011     Team 3
7012    9999     NONE
 A0A    Y1Z99    Team 1
Y2        ZZZZ    NONE

Now, lets say you enter 503 into cell F2 as the zip code to lookup, then this formula in a cell would return the associated Team #:
=INDEX(A$2:C$8,MATCH(F2,A$2:A$8),3)

For 503 in F2, it will return Team 2,  for 350 or 600 or 8000 it would return NONE.  For pretty much any entry beginning with a letter from A to Y,  you'll get Team 1.  You'll get Team 3 only when you look for 7011.

Pointing to text within a Word document - Microsoft Community

easyjohn say:

Pointing to text within a Word document

Does anyone have a technique or tool to be used with Word to facilitate bringing attention to a topic or picture in a document?  I would like to highlight, or point to, a topic in a Word document with an arrow, or pointer, symbol by overlaying the symbol on the text in the document without altering any text that might be overplayed.

jay freedman say:

The setup for this may seem a bit complicated, but it will make its use as simple as possible.

For some background, read Marginal Text. The difference in what follows is mainly that the item in the margin will be a shape rather than text, and that the whole thing will be stored as an AutoText entry. 

To start, open a document or start a new blank document. Right-click the Quick Access Toolbar and choose Customize Quick Access Toolbar. In the dialog, set the dropdown above the left list to "All Commands". Scroll the list and click on Insert Horizontal Frame, and click the Add button between the lists; then click OK.

In the document body, click in any paragraph. Click the Insert Horizontal Frame button on the Quick Access Toolbar, and drag a rectangle in the left margin to about the size you want for your arrow or a little larger.

Click the Insert tab of the ribbon, click the Shapes button, and click the right arrow in the Block Arrows group on the gallery. Drag the cursor to create the arrow at the size you want. Click the Layout Options button that appears immediately to the right of the arrow, and click the In Line with Text icon in the gallery. The arrow will go into the text paragraph next to the frame; drag it and drop it inside the frame.

-----

Note: Having the shape inside a frame and set to In Line with Text will not allow you to place the arrow in the middle of text or a picture without disturbing the text or picture. If you want to do that, don't use a frame at all, and set the shape's layout (text wrapping) to In Front of Text.

-----

While the arrow is selected, you can use the buttons on the Drawing Tools tab of the ribbon to change its fill color, its outline color, and its size to your liking. The yellow dots also enable you to change the thickness of the arrow's shaft and the length of the arrowhead.

Move the mouse cursor to the box around the frame (the cursor changes to a four-headed arrow), right-click, and choose Borders and Shading. In the dialog, click None to remove the solid border (you'll still see a dashed box until you deselect the frame).

Right-click the edge of the frame again and choose Format Frame. In the dialog, adjust the Horizontal and Vertical position settings. For the Horizontal, I recommend setting the Position to the negative of the Width value relative to the Margin -- for example, if the Width is 0.7", the Position should be -0.7" relative to Margin, and the Distance from Text should be set to zero. For the Vertical, set 0" relative to Paragraph, and again the Distance from Text should be set to zero. Click OK.

Finally, with the frame still selected, press Alt+F3 to open the Create New Building Block dialog. Enter a name such as Arrow. Leave the gallery set to AutoText. Click in the Category dropdown and click Create New Category; in the smaller dialog, enter a name such as _Importance (the underscore will ensure that the new category will appear at the top of the AutoText gallery when you want to insert the arrow). You can leave the other fields as is, or click the Save In dropdown and choose a different template to store the entry. Only templates that are currently open in Word will be available.

You can store as many different AutoText entries as you want, such as arrows with different colors, or other shapes, or shapes with text inside them. Each one just needs a different AutoText name.

To use a saved entry, click in the paragraph where it should appear, click Insert > Quick Parts > AutoText, and click the picture of the desired entry in the gallery. For maximum efficiency, you can add an AutoText button to the Quick Access Toolbar, which will open the gallery with one click.
