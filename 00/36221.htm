joel ivory johnson say: You are going to need a program that will record the audio. I typically use Audacity. Audacity has a lot of editing options. You may want to ignore most of the options and just make use of the record, stop, and play buttons. 

Your computer may be able to record from more than one source (Line in, Microphone, other programs, so on).  In Audacity if you go to the "Edit" menu and select "Preferences" the window that pops up (Audacity Preferences) will let you select a recording source. Once that is done press the record button and start playing your music from your device. Your computer might not be able to "Play" sound that is coming in on the Line In. If there is some way for your audio system to play something through both the speakers and the line out then you will want to use that.  If that isn't an option then watch the screen. You should be able to see a graph of the sound waves as the recording is going on. When the sound wave goes flat you've reached a point of silence (and likely the end of a song). You can press "Stop" to end the recording and then save it as an MP3. 

There are also products available in the software section of some electronic stores that will automate part of this process for you.

bill smithers say:

If the Laptop/Computer came with the Windows Operating System preinstalled (Dell, HP, Acer, etc) , that Product Key that came with the Laptop/Computer is for the preinstalled OEM License and is non-transferable.

The OEM License/Product Key (preinstalled Operating Systems on Dell, HP, etc machines) is tied to the first machine it is installed on and dies when that machine dies.

The above rule also applies to OEM System Builders Licenses.

Retail Operating System License, that you buy and install yourself, are allowed to be transferred to another machine.

But Retail Licenses can also only be installed on one machine at any particular time.

Q: Does the Win10 computer-in-question ONLY connect to the internet via Wi-Fi (i.e., a wireless router), not mobile wireless?

A: Yes

Is the Win8.1 computer's Wi-Fi connection also configured as a metered connection?

COMMENT: After setting your network connection to metered, Windows Update will only download priority updates. Very generally speaking, the vast majority of Win10 updates are "priority" updates. These include cumulative updates for Win10 (once-a-month or as many as five-a-month or more), Flash Player updates and (I assume by now) the Win10 Anniversary Update & any prerequisite updates required.

The cumulative update for Win10 1511 released earlier this week would be 989.9 MB if you downloaded/installed it manually (a little smaller via Windows Update). If Win10 Anniversary Update was installed, it'd be 779.7 MB manually.

Are you familiar with Win10's Delivery Optimization feature? => https://privacy.microsoft.com/en-US/windows-10-windows-update-delivery-optimization

If it's a Notebook, Tablet or Surface PC, you may be better off updating the computer at work (if you're allowed to do so) or at a library or "hot spot" - after disabling the metered setting & re-enabling Windows Update. Doing so would allow the computer to get its updates from other computers as well as via Windows Update.

COMMENT: For the last decade, at least, the Conventional Wisdom in Redmond (home of Microsoft) has been that everyone in the world has unlimited access to a broadband connection 24/7. Sadly, such is not the case.

PS: You want to get KB3192441, KB3194343 and possibly KB3191208 (if offered) installed ASAP. Better yet, get Win10 Anniversary Update & then KB3194798, KB3194343 and possibly KB3197099 installed.

...and don't forget to update Windows Defender manually (via the UPDATE button on Defender's Update tab) at least three times-a-week, preferably daily.
