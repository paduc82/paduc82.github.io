bob jones aka: cybertaz say:
Two primary factors:

  1. Apple's Migration Assistant is a dependable convenience for transferring user files [documents] as well as simple,standalone apps, but it usually fails with regard to complex, integrated suites of software [e.g., Office, Adobe CS].
  2. Office 2011 is device-specific, so it cannot be simply copied from one Mac to another. Even if Migration Assistant was effective, the same Product Key cannot be used for more installs than allowed by the license. [In this case you're essentially trying to do a 4th install.]
You need to Remove Office 2011  from the original Mac, remove what was migrated to the new Mac, then do a fresh installation on the new Mac.

Future Value Formula Compounded Daily - Microsoft Community

ronaldornal say:

Future Value Formula Compounded Daily

I am having trouble with the =FV formula.

I would like to make two formulas:

One formula where I put in $5,000 into a savings account at the beginning of the year at 1.05% interest compounded daily.  No further deposits and no future years just want one year on how much it will accumulate.

Second formula where I put in $5,000 into a savings account at the beginning of the year at 1.05% interest compounded daily and I deposit $1,000 every month.  Since it is compounded daily, it would matter which day I deposited into the account so how would I account for different days that I deposit it into the account?  For example:

Start ~ $5,000
Feb 12 ~ $1,000
March 3 ~ $1,000

VS.

Start ~ $5,000
Feb 25 ~ $1,000
July 3 ~ $1000

Since it is compounded daily, the depositing dates will make a difference.

What would be the two formulas I would use or how should I structure the second scenario?
bernard liengme say: Best wishes

joeu2004 say: Ronald wrote:
One formula where I put in $5,000 into a savings account at the beginning of the year at 1.05% interest compounded daily.  No further deposits and no future years just want one year on how much it will accumulate.

Ostensibly:

=FV(A1/365,365,0,-A2)

where A1 is 1.05% or 0.0105, and A2 is 5000.  Note that sign change (-A2) in the 4th parameter.

Caveat:  In the US, there is a difference between the term "interest" and "APY" (annual percentage yield).

If the annual rate is truly called "interest rate" or "APR" (annual percentage rate), the daily rate is 1.05%/365.

But if the annual rate is called "yield" or "APY", the daily rate is (1+1.05%)^(1/365)-1.

(And US banks have the option of using 366 in leap years.)

This will probably not make a significant difference for one-year calculations.  But it might make a difference over longer time frames.

Ronald wrote:
Second formula where I put in $5,000 into a savings account at the beginning of the year at 1.05% interest compounded daily and I deposit $1,000 every month.  Since it is compounded daily, it would matter which day I deposited into the account so how would I account for different days that I deposit it into the account?  For example:
Start ~ $5,000
Feb 12 ~ $1,000
March 3 ~ $1,000

Ostensibly:

=SUMPRODUCT(FV(A1/365,EDATE(A2,12)-A2:A13,0,-B2:B13))

where A1 is 1.05% or 0.0105, A2:A13 are the deposit dates, and B2:B13 are the deposit amounts.

Note that A2 should be the date of the first deposit, not "start".  And A2:A13 should be actual Excel dates formatted as Custom "mmm d" (without quotes) or the equivalent, not text that looks like a date.

Rounding note....  Both formulas should be rounded to 2 decimal places explicitly.  And these formulas do not account for periodic rounding if interest is paid to the account periodically (which might be a different schedule than your periodic deposits),
