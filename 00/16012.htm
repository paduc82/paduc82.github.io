a. user say:

Microsoft: Why do we see unfinished features?

It may seem like an obvious question, but...  I have been around software engineering for a LONG time, and there's a deeper meaning.

In some cases we see entries in the Modern UI for things like backup that are just stubs.  There are completely non-functional dialogs.   There are entries like zPC Settings.  Even the Windows Update interface is only about 1/4 baked.

Professional software engineering does not expose features in the midst of development that do not yet come even close to working.

Nor should they be exposed in this state!   Instead you should be waiting until "feature complete" before making it available to the public.

It's impossible for users to know the context of partially complete software in order to provide meaningful feedback.

Users - even seasoned software engineers not privy to inside info at Microsoft - can only perceive half-finished software as "Microsoft must think this is finished enough to show it off - so I need to complain that it doesn't work or that it just sucks".  And frankly it DOES suck to walk into a menu and find out the functionality is just not there or is just broken - especially in light of the fact that you're already actively deleting the "legacy" features the new work is supposed to replace.

It's not hard to make sure the top level menu items just don't show if the feature is simply non-functional.  Who benefits, exactly, by a Backup entry in the Settings App that does nothing at all?  YOU know where it's going to go in.  That's enough for now.

Even your support engineers respond to questions about unfinished features as though they should be expected to work.

If I may be so bold, there are only a few reasons why we should be seeing your work unfinished in such a manner.

...Your Marketing people are forcing for you to expose work in progress, so as to engage the public.  This is a good idea at a high level, but it actually takes work and especially funding to do properly.  There is no way to just pop out internal builds to Joe User and hope for the best.  User's don't know the context!  You need an organization to approve and test what goes out, implying someone in charge of what the public sees.  That needs to be someone's FULL TIME job, not just an afterthought.  And that someone needs to have experience.

...Your software development management (and systems?) have gotten so out of control that you can't actually predict what's going to be in a particular set of software until after it's built.  This would be a nice way of saying "abandon all hope", because something as complex as Windows simply cannot be managed in a seat-of-the-pants fashion.  You folks still DO care about things like quality procedures, ISO 9000, etc., right?

...You have de-funded or under-funded your software configuration management organization to the point where they can't support the need to make clean builds available to the public, so you're trying to "wing it" by just sending out what your engineering folks do directly.  Trust me, this isn't a good thing to do.

...You think these features actually ARE finished and think that somehow getting people to use software that doesn't really work is going to lead to good things.  If this is the case, you're so disconnected from reality that you can never possibly succeed in creating an operating system people will want, and is frankly the scariest option of all of those here!

I have near 40 years experience in professional software engineering.  What you're doing is not how it should be done.  You need to think about this.

-Noel
