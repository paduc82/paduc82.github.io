kosh vorlon say:

Hi Leajune,

 

If you have a 3rd party security suite containing a firewall and want to install MSE, then you will of course need to uninstall and use the appropriate removal tool to get rid of that security suite (and, in fact, even if it doesn't contain a firewall, you still need to uninstall and use the appropriate removal tool to get rid of the AV program or whatever it is you were using).  Sometimes if previousing using a different Firewall program, uninstallation and removal results in Windows Firewall automatically enabling and sometimes not.  Simply check.  If not, simply enable it and you'll have a perfectly fine firewall and absolutely no need to purchase or even install a free alternative as I've found that while some have a few more bells and whistles, none do a better job than Windows Firewall and Windows Firewall is certainly going to have no conflict or other such issues with your operating system or with MSE.

 

If you forget to turn on Windows Firewall or if it's already enabled (because it was in use all along or because you did so as noted above), then MSE will enable it and turn it on if it isn't already and may make some minor changes to the configuration (whether it turns it on or it was already on) but they should not be noticeable.  Otherwise, they will begin working together and all will be well.  Again, it doesn't hurt to check to make certain.

 

If you do decide to go with MSE, post back and we'll give you a set of instructions that is far better and more detailed than you'll find anywhere else and help you get it all done right the first time and without problems.  It may seem simple and just like any other installation, and to a degree that's true, but there are some things that must be done and checked first that are not typical.  Plus, if you encounter problems, what we'll send will cover them and tell you what to do about them at each step of the way.  Even if you're sure you won't need them, what can it hurt to have them available and maybe even take a few moments to scan them to see if you find that maybe they actually can help?

 

With any firewall, it's only as good as it's configured.  Generally, they're set to block everything but the exceptions you specify.  Every exception represents a tiny hole in your overall security.  Some are needed and you allow the exceptions and that's fine.  But most defaults include exceptions that you will never use or use only in a blue moon.  To improve security, those exceptions should be unchecked (but make sure you know what you're unchecking before you do it so you don't end up blocking something required by something you use or something you don't realize you're using).  Of course, it's really a simple matter to fix by just rechecking the exceptions until you find the one you didn't mean to block, leave that one checked, and uncheck any you checked while testing and you'll be fine. 

 

This certainly applies to Windows Firewall.  I've personally never used or recommended anything else (though I've tested some to see how they worked and was either not impressed or saw nothing I considered worth using a 3rd party option which might cause issues when Windows Firewall did the trick just fine, so I'm not positive how they operate or what other features they include that may or may not need attention.  But seriously, you honestly won't really do better than Windows Firewall no matter what the others claim or how much they cost.  Also be aware that some 3rd party Firewall options WILL conflict with certain versions of Windows using certain types of software, so if you decide to go that route anyway, make sure you choose one that isn't going to either cause you problems or leave you less protected (meaning research it thoroughly first and I don't just mean their website which might be somewhat biased - LOL! - but google or bing it and read the reviews and blogs and feedback and other commentaries).

 

For basic information about firewall options, features, and choices, see: http://www.microsoft.com/security/pc-security/firewalls-choosing.aspx.

 

For highly detailed information about firewall options, features, and choices, see: http://technet.microsoft.com/en-us/network/bb545423.aspx

 

I hope this helps.

 

Good luck and best wishes.

christopher miller say:
There are valid security reasons to disallow 3rd party apps from changing other apps or their settings.  There has been plenty of incidents on the Android platform where malware was installed from the Google Play Store. You are far are less likely to have a hacker gain your control of your Windows Phone (or iOS, for mostly same reason) than on Android because of the strict limits placed on Windows Phone apps.

As for the tracking cookies, web sites that display ads will display ads whether you have the tracking cookies or not.  Google, Facebook, and other sites use the cookies to better match the ads with the consumer.  This is what pays for those services.

You don't need an additional apps to manage cookies or temporary files.  Out of the box, Internet Explorer (including on Windows Phone) sends a "Do No Track" (DNT) request to web sites that you visit. This prevents tracking cookies from being left on your phone from web sites that honor DNT. As @7thSense posted, it's easy to clear your history from IE on the phone.

You can also decide which cookies will be accepted by IE

In Internet Explorer, tap More  > Settings, and then tap Advanced settings. (You can also access Internet Explorer's settings through Settings  on the App list.)

Under Cookies from websites and apps, select Accept all, Block some, or Block all to set which cookies Internet Explorer will accept. Block some blocks third-party cookies that do not have a compact privacy policy and third-party cookies that save information that can be used to contact you without your explicit consent, and restricts first-party cookies that save information that can be used to contact you without your implicit consent.

Under Website file storage, select Allow sites to store files on my phone to allow websites to use your phone storage to store things like in-progress emails or RSS feeds. Tap Manage storage if you need to free up some storage space.

FWIW, the tracking cookies do not contain any personal information, just the sites that you have visited and just what you potential interests are.  If you were searching for hammers, you'll start seeing ads for hammers and other tools.  The ad hosts will know that you had expressed an interest in hammers, but they wont know who you are.

As for the NSA, they are not accessing your phone, they are accessing your phone records from your wireless carrier.  There is nothing on your phone that can be removed or disabled to prevent that.
