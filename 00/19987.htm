cbarnhorst say:

Your Windows 10 license is based on the unique IDs of the motherboard.  That is what is registered with Microsoft.  When you change the mobo, the unique IDs change of course and you have a different and unregistered device. 

If you replace a mobo with the manufacturer's recommended replacement part ("repair replacement"), you can transfer the old license to it.  If you replace a mobo with a retail upgrade part ("upgrade replacement"), you have to purchase a new license and register the new device.  For licensing purposes, the mobo is the computer. 

New mobo, new computer, new license required.

If you think you have made a repair replacement or have further questions, please use the activation wizard (type "activation wizard" in Cortana/Search) or use the Get Help icon on your Start Menu to contact a Microsoft Answer Desk agent for help.

cbarnhorst say:

The license is not part of the software in Windows 10 so it doesn't matter whether you have installed an Insider or non-Insider copy.  Insider previews do not have their own licenses (since July of 2015) and previews older than about six months will not start the device since they are all time-locked to an expiration date.  The expiration date applies only to the Windows 10 build.  It does not apply to the license, which is permanent for the life of the device (mobo). 

Device means the motherboard.  Your problem has nothing to do with what build or version of Windows 10 is currently installed.  The problem is that the unique hardware IDs of the new mobo are not yet registered so the device cannot activate any copy of Windows 10.  With Windows 10 the device activates Windows, not a product key.  The product key is only used for the one-time registration of the device as a "registered Windows 10 device" on Microsoft's servers.  After that, a product key is not needed to install Windows unless you are installing a different edition.
