dan at it associates say:

It is the latter, to ensure that the person selling the product does not continue to use it.

 

Unlike for example de-authorizing a computer with iTunes on it before uninstalling iTunes, therefore notifying Apple's servers that said computer is no longer allowed to access the iTunes store with a certain account, Microsoft's product activation logic does not use a "deactivation" methodology.

 

Instead, the logic used is that of supercession, in that the newest activations will (in a manner of speaking) overwrite, bump off, or supercede previous activations. That's where telephonic activation comes in, if necessary, a special step that denotes that the activation servers are being put on notice that "old" activations of a given product key are no longer current.

dan at it associates say:

Traditional retail packaged discs are retail (aka "FPP" full packaged product, as noted on the CoA) licenses.  These can be moved from computer to computer as necessary (para 19 of the retail software licensing terms [SLT]).  These can also be transfered from owner to owner, as you and your friend would like to do (para 20 of the SLT).

 

http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-software-license-terms-for-microsoft-office-2010-HA101817777.aspx 

 

The SLTs allow you to have the product concurrently activated on your "main" computer and your "portable" computer (para 2).

 

Since the license has been activated before, your friend should uninstall it from his computer before he sells it to you.  When you install and go to activate, there is a slight chance that you may run into the "activated too many times" error message.  If so, rerun activation and this time choose the option to do a telephonic activation, and follow the prompts.

kburgoyne say:

Your "Email Profile" has been corrupted.  I just finished working with Office support last month to track down and fix the problem, while at the same time getting educated about what was broken.

"Email Profiles" are now managed under Windows 10 (not sure about 8 or 7) because Windows also wants to use them.  An email profile is the collection of email account settings you've created.  Most people only have a single email profile which contains any multiple of email accounts settings they have. There is usually no need for most people to have any secondary email profiles.

You get to the email profiles by: Control Panel>Mail>Profiles. Getting to "Control Panel" is becoming increasingly tricky with each new Win10 version as Microsoft tries to replace Control Panel's more "administration" style user interfaces with more the more "user friendly" "settings" being offered by Win10.  I usually get to "Control Panel" these days by typing "Control Panel" into Cortana (aka the search option on the toolbar).


If you don't have a backup profile you can easily restore (most people don't), you'll have to try the various "repair" options people refer to. They appear to work for some people and not for a lot of people. If they don't work, you'll have to delete your profile and start creating a new one with all your email account information.  Often the Control Panel settings will still be able to show you the corrupted profile's settings, so make sure to write down all those settings before deleting the old profile and creating a new one.

Once you have Outlook back to running and appearing to be correct, go back to Control Panel and have it make copies of your working profile.  So typically your "in use" profile will be named "Outlook". I then copy that to profiles named (for example) "Outlook 20190714A" and "Outlook 20190714B".  The "20190714" being the date I made the copy.  If you later make changes to your email accounts (adding one, etc), you should go back to Control Panel and make new copies of your profile.

Note there is also a drop-down selection box which says which profile the system (and Outlook ) should default to.  Be sure to pay attention to that.  Make sure it remains set to your primarily profile (probably "Outlook") and it doesn't change to one of the backups you make.  When you do things like delete profiles, etc, Control Panel may change that setting trying to be "helpful".

In the future if the problem happens again, all you have to do is delete your in-use profile (probably "Outlook") then copy one of your latest backups to your normal in-use profile name. Try to stay with using the same "in-use" profile name since some things like the naming of local files containing copies of your emails can be influenced by what the profile name is.

Oh... and it's best to have Outlook (and any other email accessing apps) closed when you're doing this stuff.
