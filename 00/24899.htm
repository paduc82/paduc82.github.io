Word Count Word 2013 vs Document Properties - Microsoft Community

geridg say:

Word Count Word 2013 vs Document Properties

Hi...

There are a lot of subjects dealing with word count. Questions, and answers. The answers are wrong. It doesn't matter the system, it doesn't matter the Windows version.

I write large documents -- manuscripts -- which can have word counts (Word) in the 150,000 - 200, 000 range. The Word count in document properties is always staggeringly smaller -- 25,000 or 30,000 less. What then is going on? Simple. The staggering difference was the clue. Document Properties is not counting words you have entered in the dictionary. Characters names and other proper nouns, for example, foreign phrases, and other foreign words, the ooohs and aaahhs etc.

Word is counting all words. While it may not be perfect, it far closer to the actual word count providing you create a new document and copy and paste your completed document into the new file. Word 2013 continues to be notorious for counting "ghost words" with documents that have undergone heavy and frequent editing, as Word always has been troublesome with this -- I have been using Word for 23 years, since version 7.0, Windows 95, Pentium 1.  For documents that have undergone extensive/constant editing, you will also notice when you create you new file, it will be smaller in KB size because the dead ghost spaces (not just ghost words) will be gone.

Actually Skype supported Windows Mobile for years, at least as far back as 2005 or 2006, before finally discontinuing the app officially in late 2009, claiming that the experience was subpar compared to other platforms.  (And it was- due to the nearly infinite hardware variations in Windows mobile-based devices, Skype never got the earpiece audio working properly- caller's voices are heard through the device's system speaker/ringer, usually on the back of the device, rather than through the phone earpiece mobile phone calls are heard through.  Wired and wireless headsets work fine however, and using the phone without a headset just means you have to hold the phone "backwards", with the screen facing away from your face instead of towards it.)  Also, since the app was designed back in the days of less powerful devices, it's audio-only, the WinMo versions never supported video, either incoming or outgoing.

 

Though Skype no longer distributes the app, it is still kicking around the web, and still works as well as it ever did- I've been using it for years and still do to this day.  You can download it from c|net here:

http://download.cnet.com/Skype-for-Windows-Mobile/3000-2349_4-10319520.html    

 

On the bright side, since this is WinMo, Skype can run continually in the background, and receive calls when the app isn't active on screen, unlike with the Windows Phone 7 version of Skype.

Fring, a competing VoIP app that also supported Skype, ran Skype calls through their servers and used an ultra-high proprietary compression that worked quite well over slow cellular connections like EDGE (which "real" Skype didn't!)  Skype eventually threw a hissy fit and forced Fring to drop Skype support, though the app still worked with the (far less popular) Fring service itself.
