jay freedman say:
First, let me quote some excellent advice from the page at http://www.word.mvps.org/FAQs/Formatting/CreateIndex.htm:

"I implore you not to waste your time with a Concordance Index for most publications. It results in a huge pile of rubbish that is of very little use to the reader. And it takes nearly as long to make as it does to generate an index properly. The Concordance Index is a hangover from the past when people were desperately hoping to produce an “automatic index” to reduce the labor. Every major word-processor will do them, and no professional writer or editor would, these days, permit one."

In other words, don't do it. Take the time to mark just the most important occurrences of the most important words and concepts, using the built-in Mark Index Entry tool. In most cases, you should not mark every page in which the word appears, because that won't be useful to your readers. Mark the pages where a term is defined, or where a person is introduced or does something significant.

To make separate indexes for people or for places, you can insert "switches" into the XE fields that mark the occurrences, and then insert the same switches in the INDEX fields that create the indexes. A switch is a backslash followed by a letter and then an "identifier". For this purpose, the letter after the backslash must be f, as defined in this page. As you mark the entries, insert the \f switch followed by a letter in quote marks. I suggest using "p" for places, and "n" for names. So two of the fields might look like

   {XE "Paris" \f "p"}
   {XE "Sam" \f "n"}

At the end of the book, you would insert two INDEX fields, with the same switches as defined in this page:

   {INDEX \f "p"}
   {INDEX \f "n"}

Each INDEX field will include only the entries with the same value after the \f switch.
