cmdrkeene say:

This is not a feature.  The design goal was that your icons never move.  If your email icon is your 4th button, then it's always the 4th button.  No matter if it's not running, is running, minimized, or what.  It never moves.

Bonus: it also gets an automatic keyboard shortcut.  You can type Win+4 to press the 4th taskbar button. 

Double bonus: it also works like a web browser.  Add "shift" to open it in a new window.  So if you wanted to open another copy of Chrome for example, you could press Win+Shift+3 (or more realistically, just hold shift while you click it, or middle-click it).

It's obviously impossible to please everyone, but this was done with customer feedback as the reason.  Customers like confidence when their icons stay where they want them. 

In the old days (like Windows Vista), one top complaint was exactly this.  To reach your mail, you had to find Outlook. Which was in your Start menu under programs/Office when you're just getting to work.  Unless Outlook was running, then it would be on your taskbar as a running program.  Unless if it was minimized, then it would jump to over by the clock "system tray" for some reason.  Unless you had that hide your inactive icons, then it would be under the > symbol.  Depending on the time of day or if you minimized it, there were 6 or 7 places you might to have to check to figure out where your email icon might be at that time. 

It was all a ton of complaints, and so in Windows 7 and onward since, the icons stay in place and never move.

--

Now if you REALLY want separate icons: one that opens your email and another one that shows up at the right when it's already running, you can do it.  Just right-click a blank area of your taskbar and turn on a toolbar for a folder of shortcuts, and then you can put that folder to the left for starting things, while running things show up on the right.

Some reasons for computer slowness:

1. Computer hasn't been maintained - http://www.elephantboycomputers.com/page2.html#Maintenance

2. Computer is infected with malware - http://www.elephantboycomputers.com/page2.html#Removing_Malware

3. Hard drive is too full. Remove unnecessary stuff, uninstall unused programs. Don't forget to back up!
http://www.elephantboycomputers.com/page2.html#Backing_Up

4. Flaky third-party software. Toolbars and add-ons are particular culprits in this area. Uninstall toolbars (Google, Yahoo, ISP-branded, etc.) and look carefully at what add-ons are in use in your browser(s).

5. Computer has too many unnecessary programs/processes running in the background. Manage your Startup:

The free Autoruns program is very useful for managing your Startup - http://www.microsoft.com/technet/sysinternals/default.mspx

6. User is running a bloated/invasive antivirus program such as ones from Norton and McAfee. Replace with a better program. I recommend either NOD32 (commercial) or Avast (free). Avira is also good but the free version has an unpleasant nag screen (Google for instructions as to how to disable this). User may also be running more than one real-time antivirus/firewall/security program.

7. User has installed new programs that are processor and/or memory-intensive (Photoshop, AutoCAD, Mathmatica or the like) and doesn't have the necessary hardware power.

8. Hard drive is failing and is in PIO Mode. This is very dramatically slow (like being back in 1985). See Hans-Georg Michna's information here:
http://winhlp.com/?q=node/10

In addition to the above, also see http://miekiemoes.blogspot.com/2008/02/help-my-computer-is-slow.html
