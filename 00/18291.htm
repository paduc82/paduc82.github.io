jay freedman say:

If some or all of your text was selected at the time you inserted the page break, then the break replaced the selected text -- just like typing words would replace anything that was already selected.

Unfortunately, exiting and reentering was exactly the wrong thing to do, because it made the replacement permanent. The proper response -- any time something unexpected and unwanted happens in Word -- is to click the Undo button. Once you close Word, though, the memory of what to undo is discarded.

There are two possibilities to recover:

  • Your text might be hidden inside a field instead of replaced. Open the document and press Alt+F9. If the text appears, and has curly brackets { } before and after it, you can restore the text. Select everything except the brackets, cut it to the clipboard, and paste it outside the brackets. Then select and delete the brackets.
  • If you had Windows 10's File History turned on, there could be a copy of the document from before the page break was inserted.

rohn007 say:
First question, why do you want to put your recipes into the computer?

How will digitizing them make your life easier/better? The one thing almost any computer program will do is allow you to search for things, maybe better. Are you going to have a computer, a tablet in the kitchen when you are cooking? Or are you going to print out the recipies every time?

Are you going to "publish" a cookbook, even if it is just sharing the collection with your family?

Do you want to use the computer to help you generate menus, or automate calculating mass producing recipes?

So far the consensus has been OneNote. That's fine. Personally, I'm more comfortable with word, and that is where I am collecting new recipes.

Do you plan to retype all of the recipes or do you want to scan them in to the computer? If you scan them, do you want to convert them into editable text?

So, potentially you are going to need some sort of a scanner. Many consumer printers now are "multifunction" devices, that include scanners. Or you can buy a dedicated "flatbed" scanner or even small hand held scanners.

If you are ok with using images, that is all. Otherwise you are going to need a program that does "OCR", Optical Character Recognition, to convert the scanned images into editable text. Depending on the quality of the original (cooking smears?), the scanner and the OCR program you will have to do some (a lot or little) correction of scanning errors.

Note: Only OneNote in Office includes a true OCR tool. It is simple but adequate for most uses. It only captures text, not images embeded with the text. But it is reasonably accurate.

When my dad died a few years ago, when we were going through his stuff we found thousands of recipe cards and clippings. Stuffed in envelopes, file folders, card organizer boxes etc. No he wasn't the cook in the family, no he didn't share them with my Mom, they were never used. They were just something he collected

Given that story, there is one use I can think of for a computer in this question. That is to provide you with an index or table of contents to make finding paper copies easier. Organize all of your recipes as best you can. Put the 2 sided cards into appropriate card holder boxes, put the clippings into binders, not scrapbooks (so you can insert new pages as needed). Then create a single "database" / index / TOC that allows you to easily locate specific recipes. You can copy and create alternate organizations or sort orders as needed. For this sort of use I would go with Excel to create an informal user friendly and user customizable database. Just keep adding new columns for new categories as you need them:
- meal (breakfast / brunch / lunch / tea / dinner / desert / apres dinner etc)
- food group
- large / small event
- dietary restrictions
- last used date
- last used occasion
- personal favorite of "person x"
- reviews / opinions (general favorite, or eww it sucks, never use again! etc)
- etc

Then you can sort and/or filter on the various columns to make it easier to identify the recipe you want to select and where in your paper, and potentially online, collection(s) to find the detailed recipe.

If you wanted to get a little fancier, I could even see creating a simple pivot table and / or dashboard to make a prettier presentation than a simple spreadsheet grid.

rohn007 say:
In addition, if you do decide to go with a 3rd party OCR tool one of the features to pay attention to is how the text is treated when it is extracted.

Some tools, explicitly control the position of each line of text using frames or text boxes for each line. While this exactly recreates the original layout, it makes editing the text virtually impossible. Or at least very tedious. You want a tool that has the option to handle text in logical paragraphs, even when the result does not look exactly the same as the original.

Stingray-j,

Because of the way that iOS has designed its operating system and the restrictions that all sandboxed (App Store) apps run under, it is not possible for any app to make the touch screen unresponsive. (It is, of course, possible for apps to hang, but they cannot affect anything else on the device.)

Based on your description, it sounds like you created a PIN/password for your iPad and by default if you enter the wrong device PIN/password five times, then the device will be completely reset (that is a security/privacy/anti-theft feature).
