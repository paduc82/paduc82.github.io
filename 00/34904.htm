cmdrkeene say:

A system image makes a total exact copy of the hard drive.  So yes your bookmarks, history in firefox, etc, will all be in the system image.  But a system image cannot be browsed to get out individual things.  Example, later you won't be able to take the system image and just go in and get a single file or bookmark out of it:  It must be applied, in whole, to a new hard drive.

If you're signed into it, all your firefox bookmarks and settings might already be synchronized in the firefox server, ready to go to any other computer you login to. That's the best way to live, since you don't need to worry about anything if you dropped your computer and broke it, or it suddenly died.

Creating a system image means your hard drive will be in use for an extensive amount of time as it reads and writes every singe file across every part of the drive.  If the drive is already close to failure, this could put it over the edge. Or it may have days, weeks, or months of use left.  Most hard disk drives fail after 4 years (you can Google it). But that's just like anything else, many die younger and others older. You never really know.

My honest advise would be to start backing up things to online services (OneDrive, Google Drive, Drop Box) or another drive like a USB thumb drive stick. Start with what's most important to you considering the drive could die at any moment (and consider yourself lucky, most people don't bother until the drive is fully dead and find out it would require $10,000 or more to pay for recovery services. You have to have backups first before problems happen. Like one popular backup company says, "you have to back it up to get it back."

PS. I find it incredibly odd that the HP backup program is called "Rebecca.exe", but who knows, maybe they really did that. It just sounds suspicious to me, especially considering it's not a secure program and it is prompting that warning. But you don't need HP's program to make a system image backup, and besides I think you'd be better off just directly copying files you care about (pictures, documents, videos, etc) rather than worrying about a system image anyway.

sumit (volunteer moderator) say:
This is one of the known issues with the update as Recovery partition should be hidden and is accidentally assigned a drive letter.
Right-click Start>Command Prompt(admin)
Type in the following:
diskpart
list volume
select volume 3
Replace 3 with the number of the volume which is assigned a drive letter. (In hundreds of MBs)
remove letter="F"

Don't worry, removing a drive letter would not cause any harm to your device.

davem121 say:
Hi Mary, this is a known issue in the 1803 Update,

It is the recovery drive, you are not meant to see it, it is not meant to have a drive letter, remove the drive letter and everything will return to normal:

Click your Start Button, type cmd, then right click Command Prompt and choose 'Run as Administrator'

Run this command and hit Enter

diskpart

In the new command window, run this command and hit Enter

select volume F

Run this command and hit Enter

remove letter=F

Close Command Prompt and restart your PC
