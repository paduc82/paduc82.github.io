antoine_leca say:

But now I do understand why Outlook actually thought the extension was ".pdf" instead of simply "pdf".

It is a sidepoint but in fact, Outlook (and any other program) still consider the extension to be "pdf"; the rules to recognize the extension are always: search for the last dot (here, the second of the .. sequence), then consider all the characters after that last dot; the second to last dot is never considered in such a process, so is not part of the extension. If Outlook recognize something special, it is either the name ending in . once the ".pdf" ending sequence is removed (likely), or perhaps the strangeness of having such .. sequence in a name.

I also do not know exactly what was having in mind the programmer at Microsoft which crafted the change, or the security researchers which found the original issue that update was trying to fix.
But if there is a real issue (as opposed to a mere bug) with those ..pdf files, they need to have a pretty convincing explanation... since it is all too easy to build a attachment name as <name of client> + ".pdf"; and since to avoid that case on the user side, the programmers need to re-program an unbounded number of programs (including in the U.S.A., where firm names ending with ", Inc." or ", Ltd." are not uncommon) to swallow the last dot in <name of client> before buidling the attachment name: this is not likely to happen!

Windows Defenderâs real-time protection (on-access) has you covered for any active malware that Windows Defender is able to detect (has definitions for), and Automatic Maintenance is already running a daily Quick Scan which will find most latent items. Since Defenderâs real-world protection scores are still subpar, you should probably be more concerned with periodically running third-party on-demand scanners in place of the Windows Defender Full Scan. Just a few factors that play a part in disk related timings:

#1 - Speed of your hard drive (5400rpm vs 7200rpm) and on-board cache - if copying locally (or to put this another way - quality of the hard drive involved)

#2 - Available temp work space (depending on how a program does things)

#3 - Type of drive that is the target of where the files are being copied (you may have mentioned it but didn't notice)

#4 - Other processes going on at the time and priority given to/taken by the program (may not have anything to with Outlook or any other Microsoft program)

There are a variety of approaches you can follow to limit your exposure and be operational in less than an hour in most cases (hardware failures excluded) without similarly interfering with day-to-day operations.
