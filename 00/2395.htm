This is the search:

[\^13 ][A-Z][!.]@YOUR EXACT PHRASE HERE[!.]@[.\^13]

It will look for:
  1. [either of these] (the sequence between brackets means "either") a Carriage Return (written "\^13") or a space (it appears right after the "13")
  2. followed by an upper case letter (either A, B, ... or Z, written "A-Z" to denote a range)
  3. followed by NOT (!) a period (.), appearing "one or more times" (indicated by "@"); this will match any sequence of characters before the phrase
  4. followed by the phrase (exactly written, as wildcard searches require matching upper/lower case)
  5. followed by anything that is NOT a period, one or more times; this will match any sequence of characters until the end of the sentence, denoted byâ€¦
  6. either a period or a Carriage Return
Thus, that sequence will find these cases (where "Â¶" is the preceding Return):
  1. Â¶Start of sentence PHRASE continuation of sentence. â€“ This would be the first sentence in a paragraph
  2. Â¶Start of sentence PHRASE continuation of sentenceÂ¶ â€“ This would be a title with no trailing period
  3.  Start of sentence PHRASE continuation of sentence. â€“ Because of the leading space, this would be any sentence in a paragraph, except the first
  4.  Start of sentence PHRASE continuation of sentenceÂ¶ â€“ Again, a leading space but no trailing period (could be an error, but it's found anyway)
Because of the structure assumed by the search (namely that there should be text before and text after PHRASE), there are instances that it will not find (like "PHRASE continuation of sentence" or "Start of sentence PHRASE"), but you can find them if you force the search to comply with the assumed structure (use "HRASE continuation of sentence" or "Start of sentence PHRAS").
