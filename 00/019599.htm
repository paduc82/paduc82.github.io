Lumia 1520 - Project My Screen Works - Microsoft Community

chucke2 say:

Lumia 1520 - Project My Screen Works

I've been able to successfully use the Project My Screen feature (halfway down the list on the Settings menu) of my Lumia 1520, with Windows Phone 8.1, to mirror phone contents (e.g., Photos) and stream videos (e.g., Xbox Video, Hulu Plus, Netflix) to my TV using the Netgear Push2TV (PTV3000) plugged into an HDMI port on the TV.  Important: To get it to work, I first had to update the firmware of the PTV3000.  Out of the box, the PTV3000 firmware version was 1.0.13, and Project My Screen on my 1520 searched but couldn't find the PTV3000.  However, after updating the firmware of the PTV3000 to version 2.4.46, the 1520 found the PTV3000 and allowed me to send phone content to the TV to which the PTV3000 is connected.  (P.S.  Unlike Google Chromecast, the PTV3000 sends out its own WiFi signal that the 1520 has to detect.  Chromecast requires the Google dongle and your laptop/Android phone share the same WiFi network.)

 

Updating the firmware of the PTV3000:

 

1.  Before doing anything else, use your laptop to go to the Netgear support site and download the latest firmware for the PTV3000.  (I saved the file to my laptop's desktop.)

2.  Connect the PTV3000 to an HDMI port on your television, using your own HDMI cable (it's not supplied by Netgear).  You'll also have to attach the PTV3000 to a USB port for power, or use the adapter Netgear supplies to plug it into a power outlet.

3.  Turn on the TV and change the input to the HDMI port where you attached the PTV3000.

4.  Press and hold the "Mode" button on the side of the PTV3000 so that it's now in a mode that allows your laptop to connect to it to update its software.

5.  Use your laptop's wifi to connect to the PTV3000's wifi signal.  (I.E., in Windows 8.1, swipe to open the Charms menu --> tap Settings --> tap the Wifi icon --> connect to the "Push2TV" signal.)

5.  Your TV screen will display some instructions, including the IP address of the PTV3000.  Open a web browser on your laptop and type in the IP address of the PTV3000 and press Enter.

6.  This takes you to a screen where you should click the Browse button to find the firmware update file you saved on your desktop.  Then click Upload.  This uploads the new firmware to the PTV3000.

7.  The PTV3000 will reboot, and now can be found by your Nokia Lumia 1520.

 

Connecting the 1520 to the PTV3000:

1.  Make sure your TV input is set to the HDMI port where the PTV3000 is attached.

2.  On your 1520, tap your Settings tile, then tap Project My Screen.

3.  "Push2TV...." (the adapter name)-PTV3000 should be listed.  Tap this to connect.

4.  Connection should occur within about 5 seconds.

 

Note:  When your phone is "vertical," the display on your TV is a narrow vertical display showing what's your phone.  However, rotate your phone to "horizontal" and the display will fill the screen.

 

Overall Impressions:

I found Project My Screen to be a super-easy way to project my photos and other content to my much larger TV screen.  (If the Photos tile had a built in slide show, I'd be even happier.  Does anyone know if it has one?)  I was happily surprised (okay, shocked) that I was able to stream video content from Hulu Plus, Netflix, Xbox Video, etc., to my TV screen (including the audio).  I've paid for these services, but we've all seen how sometimes some arcane DRM restrictions prevent us from watching stuff we've already paid for.  (P.S.  The streaming was occasionally choppy when I had the PTV3000 behind the TV, but it smoothed out when I moved it so there was a clear line of sight between the 1520 and the PTV3000.)  Also, FYI, for me at least, the connection between phone and Push2TV seems to drop out if it's unused for a couple of minutes.  And, to be honest, I haven't watched any long videos yet, so I don't know how stable the connection is.  Plus, I'm unclear whether I'm using my home WiFi or cellular data plan when my phone is sending video to the PTV3000.

 

In a Nutshell:

My Droid-envy is now over.

 

(By the way, Project My Screen also allows you to connect your phone to your laptop via USB, so that phone contents are displayed on the laptop screen.  You first have to download the Project My Screen App from Microsoft and install it onto your desktop (there isn't a Metro/Modern/Whatever-they're-calling-it app).  When you connect the phone and laptop, on the Project My Screen setting on your phone you'll get a message asking whether you want to connect to the laptop.  Click OK.  An outline of your phone will appear on your laptop screen, mirroring the contents of your phone.  Unfortunately, when you turn the phone horizontal, the outline doesn't go away -- so photos from your phone will always appear within the confines of the outline; i.e., they'll never go full-screen.  The main use of the USB version of Project My Screen may be for situations where you want to quickly copy/crop an image from your phone to use it in a document you're composing on your laptop.)
