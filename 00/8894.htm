Set your Junk email filtering level to Exclusive.

If you set your filter to this, only messages from your contacts and safe sender’s lists will be allowed to go to your Inbox, the rest will go to your Junk Folder. Here are the steps to setup your junk e-mail filter in Hotmail:

1. Sign in to https://mail.live.com using your Microsoft account.
2. In the upper-right corner of your Inbox, click the Cog icon, and then More mail settings.
3. Under Preventing junk email, click Filters and reporting.
4. Under Filters and reporting, select the options that you want, and then click Save.

 

Notes: 

  • You should occasionally check your junk email folder to make sure that good messages are not sent there by mistake.
  • Your Junk folder is regularly cleaned up. So you need not to delete those emails manually.

Create an email rule that will filter junk emails.

You can create an email rule that will either automatically delete the junk message or move to another folder. Here are the steps:

1. Sign in to https://mail.live.com using your Microsoft account.
2. In the upper-right corner of your Inbox, click the Cog icon, and then More mail settings.
3. Under Customizing Hotmail, click Rules for sorting new messages.
4. Click New.
5. Under Step 1, select the email attribute from the drop-down list and type the email address that you want to apply the rule to.
6. Under Step 2, determine where you want the messages to be moved or what you want to do to that email (e.g. if it would be auto Deleted):

1.     To move the messages to an existing folder, choose Move to Inbox, and then click the folder name

2.     To move the messages to a new folder, choose Move to a new folder and type the name for the folder you want to create.

3.     To delete the messages, choose Delete these messages.

4.     To forward messages, select forward messages and type the email address where you wish to forward the said emails.

5.     Click Save.

Note: In an instance where the spammer just changes the domain (sending email address), it would be better if you would create an email rule that will specify the subject instead of the sender's email address. (e.g. If the email contains the word "Viagra", delete the email).

Password Policies for Live Accounts and for life in general

Why do we have such a complex password policy? Let's think about it.

  1. Password Complexity is created because of the traditional "dictionary" attacks. You want your password to be sufficiently complex such that it does not normally appear in an attacker's dictionary.
    • But let's think about that for a minute. We have existing mechanisms to prevent dictionary attacks! Account lockouts, retry blockouts, captchas and even dynamic IP address filtering to stop dictionary attacks.
    • Even with "complex" passwords, it is human nature to simply replaces the "a"s with "@"s, and "I"'s with 1 or ! in what is otherwise a perfectly legitimate alphabet-based word... eg: password turns into p@$$w0rd JUST to fit in with the policy.... seriously this is NOT really hard to guess at all, and we all do it!... and all dictionary attackers knows this anyway, therefore many of these permutations are already built into the attack script, defeating the purpose of "complex" passwords in the first place. Which reverts to the above point to protect us!
    • Forcing people to have a complex password is a bit like saying "we the service provider is too lazy to implement safeguards against dictionary attacks, so we'd rather make your life more difficult to make ours easier."... My question to people who think like that is: Why do you have a JOB? And HOW do you even justify a salary and sleep at night?

  2. Password Expiry policies - Why do we have this? I suspect for the same reason why Credit Cards have an expiry date on them. But again, what does it really achieve in an online security world?
    • Firstly, lets assume that it is not the service provider's responsibility to insure against the user's stupidity. Let's assume for a minute that users are actually not the mindless idiots we assume them to be, and that they, can ACTUALLY understand the fact that they should NOT be handing out their passwords to everyone who asks for it.
    • A password is meant to be easy to remember. A user is meant to remember, instinctively, what the password for a particular service is. They should not have to maintain a massive list of cryptic passwords just because different providers have different expiry periods, or simply have different expiry dates due to the fact they were signed up at different times. More often than not, users simply append a digit to the end of their usual password to suit such a policy. Therefore p@$$w0rd becomes p@$$w0rd1 .. and p@$$w0rd2... and so on so forth. Now when you have a number of services with such a theme of password, and given 3 attempts to "guess" what number you're up to, the legitimate user is essentially dictionary-attacking their own password!
    • It makes FAR more sense to allow the user to nominate their own password, and stick to it! Even if it is months, or even years old! Let's face it, we all have online services everywhere. We don't always have to manually login because we tick the "Remember Me" box. But once in a while, when we travel or get issued with a new computer, all hell breaks loose. A password is a secret that the user remembers, and it should not be an arbitrarily forced mutation of that secret. It's a bit like asking your first pet's name, except it isn't... now lets see, it is probably somewhere between Fluffy12 and Fluffy37... but which is it? Oops I've only got 5 guesses, I've locked out my own account

  3. [X] Number of Previously Used Passwords prevention - Why are we stopping people from using their previously used passwords? Actually, why would people WANT to use their previous passwords in the first place?
    • People go back to the password they're familiar with because it's what they WANT to use! It's what they remember!! A password is to identify a person! If that's how they want to be identified, what gives ANYONE else the right to tell them otherwise? If they want their password to be "password" or "abc123", SO BE IT! Let them! I've said this earlier - It is NOT the service provider's responsibility to insure users against their own stupidity! They know what the risks are, they know if they use easily guessable words like their first name it will make their account less secure, and they have made a conscious decision to USE that word. Who knows, in some kind of twisted logic, that actually makes sense! "Nah, nobody's dumb enough to put their own username as their password".... Could happen! It's not up to admins to impose their own mindset onto users!
    • In today's era of multiple mobile devices, it is very likely that a user's password to a certain service is stored on multiple places. By not allowing users to use the same password they've previously used, you are effectively forcing the user to change their passwords on all those devices. And yes some people will be compelled to respond with "that's EXACTLY what we want to do!". But again, each of those devices have their own password safeguard mechanism. The device themselves are security/biometrically locked! Plus, no self-respecting app developer will place no effort into securely storing that password, particularly when that password is used with a large cloud service such as Google or Live, the service providers themselves impose a level of security such as OAuth such that the passwords aren't actually stored on the device itself. In other words, admins are forcing people to change their passwords based on an unfounded, perceived breach of security which didn't exist in the first place! All you're achieving is pissing the users off and annoying them for no arguably logical reason! which is it? Oops I've only got 5 guesses, I've locked out my own account!
    • Using the same previous password despite resetting or "Forgotten" it is perfectly legitimate also! If a user has a number of commonly used passwords and they've exhausted their guesses (based on typos or incorrectly assuming the appended number to a password) and locked themselves out. Chances are, once given access back to their account, they will revert to one of those commonly used passwords and there's a chance that it was the same one just prior to their password reset. If it makes sense to the user, why are we stopping them from it? Why be evil when there is no need for it?

  4. Predefined "Secret" Questions - This sounds like a good idea in theory. But when a "secret question" is pre-defined, even if there's a few to choose from, don't be surprised that it doesn't always yield the same answer from the same legitimate user! And definitely not when you impose a minimum character limit to some of these questions
    • For example. My mother's maiden name is 3 letters long. This is often rejected as a secret answer because most systems needs it to be at least 5 or 6 characters long. Why ask me a question when you're going to reject the answer?? So that you know, in some cultures it is perfectly normal to have single syllable, or even single lettered names. It could even be an unpronounceable symbol in the case of the artist formerly known as Prince! If you're going to ask the question, accept weird answers!
    • Questions like the name of my first pet - Well I had a pet at home. It wasn't *MY* first pet, it was my brother's pet, although he never played with it, and I spent more time with it than he ever did. I didn't have my own pet until a few years later but it died after 2 days and I didn't think much of it. But for all intensive purposes, I remembered my brother's pet vividly as my first pet so I've put that as my secret answer. Ask me the same secret question a few years later for password recover? - heck, did I put my brother's pet or my own pet? or did I put the first pet I owned with my girlfriend as my first pet?? See how easy it is to blur your own secret answer when the question itself is vague? However if I was allowed to define my own secret question: "The name of MY first pet which didn't come in a fishbowl" ..... with enough details for my future self to know what I've previously answered - that could work.
    • Or a list of questions that may not apply to a user at all - My mother's kept her maiden name, everybody knows it, so that's no secret anymore, I've never had a pet, all my friends went to the same primary school as I did, and they were all there when I got my first car, I've publicly talked about my first job and my boss on social media and my first girlfriend made our breakup very public on a blog so even people I've never heard of knows her name and mine, there goes the entire list of predefined secret questions, what else can I do? Hint: This would be a good time to let the users define a question that makes sense to them and themselves alone!

 

So I urge developers, admins and so-called security experts of the world, think for a moment not about what you concoct in your mind to be some kind of secure design to ease your own paranoid minds, but deal with the issue of security with a balance of probability and real-life practicality in the systems you create.

And have mechanisms to safeguard a user's security without compromising on usability, such as alternate email addresses and password recovery/reset mechanisms, user-defined secret questions, and brute-force attack preventions. And above all, let the users choose the level of security they desire! Some might indeed want convoluted password policies to insure against their own stupidity. But for everyone else, at least give them the CHOICE of opting out of such convoluted systems.
