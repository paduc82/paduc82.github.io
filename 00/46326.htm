VGA will go up to 1920X1080 and beyond. 1920X1080 is HD/1080p when used on up to  24" monitor.
DVI is a bit sharper than VGA with text, but not dramatically enough to notice without
comparing two systems side by side.

HDMI is only really necessary, or desirable, when using the audio function - imo.

True HD monitor ? Depends on the size. A 27" 1440p monitor requires a lot more
graphics power than a 24" 1920X1080 monitor. 1440p also requires a Dual Link DVI vs DVI.
- If  your use of bold text is anything to go by, I'd  guess that, like me, you would find it hard to
read the text  on a monitor with 1440p resolution . I even set Windows to use 125% text size on my 24" monitor .
-Using 1920X1080 on a 27" monitor would result is something lower than HD resolution .
Just guessing but maybe 720p.

There are a number of 'stock' integrated graphics. The Intel 3000 and 4000 are reasonably
powerful. I don't know what your MSI mobo's integrated graphics is, but it depends on what you want to do with the machine. For general Windows, web browsing, watching video, is all you do then the integrated graphics should  suffice.
If you want to play games then the particulars of the integrated graphics would need to be known.
Many on board integrated graphics aren't as good with games as the more recent cpu based
integrated graphics.

The only problem with adding a dedicated graphics card is whether the power supply can
handle the extra load . The higher the level of graphics card the more power required.
.

bossdweeb say: Just to clarify the resolution a bit more - when speaking about a 27" screen vs a 24" screen @

This is where you drift away from 'true' HD, but it is not necessarily a problem if you sit back from
the screen (which you would want to do when gaming on a screen that large anyway) , because the pixels are less noticeable with distance than when close up.

Building your own is the best way to go for a gaming machine, and get value for money.
I don't see any gaming  benchmarks for your ATI Radeon-3000 Integrated Graphics , but with a

Intel-HD-Graphics-3000.

I would advise starting at a site such as Newegg to  see what's available in your budget and read
some reviews for those components. It's not necessary to read a review for every brand of
motherboard or graphics card, more for a series or model, eg - Z77 motherboard or GTX660 Nvidia
graphics vs ATI/AMD 7870  graphics, and Intel Ivy Bridge cpu versus AMD's Zambezi .
(Intel also make a Sandybridge cpu but while it will be more 'future-proof' due to the 2011 motherboard
socket it is not as good value for money currently)
Most of the big review sites have gaming benchmarks that compare a number of different models
of graphics card or cpu.

Upgrading the existing machine can be done, but a new machine would be better, long term.
It depends on your budget, what games you want to play and how important 'eye candy' is to you.
When running AAA titles at 'Ultra'  settings you get all the special effects, but the dollar vs extra graphics quality gap grows bigger with higher settings, so much so that you often need side by side screen
shots to pick the difference in detail or lighting effects, etc. between High and Ultra settings.
A good place to see some of these  comparisons is at Tweak Guides.
TweakGuides.com

- Your motherboard is still a good  gaming motherboard, and if your HP 'tower' has good air
flow (at least three 120mm case fans) it would be OK, depending on the length/depth of the case
from the back of the HDD bays to the back of the case. Some gaming graphics cards are too long
for standard cases.
- A 2.8GHz cpu is not all that bad but  a Phenom II 965 (available from Newegg for $99-),
 or a Phenom II XT1055 (scarce as hens teeth) would be better as they have clock speeds of
3.4 and 3.5GHz respectively.
There's more to it than GHz though, and AMD has dropped the ball in respect to gaming since the two processors above (even the 965 trailed a bit behind Intel when released). The latest has  shown an improvement over the 'Bulldozer' series, but still cannot match Intel. It's not a huge deal, but Intel still is the better cpu for gaming.
I say this as a long time AMD user who has recently made the move to Intel. My 'old' machine with
a PhenomII 965 and Nvidia GTX 560ti will still play most games at max settings, but as time moves on
the newer big titles will leave it further behind (won't bother the wife though ).
- 8GB RAM is more than enough. I have yet to top 46% use of 8GB with the most system intensive
games I have - Crysis 2 and BF3. That includes the approx 1.4GB used by Win7 setup at idle.
There are some uses for 16 or more GB RAM, mostly professional, but not gaming or watching video
(Professional level video editing, yes).
-  Power Supplies on OEM machines are almost always going to be too limited, particularly when
the machine was sold without a dedicated graphics card.
Power Supplies is another topic, but sufficice it to say that too many gamers get cheap and nasty
psu's. Cheap and nasty often don't have the actual watts advertised, or distribute too much power
to the +3 and +5V rails. They use cheap capacitors and coils which don't last and don't produce
smooth power delivery.
You may get away with this on a business or internet machine, but when running system stressing games this can lead to crashes, BSOD and 'random' reboots at best, a burnt out psu and possible destruction of other major components at worst.
You also have those who get too much power. A good quality brand name power supply with maybe 50-100W more than the minimum recommended by Nivida or ATI for a given card is more than enough.  (add 100-200W more if going for dual graphics cards, depending on the card ) The reason for this is that a power supply runs more efficiently (drawing less power and creating less heat) at between approx 50-85% of it's total capacity.
- SSD - Fun. Windows boots very quick and may run a bit snappier. Other software may load quicker
as well. Games will load quicker too, but there is no performance increase vs a 7200rpm HDD.
I keep all my games on a 7200rpm HDD (have to as I have well over 100 installed ;D ).
imo - Until the SSD becomes  the  storage device a 120-128GB SSD is enough when coupled with
a 1-2TB 7200rpm HDD (Photos, Music, Videos and all that space using stuff can be kept off the SSD).

 - Graphics Card - What you've been wanting to know about, yeah ?
With what you have now an Nvidia GTX 650 or GTX650ti is compact and has reasonably good power
(read the reviews to see the difference between the two),
Gigabyte GTX 650ti
and a 450-500W power supply and you will do pretty well with most games.  Power supply brands recommended most often - Corsair, Antec, Seasonic , PC Power and Cooling. Even with brand names it is sometimes best to avoid the bottom end of the range ( eg - Coolermaster and Thermaltake).

Corsair 430W 
Antec High Current gamer 520W psu

- Case. My favorite  (if you like flash you won't be interested) - The Coolermaster CM 690 Advanced.
A big draw for me is the external e-SATA bay (I have about five old HDD's that I
use for backup). Aside from that it is just a good value for money case. An important feature
is the top fan mounts. Modern graphics cards with aftermarket coolers exhaust the heat back into
the case, so two exhaust fans is a good thing to have. Aftermarket coolers are the best. They are
quieter and more efficient than the 'reference' cooler with a single turbine fan.
The case also has removable HDD bays so you can improve air flow by taking a section out.

green screen on new windows 8.1 toshiba laptop say:

I Just bought a Toshiba laptop with windows 8.1 (8-23-2014). when I went to anything that had flash drive videos or any video playback, I got the audio but also got the dreaded green screen. After many online searches for solutions (none that worked) I finally found one that worked and was very simple for me to follow and implement (i'm not stupid, but my computer acumen is well below professional programmer) so I was looking for an easy to follow solution, and I found it. keep in mind that the "AMD Radeon 4 Graphics" card that I show on my steps is specific for my computer, but I bet your graphics card will work similarly...

 

1 - go to "control panel"
2 - find "hardware and sound"
3 - find "devices and printers"
4 - find "device manager"
5 - find "display adaptors" (or something similar)

6 - click on "display adaptors"

6.1 - the name of your graphics card will come up. mine was "amd radeon 4

        graphics" (amd radeon 4  was specific for my computer, i'm sure yours

        will have a different graphics driver.)

7 - right click on "amd radeon 4 graphics", a drop down box will open with choices
8 - click "update driver software"
9 - pick either "search automatically" or "search manually"
9.1 - I picked "searched automatically" (it took several minutes)
10 - install the driver that comes up.

11 - restart windows 8.1

flash videos ran, and the green screen was gone.
