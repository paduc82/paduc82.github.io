rob koch say:

Even if there was a list it would be impossible to do what you are thinking, since there is no standard for naming of malware and so even the same malware often has different names within the documentation of different antimalware providers.

 

The other even more complex issue is that the technical methoids used by each vendor to detect and remove malware are different as well.  This results in situations where an entire family of malware may be detected by the same definition in some cases or only a sub-family or specific malware in others.  So there's no way to "match" the difinitions of one product with another in a meaningful way.

 

The last issue is specific to MSE, but probably has parallels with other products today as well.  In the case of MSE, it has the ability to "phone home" whenever something that may be malware is detected, but none of the current definitions in the local

 

If MAPS recognizes the malware, it provides a pointer to the definitions database for the PC to download the specific definition to resolve the known infection.  If it's unknown, the malware information is saved by MAPS along with those of other PCs and made available to the Microsoft Anti-malware Team ana,ysts to review and potentially add to future definitions if it's really malware.

 

From the above, it's easy to see that the local MSE definitions are really simply a cache of a much larger set of definitions accessible via MAPS, so the true size of the definitions database is huge, much larger than any individual PC could contain or operate effectively.  So the idea of fully analyzing any "list" of detections at the database level is not really possible.

 

I think you're looking in the wrong direction and should be comparing the AV and RootKit removal products at a fundamental differences level and not looking for a "list" of different current malware that each protects from.  In other words, what does each of them actually do that's different from the other?

 

Rob

If you are experiencing some issues with Internet Explorer and you want to turn it off you can proceed with the following steps:

  1. Launch Control Panel
  2. Navigate to Programs & Features
  3. In the left navigation pane, click Turn Windows features on or off link.
  4. If prompted for User Account Control, input your admin password to proceed.
  5. Scroll down the alphabetical list of features to Internet Explorer 11.
  6. Uncheck its check box to turn Internet Explorer 11 off. An information dialog displays to inform you of the potential affects of turning off Internet Explorer. If you want to continue, click Yes, then click OK.
  7. Windows will display progress dialogs, such as Searching for required files, and Applying changes, as the associated Internet Explorer files are moved.
  8. Once the process has been completed, Windows will prompt you to restart the computer. Internet Explorer will not be turned off until the computer is restarted.
