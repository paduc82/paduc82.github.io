I wasn't trying to say "you're doing it wrong" but rather something like "you're not thinking about this the way the Microsoft programmers have thought about this" which requires two explanatory notes: 1. I am assuming I understand how the MS programmers did think about this, which could be entirely wrong, 2. Even if I did understand correctly, it's not to say that the MS programmers' approach is correct (we all make mistakes, after all).

but first up, in the nasty old days of DOS computers, printer drivers were not handled by the OS, but by the program. So Word, for example, might have drivers installed for a printer, but another program could not have drivers installed for that printer. Granted, the printers in those days were also not that great (we're taking dot matrix printers, here!). Windows changed that, allowing printer drivers to be installed to the OS, and all programs could then access those. But note that it is the printer driver that determines what the printer can and cannot do, not the program. The best way to access that is via the printer properties.

Next, remember that Word is showing you, in the preview, its pages, not the printer's pages. Fine distinction, and as you have pointed out, not what you needed. I suppose I have just gotten used to this, kind of like a family member with halitosis--you learn to live with it and after a while you find that you've forgotten how much you wished they would get it sorted out!

One point though. Print Layout view is not a 100% reflection of what will be printed on every page (more like 99.99%). I have found that with fiddly things like looooong footnotes, you may find print layout view showing one thing, and print preview showing something else. In those instances, Print Preview wins--that is what will be printed out.

In other words, what Word's aim is, with print preview (as I understand it) is to show you what it is sending through to the printer, not what the printer is going to do with it.

So to put it all together. Yes, Word could definitely do a better job of integrating the print settings into the preview. Why not show us what the printer is supposed to do with it? Of course, the one challenge is the plethora of printers out there, all with their own settings. You've at least got to sympathise with them on this score.

But as I say, I have learned to live with it. For standard printing tasks, print preview is fine, but the minute I need to do something out of the ordinary (multiple pages per sheet like your task, or booklet creation, etc.) I rather go to the printer options than Word's own settings.
