jay freedman say:
The way to get the incrementing numbers is to insert a SEQ field (http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/word-help/field-codes-seq-sequence-field-HP005186190.aspx). If you can construct a search string for a Replace operation, you can do it with a Replace All -- you don't even need a macro.

As an example, if your comment that the string isn't uniform means only that there are different numbers of spaces between the word Label and the following quote mark, or that there are sometimes tabs instead of spaces, then you can do it this way:

First go to the end of the document and type

Label SEQ \a "

Select the characters SEQ \a (but not the spaces before and after) and press Ctrl+F9 to turn it into a field. Press F9 to update the field (it should turn into the number 1). Now select everything from the L to the quote mark (but not anything after the quote) and cut it to the clipboard.

Now open the Replace dialog. In the Find What box, enter the string

Label^w"

(The code ^w means "whitespace", which is any sequence of spaces and tabs.)  In the Replace With box, enter the code

^c

which means "the contents of the clipboard". Click the Replace All button. Then select the whole document and press F9 to update all fields. The SEQ fields will produce the sequential numbers.

If you can't find a single search term that will match all the instances, you can  do it with two or more Replaces. Every time more SEQ fields are added and everything is updated, the fields will recalculate to produce a single sequence.

jay freedman say:

But I would still have to enter the Label SEQ \a " at the end of each document that I open?

 

No, entering that at the end of the first document and cutting it to the clipboard is all you need. The clipboard contents will stay there until you copy or cut something else. So if all you're doing is repeating the Replace on many documents, then you don't need to load the clipboard again.

gord dibben say:

You don't really need amacro for this.

 

Enter this formula in B1 and drag across to H1 then B1:H1 down as far as you need.

 

=INDEX($A:$A,(ROWS($1:1)-1)*7+COLUMNS($A:B)-1)

 

A macro could look like this since your original data is in even sets of 7 rows in a single column.

 

In your case you would enter 7 in the InputBox

 

Sub ColtoRows()
Dim rng As Range
Dim I As Long
Dim J As Long
    Set rng = Cells(Rows.Count, 1).End(xlUp)
    J = 1
    On Error Resume Next
    nocols = InputBox("Enter Number of Columns Desired")
        For I = 1 To rng.Row Step nocols
        Cells(J, "A").Resize(1, nocols).Value = _
                Application.Transpose(Cells(I, "A") _
                .Resize(nocols, 1))
        J = J + 1
    Next
    Range(Cells(J, "A"), Cells(rng.Row, "A")).ClearContents
    Exit Sub
End Sub

Kerning VS Character Spacing - Microsoft Community

samedysam say:

Kerning VS Character Spacing

In the Font Dialogue Box, under the Character Spacing Tab, there is the optiion for Kerning, and the option for Spacing. What is the difference? From the definition of Kerning, it appears these two concepts are exactly the same? Are they?
jay freedman say:

No, they aren't quite the same.

Character spacing applies equally to all characters in the selected text --  for example, add or remove 0.2 points of space after every character.

Kerning applies to specific pairs of characters when they occur together. For example, if the letters A and W occur together, kerning will push them closer together so the bottom of the A is actually under the top of the W. The same sort of thing would happen with A and T together. But it wouldn't affect A and M because there's no extra room at the bottom of the M.

The measurements needed to kern specific pairs is built into the font file -- at least, some font files but not others; commercial fonts often have more pairs than freebies (which may not have any). That's why you can't specify the amount of kerning that Word does when it's turned on.

Kerning is visually more important at large font sizes such as in headings, which is why Word lets you turn on kerning for sizes above a number of points that you can set in the dialog.
