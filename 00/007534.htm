j.e. mcgimpsey say: "Months" is a concept that gets a little fuzzy around the edges. Most of the time, 1 January 2013 to 1 February would be considered one month.  But what about 31 January 2013 to 28 February 2013? Is that 1 month since they're both the last day of their respective months? If so, is 29 January to 28 February the same one month?

Or should 31 Jan 2013 to 28 February 2013 be 0 months and 28 days? If so, there wouldn't be any date exactly one month and zero days after 31 January...

Unfortunately, encoding idiosyncratic rules can get rather complicated. 

One way to do it is to use the function DATEDIF(beginning_date, ending_date, "ymd_string") which, while undocumented, still exists in XL. See Chip Pearson's site for more details. You may get a strange result from time to time, but it is simpler than encoding your own function.

If your document contains continuous section breaks, it can be tricky trying to access the footer that is controlling the behaviour and you may need to use a macro containing the following code:

Dim i As Long, j As Long
With ActiveDocument
    For i = 2 To .Sections.Count
        With .Sections(i)
            For j = 1 To .Footers.Count
                .Footers(j).PageNumbers.RestartNumberingAtSection = False
            Next j
        End With
    Next i
End With
