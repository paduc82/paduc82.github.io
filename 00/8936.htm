How do I locate a font hidden in my Word 2016 for Mac document? - Microsoft Community

robertchalmers796 say:

How do I locate a font hidden in my Word 2016 for Mac document?

I don't mean print hidden text - I mean locate a font that seems to be hidden in there somewhere.

I have Nothing written in the KitchenSink font - but CreateSpace keeps telling me that it is there.

How can I locate it and remove it.

or

Can I embed fonts in the document yet?

john mcghie say:

Hi Robert:

Apple does not yet support embedding of fonts, and Microsoft follows their rules with Office 2016.

I assume CreateSpace is whinging about a "reference" to the font.  Since it is not in the "text", it is likely to be in the Style Sheet.

To search the styles list:

  1. Ggo to File>Print and change "Copies & Pages" to "Microsoft Word
  2. Change "Print what" to "Styles"

That will show you the font setting in each style.

The "Default" font is "The font set by the document theme" in the majority of cases.

If you can't find it there, you will have to read the native XML of the document.  To do so:

  1. Make a copy of the document (this is essential, once you have done the rest of this, it is almost impossible to restore it to a document that Word can open).
  2. Change the extension on the copy to ".zip"
  3. Right-click the Zipped version and choose "Open with" the BOM Utility
  4. Open the folder that results, then open the Word folder it contains
  5. Right-click the "Styles.xml" file and choose "Open with" and "TextEdit" (or TextWrangler, if you have it: it will give a much more readable display).
  6. Search from the bottom for the name of the font.
  7. Look left from the font name to get the Style name.
  8. Repeat the process to see how many other styles there are with that font and make a list.

Once you have the list of offending styles:

  1. Now: Open the original document in Word
  2. Go to Format>Style and click the Organizer button.

john mcghie say:

Hi Clara:

The crashes are "probably" caused by the tracked changes.

If you were to "Accept all changes in document" then save AND CLOSE the Word file, when you re-open it, it should be a lot more stable.

It is much safer to Paste>Paste Special>Unformatted text when you are pasting in from a different document.  That least the conflicting formatting from the source document behind and makes crashes much less likely.

Documents formatted with styles are far more stable and rugged than documents formatted with direct formatting.  Unless you apply a different style, everything in a document is in "Normal" style, which is Word's name for the "basic formatting".

If you leave everything in the document formatted with "Normal" style, Word has to create an exception to Normal Style for every paragraph (sometimes, more than one...).  When that happens, it's just a matter of time before crashes begin happening.

We probably need to clean that document up before we can fix it.  First, do a Maggie:  WARNING:  This will ACCEPT all tracked changes...

The Maggie:

1. Create a new blank document in .docx format

2. Carefully select all of the text in the bad document EXCEPT the last

paragraph mark

3. Copy it.

4. Paste in the new document.

5. Save under a new file name and close all, then re-open.

This technique for de-corrupting is known as "Doing a 'Maggie'", after

Margaret Secara from the TECHWR-L mailing list, who first publicised the

technique.

Now run through and apply the correct styles to your document:

Use the built-in Heading series of styles for your headings.  Use the built-in Body Text style for your text.  Use List Number for your numbered lists, and List Bullet for your bulleted lists.  Try not to use Normal style for anything: leave that for Word to apply to special things like the ends of table rows and a few others.  Of course, you are expected to customise the built-in styles so that the formatting is as you want it.  Bonus: you only need to change a style ONCE, and every paragraph in the document is instantly correct.  And if you don't like that change, you can just as instantly change again to something else, as many times as you like.

I sometimes change the font of a Normal style to shocking pink.  Then wherever I see shocking pink, I know I am looking at a paragraph that has yet to be formatted.  I change it back at the end of editing, because you can't entirely get rid of Normal style.

Turn your Non-Printing characters ( ¶ ) on when cutting and pasting, so you can see what you are doing: then be careful of tables, graphics, references, lists...  Pasting into the middle of them is a risky move: if you don't have to, paste carefully outside those complex structures.

There: you will get this done a lot quicker than it sounds reading the description, and afterwards, your document ought to be faste and stable.

Hope this helps
