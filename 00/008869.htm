joeu2004 say:

ShivaprasadKala wrote:

i have the data in the format of exponential (i.e i have the digits more than 15 in the cell), as i wanted to retrieve the same value, but it is giving the zeros at the end.  i do not have the records of previous data, so my only option is to get exact value from the existing  exponential data.  Suggest me how can i get that data.

I'm afraid the answer is:  you cannot.

I believe what you mean is:  you enter data with more than 15 significant digits (e.g. 1234567890123456789), Excel initially displays it as 1.23457E+18, and when you format it as Number or look at the cell value in the Formula Bar, it is actually 1234567890123450000.

Excel has changed the value to the latter.   There is no recovery, other than re-entering the original data (as text, not as a number).

The problem is three-fold.

First, when we enter data, Excel interprets only the first 15 significant digits, replacing any other digits on the right with zero.  So Excel interprets 1234567890123456789 as 1234567890123450000.  This is an arbitrary limitation.

Second, even if Excel interpreted all digits (as VBA does), the number might not be representable exactly due to limitations of 64-bit binary floating-point.  The limitation is not 15 significant decimal digits, an often-repeated misconception.  But in this example, the best internal representation is 1234567890123456768.

Third, nevertheless, Excel formats only the first 15 significant digits, another arbitrary limitation.  So even the internal representation of 1234567890123456768 appears as 1234567890123460000.

JP Ronse wrote:

What do you mean with "an often-repeated misconception"? 

Even the IEEE 754 standard states that 17, not 15, decimal digits are required to represent all 64-bit binary floating-point values.

But that does not mean the 64-bit binary representation and calculations are limited to 17 decimal digits, either.

If you remember your sentential calculus:  "if a, then b" does not imply "if b, then a".  ("All Greeks are people, but not all people are Greek".)

The largest and smallest positive values are (2^1023 - 2^970)*2 and 2^-1022, which have 309 and 1022 decimal digits respectively.

(Errata:  1022 decimal places, but 715 significant digits.)

Nevertheless, it is correct to say that Excel limits input (data entry) to the first 15 significant decimal digits.  That is, it substitutes zeros for any digits to the right.

And Excel formats only up to 15 significant decimal digits.  That is, it usually rounds to 15 significant digits, then substitutes zeros for any digits to the right.

Those are arbitrary limitations.  For example, VBA does not limit input to the first 15 significant decimal digits.

In any case, Excel calculations are not limited to 15 (or even 17) significant decimal digits.  See the example below.

It is also correct to say that every decimal number with 15 or fewer significant digits has a unique 64-bit binary value.  We cannot say that about every decimal number with 16 or more significant digits.

Those are properties of 64-bit binary floating-point representation.

Note that I wrote "unique binary value", not "accurate binary value".  All 15-digit integers (and even most 16-digit integers) can be represented exactly.

But most non-integers cannot be represented exactly, even if they have 15 or fewer significant decimal digits.
