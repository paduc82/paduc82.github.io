john mcghie say:
Hi Whatever Your Name is:

It's important not to get too panicked at this stage -- although you have a serious issue, it is quite fixable! And it is not that difficult.

I think your first step should be to go buy an external hard disk, plug it in, and assign it to Time Machine.  You will need a disk at least the same size as the internal hard disk.  Fortunately, 1 Terabyte hard disks are only a couple of hundred dollars these days, and that's twice what you need.

When you set it up, do not "omit" anything: you want a complete copy of everything on your hard drive, because you are about to erase or replace the hard drive.

Now:  Set the new HDD up on Time Machine and WALK AWAY!!!  {Grin}.  No matter how tempting it is to "supervise" the computer, you need to steel yourself to walk away.  You want a second completely clean copy of the internal drive on that new external drive.  This copying process will run for quite some time, possibly several hours.  It will be much slower, and possibly not as complete, if the machine is being used at the time.  Not a bad idea to disconnect the network while this is happening: makes things run quite a bit faster.

Now once you have your two complete backup copies, you can do what the system has recommended

""This Disk needs to be repaired using the Recovery HD. Restart your computer, holding down the "Command" key and the "R" key until you see the 'Apple' logo.  When the OS X Utilities window appears, choose Disk Utility.""

Obviously make your backup first, because that repair process is quite invasive.  It stresses the disk quite highly.  If the disk is just about to fail, this is the time it will do it.

I think it is highly likely the HDD in that machine is failing, which would be the cause of the trouble.  Your machine is at the age when MacBook drives start to die: in my opinion, Apple runs its internals far too hot, which is why they fail so quickly.

You could have the drive replaced, but it's REALLY not a good investment in a machine that old.  Far better to break the bank and buy a new machine with a Solid State drive.  That will be a LOT more rugged and last a lot longer.  It will also be a lot faster, with a much nicer screen, and a lot more space :-)

Now, may we please have a little "chat" about "BUT I can not (CAN NOT) loose my data."  Of course you can.  We all can.  And you know it perfectly well.  I do understand what you mean: that the data on that drive is so valuable it would represent a major loss.  Of course it would, hence the rest of my suggestions: so you don't lose it.  The only thing we can guarantee about computers is that eventually they will all fail.  No matter how good they are, no matter who made them, no matter how careful you are, eventually you are going to see the dreaded "unrecoverable disk error on file ""  The disk you're working on has a media problem"

I am old, so I have seen it four or five times.  It's the nastiest feeling in the pit of your stomach.  And at that point, whatever your data, it is either backed up, or gone!  Been there: I feel your concern!

That's why I am suggesting that you buy another external HDD.  With data as valuable as yours, you never should allow yourself to get to the point where you are completely dependent on one single device: because one day...

Now: When it comes to the "Will I or won't I wipe the drive" decision, the place to make that is while standing in the Apple Shop.  My strong guidance is "Don't bother".  Once a drive gets to the "unrecoverable error" stage, throw it out.  It's just not worth the grief to try to repair it.  Yes, it may struggle along another year or two with more and more sectors failing every month or so, and once or twice a year you will need to go through this exercise of wiping and rebuilding it again.  Not worth it: life is too short, and your data is too valuable.

Replace the drive.  And if you replace the drive, replace it with a solid-state drive that cannot suffer this problem.  And if you replace the drive with a SSD, replace the computer too, with one designed to drive the SSD and take advantage of the fact that it's 100 times the speed.

Of course if cash is a bit tight currently (I am an author too, I know about this state...) then you can work backwards: buy a new drive for the current machine.  Make it an SSD if you can.

Be aware that when you get the machine back after replacing the drive, it will have only an operating system on it.  There will be no software or data.  You will need to restore both from your Time Machine backup.  Time Machine will talk you through that, and you can get advice from the Apple Shop.

Hope this helps
