steve rindsberg say:

PPT doesn't have a clipping function in quite the same sense that, say, Corel Draw and probably Adobe Illustrator do.  You can't define a clipping path, use it to clip another shape or shapes, and later adjust the clipping path to hide or reveal more of the clipped graphics.

But you can use the Merge Shapes command on the Format | Insert Shapes area to do a one-time clip (ie, you can use intersect to combine an image, for example with a shape and end up with the image clipped to the shape boundaries ... as a new shape that replaces the original shapes).

steve rindsberg say:

Any software that produces EMF will likely have the same kinds of problems.  EMF/WMF graphics are fairly primitive and don't include the ability to handle things like gradient fills, soft edges/shadows and the like.

Instead, programs that export EMF have to perform workarounds; to "preserve" a gradient, they'll export gazillions of rectangles or polygons that, together, look like a single gradient filled shape.  Editing these?  A nightmare!

I would suggest this workflow:

Draw your graphics in any program that works well for you, but stick with simple shapes and lines.

Don't do any gradient fills or other fancy effects.

Export as EMF, import the EMF into PowerPoint, ungroup it and THEN apply any effects you like to the shapes.

Once you do this, the graphic will be in native MS Office format; you can copy/paste it from PPT to Word and it should retain all of its characteristics.

You might, in fact, be able to import the EMF directly into Word, ungroup and apply effects there.  Since PPT is the tool I use most often, I'd rather work there and then copy to Word, but if going directly to Word works well for you, leave PPT out of the equation entirely.
