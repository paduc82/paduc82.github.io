I disagree.  Today, a computer MUST BE as appliance-like as possible for the majority of users.  If PC technology is not to the point where a computer IS an appliance, then a whole lot of PC designers and software developers have completely failed at their jobs.

For the majority of users, a computer must be an intuitive and easy-to-use office tool, not unlike a fax machine, paper shredder, digital copier, elevator, thermostat, or coffee maker.  Given how technology has taken over the corporate office of today, employees cannot be required to become experts at using these tools.  They need to concentrate on their true jobs, not waste time trying to use their office machinery.  All such office machines must be simple to use, with no exceptions, and especially not excepting the PC.

If a modern-day PC is not to the point of being an appliance, then we're going backwards, not forwards, with PC technology.  We do not need to go back to the days before Windows et al when users could only use a computer if they knew how to write programs, be it with APL, Basic, Fortran, Cobol, DOS, or whatever languages were used by non-computer-science majors.  Sure, there was some DOS-based software available, but you still had to know DOS well enough to properly use such software (e.g., to install the software, find files, make backup copies, troubleshot problems, and so forth).

As a technology parallel, a minivan or a basic midsize four-door sedan is the automotive equivalent of an appliance.  Owners need these types of vehicles to do their job as simply as possible.  The owner's learning curve must be nonexistent.  Owners need to be able to operate such vehicles without even reading the owner's manual.  Yes, owners must read the manual, say the corporate legal types, but they shouldn't have to.  If you travel, and rent one of these vehicles, then you can't be searching for the wiper controls while trying to find your exit while driving on a thruway in a city you've never been in before on a dark, rainy evening.  Your safety and the safety of others on the road require that such appliance-like vehicles be intuitive and easy to operate.
