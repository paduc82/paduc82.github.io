It used to be possible to summon the Keyboard Viewer and see a mapping of characters in any font. Unfortunately, they took the font-selection feature away from the latest versions of Keyboard Viewer. The good news is, the old version from OS X 10.4 can be made to work as a separate application. At least it works here with Snow Leopard. So, if you are interested:

  • Download Keyboard Viewer OLD from OS X 10.4.zip from my Skydrive account.
  • Unzip it (double click on it).
  • Move the resulting folder to your Applications folder (just to be orderly).
  • Open the purple-highlighted application called KeyboardViewerServer. It's the old keyboard viewer, with a font selection dropdown menu included at the bottom.
  • If you find it useful, drag KeyboardViewerServer to the dock to keep it handy.

I noticed font selection does not work with all fonts (Symbol is one of them), but maybe you'll be fortunate and it will work with Bodoni Ornaments, as it does function with Windings and other esoteric choices.

Accuracy calculating gamma relativistic factor

joeu2004 say:
F.Gomez wrote:

In relativistic math, the gamma factor may be computed by: C/(C^2 â€“ V^2)^(1/2) or 1/(1 â€“ v^2/c^2)^(1/2).  Excel yields different results for V â‰¥ 99%C.  Rounding of binary numbers due to IEEE 754 does not apply, since it refers to high exponents.  Which formula yields the most accurate answer?


I presume that "C" is the constant c, the speed of light.  I also assume v <= c. [1]

This is a numerical analysis question.  Generally, I would expect that the most reliable result would come from the following formula [2]:

1/((1-v/c)*(1+v/c))^0.5

I believe that formula will be well-behaved regardless of the units that you choose for v and c.

Note that if you use metric units, c is 299,792,458 m/s by definition.  If you use English units, c is about 299792458*100/12/5280/2.54 (186282.397051221) mi/s.

The magnitude of the metric units is much larger than the English units.

The problem with c^2 and v^2 is:  if they are expressed in metric units, we are more likely to loose significant precision because the results exceed the limits of 64-bit binary floating-point.

There is no difference or only infinitesimal difference for v << c, for example v = c/2 [3].  So I looked at v almost equal to c.

If you use metric units, v = c-2^-24 is the smallest difference permitted by 64-bit binary floating-point.  Then:

1. c/(c^2-v^2)^0.5 is about 52,996,320.0000958.
2. 1/((1-v/c)*(1+v/c))^0.5 is about 47,453,132.8121258.

Likewise, if you use English units, v = c-2^-35 is the smallest difference.  Then:

3. c/(c^2-v^2)^0.5 is about 67,441,431.6393488.
4. 1/((1-v/c)*(1+v/c))^0.5 is about 67,108,864.0000000.

Originally, I thought that #1 and #3 might be the better result because they are larger.  Note that the formulas should approach infinity as c-v approaches zero.

However, there is no way to know whether the larger values are reliable.  They might be larger simply because of the greater numerical "error" (loss of precision) due to squaring large numbers.

In fact, if v = c-11*2^-35 in English units, #4 is larger than #3.

You might note that #4 is much larger than #2.  Again, that might be because we lose more accuracy using 64-bit binary floating-point arithmetic with the larger metric units.

But I believe that is only due to the extremely close values of v and c that I chose.

Indeed, for the formula in #2 and #4, there is no difference between metric and English units for v <= 99.999999999%*c.


-----
[1] There was a claim recently, since recanted, of a particle traveling at v > c.  And sci-fi allows for v > c in warp theory.


[2] I wrestle between 1/((1-v/c)*(1+v/c))^0.5 and (1/((1-v/c)*(1+v/c)))^0.5.

Initially, I tend to prefer the latter because it performs the square root last.  Note that the square root is computed by an approximation algorithm.  So it might introduce a lot of numerical "error".

On the other hand, 1/((1-v/c)*(1+v/c)) is likely to be very large, since we expect the denominator to be small when v is very close to c.  The large number might challenge, if not exceed, the limits of 64-bit binary floating-point, and that might introduce a lot of numerical "error" when we compute its square root.

So I suspect that 1/((1-v/c)*(1+v/c))^0.5 is more stable when v is very close to c.


[3] [EDIT] Actually, both formulas produce the same results to 6 decimal places or more with v <= 99.99998%*c, regardless of the units (metric or English).  And the relative error (difference) between the two formulas is less than 0.01% with v <= 99.999999999997%*c for metric units and v <= 99.9999999999991%*c for English units.

Those boundaries are not based on an exhaustive analysis, but on a crude comparison of the form v = 99.9...9x%*c, where "9...9" is 0 to 12 nines, and "x" is the digit 1 to 9.

So while the question might be interesting academically, it seems like much ado about nothing in practice.
