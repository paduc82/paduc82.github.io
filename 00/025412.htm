steve rindsberg say:

As you've noticed, once the images go bad, they're lost permanently. There's no repairing them at that point.

I'd start by making sure your users are up-to-date with any available service packs/patches for Office.

As a general rule and especially if you're having problems with images going missing, it's best always to work with the presentation on the local hard drive.  Copy from jump drive/cloud storage/etc to the local HDD, open it from there, save, then copy back to the cloud/jump drive when done.

When copying PowerPoint files to the local HDD, put them in a folder that's not being "watched" by any file synchronization software (Dropbox, OneDrive, Carbonite or other backup apps). 

Another general rule:  it's safer to save images to the local HDD and insert them into PPT from there.  Once inserted into PPT, they become part of the presentation.  You shouldn't thereafter need to keep them with the presentation (but if you continue to get occasional image corruption problems, it might be a good idea to keep the images and PPT file together).  Copy/Pasting images can be problematic; I'd avoid it from the web. Screenshots made with e.g. the Windows snipping tool seem safe to paste into PPT though. 

It shouldn't be necessary to save every five minutes, but a good habit to develop is:  whenever you've done more work on a file than you would risk losing, save.  Before making any drastic changes, save.  And every so often, save to a new version of the file (numbered, dated, whatever suits you best).

There are add-ins that will automatically save to sequentially numbered files every so often. 

Shyam Pillai's Sequential Save add-in comes to mind.

There are a number of possible explanations for why verification codes don't always arrive. Read through the list of the most common causes, and see if any of them apply to you.

Does your phone block calls or texts from unknown numbers?
If so, change your phone settings then choose I don't have a code. We'll send another verification code.

Did your email send your verification code to your junk folder?
Check your junk email folder for a message from a Microsoft account, and use the code sent to you. Valid verification codes come from an @accountprotection.microsoft.com email address.

Mark @accountprotection.microsoft.com as a trusted sender to receive your verification code in your inbox.

Is your phone number or email address entered correctly?
To keep your information safe when you're signing in, we only show you the last two digits of your phone number or the first two characters of your email address.

To see if your security info is correct:

Sign in to your Security basics page (See reference below) with your Microsoft account.

Sign in using:

https://account.microsoft.com/security

Select Update info.

Choose a method that you know works, or choose I don't have any of these to replace your security info.

Does your alternate email address end in @outlook.com, @hotmail.com, @live.com, or @msn.com?

If so, you're using one Microsoft account to verify another Microsoft account. This can make it tricky to keep track of which one you're signed in to. When you sign in to the second account (to get the code sent to that email), most browsers automatically sign you out of the first account (the one that's actually requesting the code).

To get your verification code:

Using a browser in privacy mode, sign in with the first account. This lets you stay signed in to both accounts at the same time.

When you're prompted to enter the verification code that was sent to your alternate email address, don't close the browser window.
Open a new window in privacy mode. Ctrl + Shift + P is the shortcut for InPrivate Browsing in Microsoft Edge and Internet Explorer. If you're using a different browser, check that browser's help for info about privacy mode.

In the new window, sign in to your alternate email account and look for the message from the Microsoft account team. Copy or write down the verification code from the message.

Return to the window that is asking you for the verification code. Enter the code and follow the instructions.

I decided that would be helpful to automatically fill in the background cells of a column based on the first word contained in the cell itself. Since I would have to adapt this concept from a situation to another, therefore the words of the criteria will vary, I decided to have a editable reference legend that the criteria would pick up automatically. Hello John,

There are few factors that can contribute to the issue you are facing. It might be caused by network connection or time and date are not correct.

We suggest that you use the activation troubleshooter which can help you resolve errors for Office 365. Download it on this link and follow the on-screen steps to troubleshoot the issue.

You can also try the following steps provided below to help resolve your concern:

Try to check and update the date and time on your device.

Connect to the Internet

While you don’t need to be online at all times to use Office, you do need to connect to the Internet intermittently so Office can verify that your Office 365 subscription is still active. If Office can’t check the status of your subscription for an extended period of time (usually around 30 days), you’ll see the Couldn’t Verify Subscription message, and Office will eventually go into reduced functionality mode. To fix this, reconnect to the Internet, and then choose Retry.

NOTE: Make sure that your Office 365 Home subscription is active.

  1. Close all Office apps.
  2. Go to your Services & subscriptions page.
  3. If prompted, choose Sign in and enter the Microsoft account email and password associated with your Office 365 subscription.
  4. Review the details under the Subscriptions heading.

Deactivate your Office subscription

If you've exceeded the number of installs that your Office 365 subscription supports, you can deactivate an Office install that you're no longer using so you can install Office on a different device.

  1. Go to https://office.com/myaccount and sign in using your Microsoft account, if prompted.
  2. Select Install >.
  3. Under Install information, select Deactivate Install to deactivate the Office installs that you no longer use.

After you deactivate an Office install, you still can view and print documents from that device, but you won't be able to edit them or create new ones. Office lets you know that the install is deactivated by showing Unlicensed Product errors. If you want to use Office on the device, you'll need to reactivate Office by signing in to your Office 365 account when prompted.
