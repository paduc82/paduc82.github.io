The manufacturers recovery partition likely got wiped out when the engineer reinstalled Windows 10.

How to: Perform a clean install using Reset this PC in Windows 10

Another way you could try reinstalling Windows 10 if the recovery manager is still functional is by booting into it.

New computers that come preinstalled with Windows often have what is called a recovery partition. This is used to reinstall the operating system in the event of a system crash. To access it, you will need to boot into when you start your computer by pressing a function key. This can be either F1, F2, F9, F10, F11, F12 or even DEL or Tab key. Consult the owners manual that came with your PC for instructions about how to reinstall Windows.

If your recovery partition is not available or damaged, you should contact the manufacturer of your computer and request a recovery disc set you can use to reinstall Windows 7. They might charge a small shipping and handling fee.

Maybe not exactly what you're after, but I go on anyway... So, hah ! ....

First - It really comes down to the game in question.  Why it works better ? Well with controllers

it may not, but with wheels and joysticks, oh yeah! (more later).


- Games that do use analogue, such as Tomb Raider, and Hitman (at least the older ones I've played used analogue movement), where there is a fine point between walking and running would benefit to some degree if you find the walk run point difficult to adapt to, but you can still argue - 'just get used to it'. 

So, whether calibrating makes a controller work better, or not, is largely a matter of personal perception.

- I can also see the potential for having the 'twitch' right when using a controller with driving games. If a controller can be calibrated well enough it could make pushing, or wiggling the stick more of an option than flicking the stick (which is what I find I do in driving games regardless),

and this may suit some people more than flicking.

Wheels rule for racing though.

- For racing games I reckon calibrating the steering wheel is very important, and I have spent a fair

bit of time fine tuning my wheels.

Nuance would be the operative word when comparing using a well calibrated wheel to a controller.

Early on I tinkered with individual calibrations for different games.

Getting the drag, and spring right, and deciding whether to use full rotation, or not, are the main focus. Over time I've pretty much settled down to one profile, but I do still tweak the profile

if a game desperately requires it.

In conjunction with a suitable wheel calibration, being able to calibrate the throttle and brake is a big help with controlling brake vs acceleration ratios, making for more controlled turns, whether trying to keep a line, or drifting. I always use a bit more dead space on the brake than the throttle.

- Calibrating a Joystick for flight sims to suit the 'pilots' style also benefits.

When learning both driving and flying calibration would be different from when one is

experienced due to less over-steer from panic (The Jerk on the handle ;D ). 

For example, a bit more drag and maybe some extra dead space on the wheel, with a less sensitive brake and throttle should help a learner.


- I reckon both of these peripheral types are more suitable to calibrating than a controller due to them having a greater degree of movement in the axis ( maybe a joystick only has a greater perceived movement due to it's length and full hand grip).


btw -  I rarely use controllers these days, except when hoping in a car, as in the GTA series, BF or

Far Cry 2&3, where I keep the controller handy, but I don't calibrate to suit the game.

I just set-up the basic global calibration to make sure the sticks are centred properly.

- My main control option  is a Logitech G13 keypad with mouse, and while the G13  does have an analogue option for the thumb-stick I stopped trying to find out if games work with the analogue option (so few do) and just go with using  WSAD on the thumb-stick.
