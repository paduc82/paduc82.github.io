a. user say:

As Suzanne suggested, start with the equation editor (Insert tab->Equation).

There are several ways you can use the editor.

You can for example insert predefined "blocks" (such as one of the Summation blocks), then fill in the small square boxes. 

Or you can try to type your equation using "UnicodeMath," which is "A Nearly Plain-Text Encoding of Mathematics" described in a paper you can download from http://www.unicode.org/notes/tn28/UTN28-PlainTextMath-v3.1.pdf

This paper is by Murray Sargent, who is the author/designer of the Equation Editor (and several other layout features) in Microsoft Office products. You may find his blog articles at https://blogs.msdn.microsoft.com/murrays/ useful too. Just bear in mind that not everything described in the UnicodeMath paper is actually available in Word. For example, I do not think the Size operator (upside-down F) works at all, and the parameters for \box do not seem to work as described in the paper. In particular, you may find that the equation numbering technique (which uses a # sign) does not work in Word 2013 - it's a relatively recent addition in Word.

Personally, I find it easier to use UnicodeMath in conjunction with the Math autocorrect keywords recognised by Word (and other Office programs). So for example, one way you could type this equation would be

cov(X,Y)=1/(n-1)\cdot [\sum _(i=1)^n\naryand \begin Y_i X_i\end -\begin \begin \sum _(i=1)^n\naryand Y_i\cdot \sum _(i=1)^n\naryand X_i\end /n\end ]

You should be able to

 a. insert a new blank equation

 b. copy and paste that code into it

 c. use the drop-down menu at the right-hand end of the equation to change the equation into "Professional" mode. The first time you do that, you may see some of the parts of the equation changing and perhaps even looking like the real thing. However, you may need to use that menu option 3 or 4 times for the equation to be displayed as it should be. (It should look like the one Suzanne posted).

If you now change the equation back to "linear" mode, you should see the equation approximately as it is written above, except that each of the autocorrect keywords (\cdot etc.) will have been resolved, so \cdot appears as a dot, \sum appears as a Summation Sigma, \naryand appears as a sort of hatched block and so on. This is often easier to work with than the Professional mode display, although in my experience, Word has a habit of adding stuff such as spaces where you do not want them and which can prevent correct redisplay in Professional mode. 

You can see above that I have put a space after every autocorrect keyword. This is not strictly necessary, but I find it simpler to do it that way than to distinguish between delimiter characters (which would also tell Word it had reached the end of the autocorrect keyword) and others. For example, I think in the above code you could probably remove all the spaces after the autocorrect keywords except the ones after "\naryand" (because they are both followed by letters).

You will also see that in the above equation, the first sum is "full height," with the i=1 and n below and above the Sigma respectively. However, the other sums are "squashed," with the i=1 to the right of the bottom of the Sigma, and so on. This is a layout decision made by the Equation editor, and as far as I know there are no options to change this behaviour. To lay it out more like the image you have, you can use a 2-row, 1 column matrix instead, and put an underbar under the product of the two sums. However, the vertical alignment of the two parts of the formula then goes wrong. You can try to fix that using a kludge by adding more rows to the matrix. Your equation also has taller square brackets surrounding the expression than Word would create. You can also fix that by adding a "vphantom" - you put something in the vphantom that would increase the height of the expression, and Word treats the expression as having that height, but does not display the "content" of the vphantom.

The code I ended up with was as follows - I am sure with a bit more tweaking you can make the formula appear how you want, and there may be a much better way to control the alignment.

cov(X,Y)=1/(n-1)\cdot [\vphantom (\matrix (\ensp @\ensp @\ensp @\ensp @\ensp @\ensp @\ensp @\ensp ))\sum _(i=1)^n\naryand \begin Y_i X_i\end -\matrix (\underbar (\sum _(i=1)^n\naryand Y_i\cdot \sum _(i=1)^n\naryand X_i)@\ensp @n@\ensp )]


IMO two of the most useful math autocorrect keywords are \begin and \end. They insert left and right hand braces that can be used for grouping other symbols so that (for example) you can ensure that the "n" is the denominator for both Sigma Yi and Sigma Xi, and not just one of them. Unlike ordinary braces, which may appear when you don't expect them, these braces should always be removed (as long as they are in pairs!)

There's quite a handy reference at https://www.cs.bgu.ac.il/~khitron/Equation%20Editor.pdf
