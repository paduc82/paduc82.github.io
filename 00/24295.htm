cbarnhorst say:

Yes, rollback to the immediately previous build is supported, but not back to any build before.  That means when you first join the program and get your first preview build you can roll back to 15063.  But after the second preview build you can only roll back to the last preview build but not all the way back to 15063 anymore. 

Insider previews are not as safe as just staying on the general release that you are on now.  Previews are not upgrades to 15063 in the sense of cumulative updates.  They are whole new trial versions of the new version of Windows 10 due in the Fall, called Fall Creators Update.  They are known to be buggy from the word go.  That's why the Insider program exists:  to find and report bugs remaining in the software.  Think "BETA" software.  Do not use ANY preview build on your primary, production, or every-day devices.

Slow Ring is safer than Fast Ring but that is not the best criteria for choosing your ring.  The best criteria is whether you are interested in usability issues or reliability issues.

Slow Ring builds are buggy, but a little time and a lot of exposure in the Fast Ring has shown them as "stable" (not known to crash or have major deal-killer flaws).  Slow Ring users don't like hunting for bugs but like to try out the usability of new features.

Fast Ring builds are buggy, but newer and therefore relatively untested in the wild.  They are believed stable enough to expose to a large number of mostly casual testers (us) but are expected to have significant unreliability problems that may affect usefulness of a feature.  Sometimes a major feature.  Fast Ring users like to hunt for bugs and be the first to report them and like to discuss them in forums and such.

Slow Ring is like a new game release trailer.  Fast Ring is like a new game released too soon.

daniel g. grau say:
I sympathize, because bullets and levels are hard to understand. At times they seem to take a life of their own. Try this method:
  1. First, define a tight default tab setting, as Word will be indenting/de-indenting the bullets according to these positions. Select Format > Tabs and type .25 in the "Default tab stops" field.
  2. Select Format > Bullets and Numbering. Click con the last tab, List Styles, then on the Add button and type a descriptive name. This will be the name of your list style for all bullets in the hierarchy. Say you call it "MyBullets."
  3. We'll now define the indents of the first bullet. Make sure "Apply formatting to" is set for "1st level." Then click on the Increase or Decrease indent icons to define the paragraphs indents. Here you can also define the bullet style (or whether you want numbering). This is controlled through the dropdown menu right above the frame that shows a depiction of the bullet hierarchy. You will probably be selecting "â€¢" as your first bullet.
  4. Select "2nd level" and repeat step 3 above. Keep on doing that for as many indentation levels as you plan to use and OK your way back to the document.
Now the list style MyBullets is defined. Let's apply it:
  1. Select one or more paragraphs that you want to be in the first bullet level.
  2. Using the Formatting toolbar  apply style "MyBullets" by selecting it from the first dropdown menu. (If you don't see this toolbar, do View > Toolbars > Formatting, and if you still don't see it, click on the oval button at the top right corner of the document's window.)
  3. To indent or de-indent bullet levels (a.k.a. demote and promote), use the "Increase indent" and "Decrease indent" buttons in the Ribbon's Home tab.
An important concept is that list styles are superimposed to paragraph styles and their formatting. Thus, if you select a paragraph that is already formatted and you apply the list style MyBullets, its indents will change accordingly. And conversely, you can apply a paragraph style over a list style. Whatever your sequence, the last operation will prevail.
