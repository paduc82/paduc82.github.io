a. user say:

The upgrade will come in the form of a "Windows Update", and there is some chance that hardware in your computer will not have a fully functioning (or functioning at all) driver for Windows 10.  In that case, you may lose networking, display, sound, or anything that relies on a driver.  Please browse the forums to see the numerous issues people are having with drivers from AMD, Intel, Nvidia, VIA, Realtek, Synaptics to name a few.  This does not mean that all people are having problems with these drivers, just some.  Do you feel lucky?

While Windows will attempt to preserve all of your programs and files, there are some programs that are noted to have problems with Windows 10, and an update may or may not be forthcoming.  Examples include certain games, antivirus, etc.  Again, browse the forums to see what issues people are having.

The final version should be more stable, however I'm certain that they won't have 100% compatibility with the vast array of hardware and devices from Windows 7 and 8/8.1. 

If you currently use Windows Media Center, since Windows 10 will not support it, you will lose Windows Media Center.

If you currently use Windows 7 and enjoy a powerful start menu, you will lose that, because Windows 10 does not have one, or you could use a third party program to restore that functionality.

If you currently use Windows 8/8.1 and enjoy a touch-centric interface, you will lose that, because Windows 10 does not have full support for gestures, charms are missing, etc.

The upgrade is free if you upgrade within one year of Windows 10's release, and Microsoft says that it will continue to be supported for the lifetime of that device, so most people interpret that to mean that you will never have to pay anything for Windows 10 to be continually updated with whatever revisions come out, for the lifetime of that device.  Generally, the device means the motherboard of your computer or laptop, so you should be able to change graphics cards, hard drives, or whatever, however Microsoft has not been 100% clear on this, so take this with a grain of salt.

Excel always uses radians for trigonometric functions. Unlike a calculator, it does not have a setting to use degrees by default.
