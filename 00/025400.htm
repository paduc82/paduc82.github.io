bill manville say:

The hyperlink address should appear when you point the cursor at a cell containing a hyperlink.

There are 3 ways to create a hyperlink.
a) Have Excel do it for you when you type an email address in a cell (requires Tools > AutoCorrect Options > Autoformat as you Type > Internet and network paths with hyperlinks.
  - the cell content and the hyperlink will be consistent
  - however, if the cell content is subsequently changed by someone who does not have that option set they will become inconsistent

b) Use Insert > Hyperlink.
  - the user specifies separately the text in the cell and the hyperlink so they may not be consistent
  - if the cell content is subsequently changed by someone who does not have the Autocorrect option set, or if the new cell content is not an email address they will become inconsistent

c) Use the HYPERLINK worksheet function.
  - the user specifies (as a quoted string, or as a reference to a cell) the text to display and the hyperlink
    e.g. =HYPERLINK("mailto:*** Email address is removed for privacy ***","*** Email address is removed for privacy ***")
  - if a cell reference is used for one of the arguments but not for the other, it is possible for the displayed text to be inconsistent with the hyperlink

If you want to display the hyperlink address in a cell, to aid checking, you could use this VBA function;

Function ShowHyperlink(C As Range) As String
  If C.Hyperlinks.Count = 1 Then
    ShowHyperlink = C.Hyperlinks(1).Address
    If C.Hyperlinks(1).SubAddress <> "" Then
      ShowHyperlink = ShowHyperlink & "#" & C.Hyperlinks(1).Address
    End If
  Else
    ShowHyperlink = ""
  End If
End Function

Enter =ShowHyperlink(B2) in a cell, to show the hyperlink address (if any) in B2.
Copy the formula down to show hyperlinks for all cells in a column

They reason why Microsoft Outlook is considered a less secure app is because you let it remember your password. For desktop users that keep their computers at home, it's not that big of a deal.

But say, you use a laptop, and say you travel around with your laptop and it gets stolen, if the thieves get into your operating system, they could easily get into MS Outlook since it remembered your password. Same thing goes for apps like Windows Live Mail, Mozilla Thunderbird, etc. because you are letting them remember your email password for your convenience. That's not to say webmail absolutely is more secure because you can also let your browsers save your passwords and you're falling in the same trap.

joelcoehoorn say:

Outlook is a less secure app because it only does a simple username/password login. Apps that are "more secure" also use an OAuth authentication token. This allows you to approve the specific app before it can connect. It also means you only give your Google password to Google; Outlook never sees the password directly. It's like a slightly weaker two-factor authentication.

And speaking of two-factor, IF (and only if) you enable two-factor authentication on your account, it's probably okay to allow those less secure apps, like Outlook. The second factor can take the place of the authentication token you'd get via OAuth, because even if someone compromises your Google password via Outlook or similar, it won't do them any good. If you do NOT want to use two-factor authentication, I **STRONGLY** suggest you use something other than Outlook to access your e-mail. 

I administer both a Google Apps (G-Suite) and O365 domain for a small college, and I really wish **both** that Google's administrator dashboard would allow me two require two-factor for users when (and only when) they opt to enable Less Secure Apps, **and** that Microsoft would add OAuth support to Outlook.

joelcoehoorn say:

This is only sort of true. It's not just that Outlook remembers your password. Not allowing "Less Secure Apps" mean you use OAuth, and with OAuth, Outlook would never have to see your password at all. Instead, you log directly into Google. Only Google sees your Google password. Google sends you an OAuth token, and from then on Outlook would use that token.

This is important even for desktop users at home. Allowing Less Secure apps opens up your account to brute-force attacks from users who are nowhere near your home that would fail otherwise. I manage a Google Apps domain without 1800 users, and I get reports from my users all time asking about a "Blocked Sign-In Attempt" message they received from Google. These are cases where an attacker successfully brute-forced a password, but was blocked from using it because Less Secure apps were disabled.

In the stolen laptop scenario, it's not just that thieves could use Outlook to get into your e-mail account. Apps like Outlook must store your password using reversible encryption (or worse, no encryption), allowing the thieves to eventually recover that password, where they could use it to also log in via the web interface. With Less Secure apps disabled, Outlook (assuming they add support for this) would never need to store the password at all. It would store an authentication token, which you can revoked at any time. Thus, you can lock out the stolen laptop even after it's stolen.

It's worth noting here that Outlook.com also uses OAuth.
