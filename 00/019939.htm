UI consistency on phone - Microsoft Community

asbjørn say:

UI consistency on phone

Now, I haven't installed the 10051 preview, as I have no spare Windows Phone lying around and it is a bit too rough to use on a main phone.

But I have been looking at screenshots and videos and I find the lack of UI consistency alarming. I count at least 3 completely separate styles:

The Phone app has that funny-looking tab/toolbar thing at the top which is used in no other app. It looks like it was borrowed from Android 2.x. That just needs to go!

The Outlook E-mail and Calendar apps look completely foreign. They look like warmed-over Android apps (which they probably are). Now, the features are nice, but why are the commands suddenly at the top? That is completely non-standard on Windows Phone. Also, they do not appear to respect the light or dark theme (if that is still going to be a thing in Windows 10 for phones).

The People and Messaging apps on the other hand look more like what I'd expect - an evolution of the Metro design language.

The biggest problem here is inconsistency. Commands are seemingly randomly distributed around the screen. No coherent color usage. Pick one style and use it consistently. Not that every app has to look exactly the same - but they do have to share a common design language and work the same way.

Of course the hamburger menu is a separate discussion in itself. I don't mind it particularly and it does serve a purpose in some cases. For example, switching folders in the Outlook E-mail app is easier using the hamburger button than having to press "...", then Folders, then selecting the folder. But - it is placed in the worst possible place on the screen, making it difficult to use one-handed. Sadly, it is often the most logical place to put it at the top.

Another problem is the increasing Android-ification of Windows on phones. If I wanted to use Android, I'd buy an Android phone. I like the fact that Windows Phone is doing something fresh and different on the UX side. That was one of the reasons for choosing Windows Phone. With this strategy, you are making it increasingly hard to answer the question "Why should I get a Windows Phone?". Most Microsoft services have better apps on Android/iOS than on Windows Phone (Office being a major example), so superior Microsoft ecosystem integration is no argument. A major argument is the UX, but you are slowly eroding that as well. What is left?
