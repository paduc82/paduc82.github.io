jp ronse say: Hi,

"<" and ">" are operators and I think that Excel is confused by what you are asking.

I redefined the formula by saying that Excel should look and count the cells that are equal to "<3 years".

=COUNTIFS($B$6:$B$15,$B$1;$C$6:$C$15,"="&B2)
=COUNTIFS($B$6:$B$15,$B$1;$C$6:$C$15,"="&B3)


And that gives me 6 and 4 as result.

Maybe an MVP can come with a better explanation, my interpretation is that Excel considers it as formulas, by saying look for "="&B2, it considers it as a string.

Logic or not? I agree that the modal user will not think about and Excel should interpret it in a better way. But it is what it is.

Note: be very careful using relative addresses as I saw in "=COUNTIFS(B6:B15,5,C6:C15,"< 3 years")". The results become really unpredictable (and wrong) when you drag and drop.





jp ronse say: Two alternative formulas:

=SUMPRODUCT(--($B$6:$B$15=$B$1),--($C$6:$C$15=B2))
=SUMPRODUCT(--($B$6:$B$15=$B$1),--($C$6:$C$15=B3))

gives also and directly the result.

"--" is known as the unitary operator, it translates TRUE and FALSE to 1 & 0.
