Bob has given you the correct information. I'm just adding a note that different versions of Office have different defaults or "original" values. You can always check your version's defaults using these steps:

  1. Close Word.
  2. Hold down the Alt key while clicking on the Go menu and choosing Library. The hidden Library folder opens.
  3. Open the Word templates folder. For Office 2016 for Mac, that is Users/YourUserName/Library/Group Containers/UBF8T346G9.Office/User Content/Templates. Rename Normal.dotm to Normal.dotmOLD, but keep the window open.
  4. Open Word again. A new blank document is created that has the default formatting for your version of Word.
  5. When you're finished checking, close Word, delete any new Normal.dotm that Word may have created, rename Normal.dotmOLD back to Normal.dotm and close the window.

The 'original' specification for indentation is that text is flush with the margin, or zero.

Right click the media player object and choose Properties.

In the properties dialog box, click Custom, then click the "..." at the right of the Custom property.

This opens another dialog box; on its General tab, you'd find an AutoStart checkbox, which'll be checked by default.  Uncheck it.

OK your way out and save the file.  The video should no longer auto-play when opened.

bob jones aka: cybertaz say:

Themes have nothing to do with Styles in the manner you seem to think :-) This is especially true in Mac Word & it's not surprising that tier 1 outsourced "Support Engineers" who don't use the product are confused.

The first point to clarify is that Themes do not 'contain' Styles. Themes are nothing more than a pair of specified Theme Fonts: one for Headings & one for Body Text, plus a set of 10 Theme Colors. These are used as the 'defaults' in a new document because they have an impact on built-in Styles as well as user-defined Styles based on them. The font of the text & colors applied will change when a different Theme is selected provided that the content has not been reformatted in a manner that deviates from fonts or colors called for by the currently applied Theme. Also, Themes have no influence on character attributes such as font size, bold, italic, etc. or any type of Paragraph Formatting.

I would suggest that you not even give any consideration to Themes. Their primary intent is to provide consistent use of specific fonts & colors in Office [not just Word] documents. The problem is that unless you have the complete array of Protection tools provided in Win Word, Themes really just don't insure anything. Even then, they often cause more issues than they resolve - and this is coming from someone who is far less anti-Theme than most... Just wait to see what he has to say if John McGhie comes along :-)

There are a number of other distinctions as well, but the bottom line is that it sounds like what you actually need to do is create a Template file on which new documents are to be based. Concentrate on the Styles you wish to have used... They are the most important consideration. Be sure to check the Add to template box in the Style definition dialog. That will insure that your Styles are stored in your template & will be available in any new documents generated from it.
