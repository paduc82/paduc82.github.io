Apparently a lot of people thought it looked (sort of) like a pizza (it's round, and it has many colored items sprinkled on it), as the term caught on quickly. Although it is important, when you are describing a problem or explaining a solution, to know what Microsoft calls things (the difference between a "radio button" and a "check box," between a "menu" and a "toolbar"), people who work with Microsoft products a lot often take a somewhat irreverent approach to them.

 

When the Ribbon interface was first introduced, the subdivisions of each menu tab that are now called "groups" were referred to as "chunks." What is now the Mini Toolbar was the Minibar. Some of us may think the developers' original terms are more appealing, but when marketing gets involved, and especially when terms have to be translated into myriad languages, then names become more bland.

 

One of the real difficulties of working with Word is that Microsoft (and the computer world in general) has a tendency to appropriate ordinary words and give them a Word-specific meaning so that they become "terms of art." Some examples are "template," which is easily enough understood in context, and "document." More difficult is Microsoft's use of "section," which may mean something different from, or at least more limited than, what you mean by it; if you describe a portion or part of your document as a "section," we will assume that you mean it is separated from the rest by section breaks. And many users confuse headers and headings, footers and footnotes, making it often difficult to assess the problems they are attempting to describe.

teylyn say:

Hello,

does the second value, i.e. the 21% contain the number 0.21 that is formatted as a percentage? In that case, the percent sign won't be picked up as text. You can use the Cell function with the "format" option. If the cell is formatted as a percentage, the result will start with the letter P.  

IF(SEARCH("P",CELL("format",B10)),"percent","")

If the cell does not contain a number, this function will return an error, so you may want to wrap it in IFerror

=IFERROR(IF(SEARCH("P",CELL("format",B10)),"percent",""),"")

This alone will not pick up the % sign that may exist in text. For that, you need 

IF(SEARCH("%",B10&""),"percent","")

Again, wrap in IfError to mask errors. Then append the two formulas. Only one of them will return "percent" and in some cases both will just return blanks.

=IFERROR(IF(SEARCH("P",CELL("format",B10)),"percent",""),"")&IFERROR(IF(SEARCH("%",B10&""),"percent",""),"")

For Phrase use

=IFERROR(IF(SEARCH(" ",B5),"Phrase",""),"")

The combo of numbers is difficult to determine.  The value in your data could be stored as a number and formatted as time. If that is what you mean with "combo of numbers" then you can use

=IF(ISNUMBER(B5),"numerical","")

But this will not pick up "21 percent" as a number. That is text and a phrase with a space.

And for the Title you need to use four " signs if you want to search for a " sign. You can use

=IFERROR(IF(SEARCH("""",B5),"Title",""),"")
