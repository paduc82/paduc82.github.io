AFAIK, there is no general method for estimating the "guess" parameter that works in all cases.

(Hmm, I wonder if MIRR would be suitable "guess".  I have not tried it myself.)

First, note there are cash flows for which the IRR method (not just the Excel implementation) does not work.

Second, note that there are cash flows for which the Excel implementation of the IRR method does not work.  (That might be less true in Excel 2010, which re-engineered the IRR implementation to some degree, according to Microsoft.)

Finally, where or not a "guess" parameter works depends on the nature of the cash flows.

It is both easy and hard to explain how the "guess" parameter influences the behavior of the Excel IRR implementation.  The ease of explanation depends, in part, on your mathematical background and your ability to gronk mathematical algorithms.

To start, you might take a look at the wiki pages for the IRR method [click here] and for the Newton-Raphson and Secant methods of approximation [click here and click here] [1].

To over-simplify, one approach to determining IRR is to guess what it is, then use that guess to compute the NPV.  If the NPV is "close" to zero, we are done.  Otherwise, choose a new guess that is between the current guess and some boundary; then repeat the process.  (The initial boundaries might be based on the initial guess as well.)

One of the problems with the Excel implementation is that it has a fairly small arbitrary limit on the number of iterations before giving up.  That limit is 20, according to the IRR help page.

Another problem with the Excel implemenation is that it is not capable of restarting with a different guess automatically if the initial guess does not seem to converge on a reasonable answer.  I suspect that (even) the HP-12C calculator, which requires no guess, does exactly that.

Nevertheless, again, there are cash flows for which no IRR implementation can find a solution.

EA wrote:
I created a GUESS forumla basically calculates net cash flow growth rate between period.

=IRR($E$87:F87,SUM($E$87+F87)/$E$85)

E87 = (year 0 flow + f87( year 1 flow))/year 0 flow 


Aside....

You do not need SUM in that formula.  ($E$87+F87)/$E$85 [sic] would suffice.

Also, you do not need IRR for those particular parameters.  Since there are only 2 data points, the IRR (CAGR) [2] is simply -F87/$E$87-1.  The unary negative is needed because one of E87 and F87 should be negative.

(Is there a typo in your formula, and you meant to write $E$87 in the denominator?  That would make more sense; in fact, then it is simply the formula in my second paragraph above.)

But I believe that I tried ($E$87+Fx)/$E$85 myself in the past, where "Fx" is the cash flow corresponding to the latest period.  That computes the CAGR, which seems logical.  However, it did not work very well for me as a "guess" in some/many circumstances.

If it works well enough for you, so be it.


-----
[1] http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_rate_of_return
     http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_method
     http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secant_method

Note:  Microsoft documentation claims that Newton-Raphson is used.  However, some details of the algorithm in that documentation sound more like the Secant method.  Moreover, my own Newton-Raphson implementation often has different results than the Excel IRR function.  However, those differences might be explained by details of the implementation.

[2] Note that some references translate CAGR into compound annual growth rate.  But compound average growth rate or geometric average is the correct translation, since whether or not it is annual, monthly or whatever depends on the definition of the period.

I'm mostly going to provide some basic clues as to why such things might happen, as well as why things in forums or support work as they do so you can stop assuming the world's out to get you personally.

Before I proceed though, I will mention that other than the 3rd-party AV issues you already mentioned, malware can be involved in such issues, I'm just going to assume this isn't your problem and focus on other possible causes based on your post.

Note that these community forums began auto-locking all threads over a certain age, roughly 90 days if I recall correctly, over a year ago.  That's all threads, so it isn't targeted at those with this subject, though tagging on to old threads is almost always a bad idea, since the specifics of a new problem are often different even though people don't see it that way.  Opening a new thread as you have was the correct and best action to take.

As for telling you to try things you've already done, support groups always provide complete instruction sets, since leaving  out steps leads to errors.  In some cases these need to be done in sequence even if you've tried them before, but even if not, the helpers in these public forums must assume someone else [like you] might review them out of context in the future, so they include all steps in order for that reason.

As with most consumers who don't know how to interact with support, you've included some spurious additional information about past failures.  These indicate an inability to focus and show helpers here that you're unlikely to properly follow instructions, which reduces your chances of gaining aid from the most valuable volunteers who might truly be able to help.

As I stated above I'll just attempt to help you understand why your attitude towards support of Windows 10 may be part of your problem and what I can see in your description that might have contributed to the cause of your current issues.

First you need to understand how Windows 10 is different from previous versions as discussed in depth in this Overview of Windows as a service.  That article is a long, involved description for trained IT experts, so I'll just highlight the important points.  With Windows 10, there are two release types: feature updates that add new functionality twice per year around March and September, and quality updates that provide security and reliability fixes at least once a month.

The reason I bring this up is that Microsoft has recently begun rolling out the fall Windows 10 version 1809 update, which had been delayed a few months due to significant issues in early testing.  Since I received the 1809 update just last week on a 10-year old Windows 7 desktop upgraded to Windows 10 in July 2016, it's being deployed widely on older systems now, so likely yours as well.

What most don't realize is that the twice annual feature updates are roughly similar to the old Windows Service Packs in size and complexity, while the monthly quality updates are mostly just roll-ups of the security and other minor updates that used to be released separately.  This means these feature updates are relatively large and take significant time and resources to install, especially on older systems like mine.  The version 1809 update took about 3 hours.

What I've learned is that though the installation processes indicate they can be performed while using the PC and also seem to tell you when it's OK to restart the system after installation is completed, it's best to handle these more like the Service Packs of the past.  In other words, wait to install until you're done with the PC for the day, then let the process download and install, including waiting extra time (I allow 10 or 20 minutes) at the end for the system to settle before restarting.  Then once the system completes the installation processes after the restart, I wait another 20-30 minutes before logging in and then perform an additional restart even if this isn't requested by the system.

The reason for all this extra handling is that though it's got a quad-core processor with 8GB RAM and plenty of free space on a 1TB HDD, this system is old and slow by today's standards, so it isn't always truly done with post-installation tasks when it appears to be.  Part of this is due to the fast login sequence added in Windows 10 and another is that various checks occur after the reboot to confirm everything completed successfully, which if I rush to use the system or reboot might be aborted and need to start again.

The reason all of this is important is that the most common complaints I hear about update failures and possible corruption are typically from those who assume things are done as soon as Windows tells them it is and tend to rush to the next step like they did with Service Packs on earlier systems.  The difference with Windows 10 is that much of the update intelligence is located in the cloud update services rather than on the individual PC, so these after update processes take significant time performing checks to confirm and if necessary repair or reinstall modules that may have failed to complete without error.

What this means to you is that situations like a partially failed Windows Defender update might eventually manage to repair itself, but by making random changes and rebooting, you're actually interfering with the automatic cloud processes trying to do the same with more true knowledge (via telemetry and the cloud's artificial intelligence) of items like that CBS.log file few truly understand.

To all of this I'll add another complication you threw into the mix, by installing that language pack somewhere in the middle of whatever Windows updates were actually occurring on your own system.  Though I couldn't find a clear statement, I've heard for years that adding language packs after the initial Windows installation can be fraught with issues, since there are dependencies that in the Windows 8.1 era required that all security and other updates be reinstalled after a language pack was added.

I don't know if that's still required with Windows 10, but found enough threads in the community and online articles that I suspect it's still a bad idea to add one later.  In fact here's some notes from a Microsoft Docs article titled Add languages to Windows images.

  • Language components must match the version of Windows. For example, you can't add a Windows 10, version 1809 language pack to Windows 10, version 1803.

If you brief that article including sections like that titled Considerations, you'll find this as well.

Install languages, then updates and apps. If you're adding languages to an image that already contains apps or updates (for example, servicing stack updates (SSU) or cumulative updates (CU), reinstall the apps and updates.

So it does appear that recommendation to reinstall apps and updates after adding a language pack holds true with Windows 10.

So what I'm trying to point out here is that along with the potential issues created by attempting to rush the normal Windows 10 servicing update processes, throwing a language pack into the mix has very possibly badly confused the update sequencing on that PC, forcing it to revert to reinstalling nearly all monthly quality and other updates performed since whatever original upgrade or initial version was installed on the system.

Yes, this explanation was long and complex, but so is Windows 10 as a service, though Microsoft tries to hide that from the user.  What I find from watching those posting in these and other forums though, is that those used to manually performing updates on older Windows versions often get themselves in trouble by trying to treat Windows 10 the same way, while in truth this is exactly like two different "experts" trying to fix the PC at the same time.

The issue though is that the two performing the fixes can't see each other and it's like one is at the PC console and the other operating remotely on a hidden screen, performing changes at the same time, each oblivious to what the other is doing.

So my recommendation?  Leave the PC on (disable sleep mode) for 24 hours and see what's happened in that time frame.  Maybe reboot after logging in to check that a Windows update isn't in progress to complete any processes that previous updates might still require.

The most important point with Windows 10 SaaS though is to learn patience, since rushing anything or trying to fix something that Windows can likely repair itself will only interfere, not aid in a solution as it did in the past.

If this doesn't work, then the backup and refresh Windows technique you mentioned may be your only recourse, but if you choose this path, I'd first do some research regarding when to best add that language pack while performing this sequence in order to make most efficient use of your time in rebuilding.

rob koch say:

The Windows 10 version 1809 update added a dark theme to File Explorer, so there's a potential for issues with certain color schemes that might cause icons to apparently disappear when in truth they're just the same color as the background.  There's also the potential that the icon simply became toggled off, or confused, so multiple online articles suggest toggling it of and on again to see if that brings it back.

Note that articles like the above might apply any time a significant update to Windows occurs, so yes, some do apply even if much time has elapsed or even on different Windows versions.  However, since the Windows 10 interface has gone through multiple significant revisions since the initial release and changes like the dark theme have occurred during specific updates, the things that fixed them in one revision may not be that same each time.

My point was never to try and fix all of your problems, only to make certain you were aware that you might be fighting Windows update itself by making random changes, so I'd concentrate on an individual item like the icon and try to search for the most current articles available to see if they're known as a problem with the specific update recently installed.

It sounds from what you've mentioned so far that your current problems may be less critical cosmetic issues, so at this point I'd try dealing with what you feel is most important first.

Using System Restore is akin to ripping out portions of the operating system or apps installed sine the date selected, so it typically does more harm than good with a software-as-a-service sequenced update system like Windows 10.  For example, we'd seen problems caused by System Restore with Microsoft Security Essentials and its update system even back on Windows XP, since this was one of the first time and sequence sensitive apps to be created.  Typically it could take a few days for MSE to recover from reverting to a prior date with System Restore, since this jerked out portions of the sequencing data the program maintained to insure the integrity of definitions and engine updates.

Doing a System Restore with Windows 10 just multiplies this problem 10-fold, since the entire operating system and app structure is now maintained with the same sort of sequencing data as that pioneered in MSE and Defender.

System Restore was designed back in the static file based operating system days of Windows 9x and though it dealt with those older version somewhat better, was always intended as a catastrophic recovery tool when all else had failed.  I personally used it only once when trying to recover enough control of a Windows Vista system, badly damaged by malware, in order to back up data before wiping the system to upgrade it to Windows 7.
