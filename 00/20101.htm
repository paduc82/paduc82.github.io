joeu2004 say: JOE wrote:

Can you explain why = 5108.51 - 4232.48 does not equal 876.03?


Excel uses 64-bit binary floating-point to represent numbers.  Numbers are represented by the sum of 53 consecutive powers of 2.  Consequently, most non-integers (and integers large than 2^53) cannot be represented exactly.

For example, try to represent 0.1 (1/10) with the sum of powes of 2:  1/16 + 1/32 + 1/256 + .....  We start with 1/16, the largest power of 2 less than 1/10.  We skip 1/64 and 1/128 because they would cause the sum to exceed 1/10.  (Although that might be necessary in actual practice.)

In fact, there is no sum of powers of 2 that equals 1/10 exactly.  And when we are limited to 53 consecutive powers of 2 starting with 1/16, the resulting sum is 0.100000000000000,0055511151231257827021181583404541015625.

For your example:

5108.51 =               5108.51000000000,0218278728425502777099609375
4232.48 =               4232.47999999999,956344254314899444580078125
5108.51 - 4232.48 =   876.030000000000,654836185276508331298828125
But 876.03 =              876.029999999999,972715158946812152862548828125

As Jim wrote, the way to avoid these infinitesimal differences is to explicitly round all arithmetic that uses non-integer and division of integers.

JOE wrote:

for MS to require me to use the round function on ALL my formulas (like adding two numbers with a decimal place to only two digets) to ensure that my functions can add or subtract correctly is appauling.


An alternative is to set the Precision As Displayed calculation option (PAD).  In Excel 2010, click on File, Options, Advanced and page down to the section titled "When calculating this workbook".

However, I do not recommend PAD for many reasons.  Among them are....

PAD does not solve all problems.  For example, IF(10.1-10=0.1,TRUE) returns FALSE, even if PAD is set.  The remedy again is:  IF(ROUND(10.1-10,2)=0.1,TRUE).

Also, the effect of PAD is pervasive:  it affects all cells in every worksheet of the workbook.

Consequently, for example, if you calculate a daily interest rate by dividing an annual rate by 365, but you format to 4 percentage decimal places for aesthetic reasons, PAD will round the daily rate to 4 percentage decimal places, which might adversely affect subsequent calculations.
