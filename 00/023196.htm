Hi Matt,

Websites like msn, weather.com and others use your geo location or your website profile to store your location details and then provide you with the relevant weather information that is available (you may live in an area where there is no official forecast and the forecast for the region is given instead. The accuracy of you (calculated) geo position depends on a number of settings on your Windows 10 computer.

Some desktop computers include satellite positioning devices which can calculate your position to an accuracy of meters. Computers without satellite positioning can use third-party services like google or your phone company or your ISP's data to obtain your street address or approximate your actual geo position to an accuracy of only of a few hundred meters, but only if you give your consent.

From your description it sounds like when you visit msn.com you have not been logged in automatically or your msn profile does not include an accurate geo-location or zip code or street address or you have disabled geolocation on your windows 10 computer or there is no forecast for your location and an adjacent location which has an official weather forecast is used . However, I am pretty sure that MSN websites only use an approximate location (accurate to 100's of yards) to find a weather forecast for your county/suburb. If you want a real-time localized weather information, have a look out the window or tune in to your local tv station. For official commercial weather forecasts use a specialized website like accuweather or noaa (available as windows store apps).

So... the answer to your question about the accuracy of your geolocation on the MSN websites is 'its not a problem'. Unless you have given permission for a website to use an accurate (satellite) geo-positioning, your exact geo-positioning (accurate to 10's of yards) is not available to websites (unless you have given it permission to use a geolocation service (like satellite, or google street maps or bing maps). For weather forecasts your exact position (to an accuracy of yards) is a non issue.

There is also a setting for 'Send do not track requests to sites you visit in Internet Explorer' in IE and other web browsers (Edge, chrome etc), which web sites are supposed to honor, which is supposed to tell websites that your geolocation should not be used (or tracked), but the effectiveness of that setting depends on how a website has been programmed. Banking websites for example, may track your approximate geolocation obtained from third party services (like your ISP) or from your street address and IP address in order to detect fraudulent activity.
