Why aren't Word and PowerPoint written to take advantage of - Microsoft Community

byte99 say:

Why aren't Word and PowerPoint written to take advantage of multiple cores?

Most of the increase in processing power over the past decade has come from an increase in the number of cores, rather than power per core. Given that large, complex documents can cause noticeable lag in both Word and PowerPoint, there's clearly a need to enable these programs to take advantage of more processing power.  

Given, this why hasn't Microsoft re-coded these two key pieces of software to run in parallel on multiple cores?*

I understand this isn't an easy problem.  Nevertheless, MS is a a large company with substantial resources and expertise, and these are among its signature products.

[*It's possible Word for Windows NT may have run on multiple cores, but I've not seen any official statement on this from MS.]

Not a practical solution for me, since (having worked extensively in both Mac and Windows) I personally much prefer the Mac environment, and don't want to take on the added inconvenience of switching to Bootcamp or another computer, or the added complexity of implementing a VM, just to use Word-for-Windows (I've used a VM in the past).

....on Windows, which is the operating system for which they were originally designed.

Not sure about that.  As I understand it, Word-for-Mac, while designed to have approximate feature parity with Word-for-Windows, is separate software, designed specifically for the Mac.  MS's first version of Word-for-MaC was written specifically for the Mac platform, in 1985 (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Word#Word_for_Mac):

"The Mac was introduced January 24, 1984 and Microsoft introduced Word 1.0 for Mac a year later, January 18, 1985. The DOS, Mac, and Windows versions are quite different from each other. Only the Mac version was WYSIWYG and used a Graphical User Interface, far ahead of the other platforms."

Development has continued ever since (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Productivity_Experience_Group):

"...after releasing Office 97 for Windows, Microsoft decided, in January 1997, to form a separate Macintosh Business Unit to focus on creating applications optimized for the Macintosh platform. In August 1997, as part of a broader partnership agreement with Apple, Microsoft committed to continue developing and supporting Macintosh software for at least 5 more years, renewing this pledge on January 10, 2006"

It's only recently that they've merged code bases: 

https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2018/01/office-for-mac-finally-has-real-time-collaboration-in-16-9-0-update/

So the discrepancy in performance raises, for me, a separate software design question:  Office for Mac is an important product for both MS and Apple (I suspect that the Mac would not have its current market penetration without the ability to run Office).  Thus it's clearly in the interest of both companies to have this software work well.  Given the decades of experience MS has developing this product, and the support Apple has presumably given MS to support its development, why can't they fix its basic performance and stability issues?
