brian tillman [outlook mvp 2007-2019] say:

> My service provider has changed my POP account into IMAC account.  In the process, they wipe out

> half of my file reducing it from 10GB to 5GB and changing it from .pst into .ost.

IMAP, not IMAC.

Your service provider cannot touch anything you might have in a PST. As Hal said, the folders in a PST are local to your PC. And you can't really copy a PST to an OST, as what I said may seem to imply. What you can do is open the PST in Outlook so you can see its folders and the folders in the IMAP account and move messages around as you see fit so that they're in the IMAP folders or the local folders; whatever works best for you.

PSTs and OSTs are essentially the same type of file, but they're used for different purposes. PSTs contrain Outlook folders that reside only on your PC. OSTs contain folders that are copies of what's in your mailbox on the mail server. Neither PST nor OST is of any value except when opened in Outlook. hey're not human-readable files. The folders in the OST will always reflect what's on the mail service. PSTs can contain any messages you want them to contain, regardless of whether or not they're on the server.

Listen to Palcouk about the size of your mailbox. Clearly you can't copy more into the IMAP folders that your mail provider allows. You can keep much more in PST-based folders. Keep in mind, though, if you lose the PST because you haven't made any copies of it, the messages in it are gone for good. If you were to lose the OST, that's no big deal because Outlook will simply create it again when it accesses the mail server.

> The endings .pst and .ost are simply describing where these files are stored (or kept).

That's not what I said. The different file types mean that they're for different purposes. OSTs are used for account types that leave messages on the server by design (IMAP, Exchange ActiveSync, and Exchange). PSTs are used for local storage of items and for holding messages for POP accounts. You certainly can have OSTs and PSTs in the same Windows folder, although it's not necessary.

Sub Test()
  Dim Dest As Range
  'Refer to the last filled cell in 1st sheet in Master.xlsm
  Set Dest = Workbooks("Master.xlsm").Sheets(1).Cells.Find("*", Searchdirection:=xlPrevious)
  'Refer to next row, column A
  Set Dest = Dest.Offset(1, 1 - Dest.Column)
  'Copy the cells in the active sheet
  Range("C16:C35,C37:C45,C48:C49").Copy
  'Paste and transpose into Master.xlsm
  Dest.PasteSpecial xlPasteValues, Transpose:=True
End Sub
