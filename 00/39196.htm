arminatorx say:

What is the best tv for use with an XBOX One X in the $500 range and 65‚Äù?

what frame rate and refresh rate and other things should I be looking for ?

The "best" mainly depends on your priorities. 

As for framerate and refresh rate: if you're looking for actual TV's, not dedicated gaming monitors (which don't come in 65'' sizes AFAIK), do NOT buy anything according to it's refresh/frame rate. 

Since regular TV broadcasts are always sent out with 60 or 50Hz depending on your country, most (if not all) TV makers produce their TV receivers to accept 50/60 Hz, and then they interpolate (i.e. "making up") additional images, so they can advertise their TVs having "more Hertz" than a competitor. 

That frame "smoothing" might work for non-interactive stuff like actual TV broadcasts, but is a disadvantage for gaming!

Unless the console detects a "FreeSync" capable display on it's HDMI port, the console will only output it's video with 60Hz during gameplay. And I've yet to see an actual TV with "FreeSync" capabilities. Usually only computer gaming monitors have FreeSync, but they don't come in your desired screen size.

So you have a console that outputs "only" 60Hz, like any standard TV broadcast, and the TV has to receive and store several frames to be able to determine what changed from frame to frame, and then calculate the interpolated images to "pump up" the 60 console frames per second to the 120 or 240 or 600 Hz refresh rate, meaning you will see images from several milliseconds "in the past" on your screen. 

For example: 

In Black Ops 4, you are standing head to head with an opponent. 

The other person pulls the trigger and shoots. The console will render the muzzle flash, and will send the image to your TV. 

The TV stores the image with the muzzle flash, and stores it, waiting for the next 2 or 10 frames. While the TV is storing the images from the console, the bullet is flying towards you, and the console registers that you're hit. 

In the mean time, the TV then calculates the interpolated images from the muzzle fire animation, and finally displays it on the TV. 

By the time you actually see the muzzle fire on your 600Hz TV, you're already dead. 

For gaming, especially competitive multiplayer games like Black Ops 4, make sure that your TV has a dedicated "Gaming Mode", that will turn off all image enhancements and will display your console video feed as quickly and immediately as possible. 

So please go to your nearest TV/HiFi/electronics store, and look at their selection of 65'' TVs in your price range. 

Ask the personell specifically if the TV has a dedicated "Game" mode, or check the manuals in the support section of the TV manufacturer.

Since you mention you have the Xbox One X:

For $550 you won't get those fancy OLED or Quantum Dot LED TVs in that size which support HDR. 

So if it's only the size that matters to you, get a good quality 1080p TV in that size which might cost even less than $500, and save up for later, when the prices for 4K OLED or Quantum Dot TVs with HDR10/Dolby Vision support have dropped to your desired price range. 

Even with "only" 1080p, the Xbox One X will internally render a game up to 4K (and sometimes even above), and then downscale the image to 1080p, which will make the image appear smoother than a natively rendered 1080p image.

Hi,

You will have to configure Windows Display adaptor to extend your desktop:

Right-click the desktop, and then click Properties.
In the DisplayProperties dialog box, click the Settings tab.
Click the Display list and select your external monitor. If you do not see multiple monitors listed, your computer hardware may not support Dualview. You can still use MaxiVista to configure a second computer as an additional display, however.
