atomtech say:

ANSWER AND SUMMATION  (CAUSE, EXAMPLE & FIX):

The CAUSE:

Microsoft Publisher 2010 (not earlier versions; I know, because I tested it), considers the size/resolution of your images to be based **ONLY** on what the images embedded dimensions are in **INCHES.**

 

The EXAMPLE:

With my 12MP digital camera, I took a shot that was a 4MB size file, 2848x4272px (72px/in resolution) and showed dimensions of 39in x 59in.  Using Adobe Photoshop, I rotated the image 1 degree and then--to crop out the blank edges it creates--I used the "crop" tool and set the RELATIVE dimensions to 4x6" (because that conformed with the same relative dimensions of the original photograph).  The result was the same picture and same quality (meaning it could still be blown up to about 39x59in), BUT its stats had been adjusted to this:  2773x4160px (693px/in resolution) and showed the size to be only 4x6" now!  Other than Publisher 2010, the image is treated with the quality of a 39inx59in image.

 

The QUICK FIX:

1.  RE-OPEN your image in Adobe Photoshop (or like program if it has the same type of cropping functionality).

2.  RE-CROP the image using the dimensions HxW that it originally had (or near to it), such as 39x59" in my case.

DONE!; Now...

3.  SAVE the image.  It will now show the image size as 39x59" (in my example), and the resolution will return to near normal (at about 68px/in in my case).  The detail of the image won't change.

4.  BRING it into Publisher 2010 and it will be treated exactly how you need it!

harry stevens say:

Hi,

There are reasons as to why you are asked for a code:

If you encounter the message "Help us protect your account", you are accessing your Account Settings. If you see this every time you login on any other service, then your Two-step Verification is enabled.

However, by turning on "I sign in frequently on this device", you are setting up a "Trusted Device" that should not be asked for a code when accessing your Account Settings or when your Two-step Verification enabled on the specific computer or device you are logging in.

If you encounter the message

"Verify your account, we've detected something unusual about this sign-in".

This is a security check that your login was not recognized by the system as your usual 'sign-in', as your logins are tracked automatically. If you logged in from an unfamiliar device, or a location 300 miles away from where you usually sign in, you'll encounter this message. But this message should not really show up as well if you are using a Trusted Device.

But, there are some block messages that WILL still prompt you for a code.

If you encounter the message

"It looks like someone else might be using your account".

This is a much more 'severe' issue - the system thinks that a hacker was able to get into your account, and now it had blocked access to it until the true owner can verify the ownership of the account. This will always show, regardless of having Trusted Device or not.

There may be other factors that affect this as well. If your browser automatically clears out all cookies/cache after each session, or you are using an internet proxy (It can trigger the security checks), or you use the browser's InPrivate/Private Browsing/Incognito Modes, the Trusted Device will not be recognized, and therefore you'll be asked for a code again. So take note of that.

IMPORTANT NOTE: Xbox sign-ins may fail, as well, as a result of service host separation. To fix this issue:

Ostensibly, interest is (curPayDate - lastPayDate)*dailyIntRate, where dailyIntRate is annualRate/365.  Principal is payment - interest; and balance is prevBalance - principal.

prevBalance*(curPayDate - lastPayDate)*dailyIntRate, of course.

As for "other  fees as they come up", I do not believe they should not affect the amortization schedule per se.  They may or may not be considered finance charges for tax purposes.   But I believe they are not considered interest per se; and they do not affect the loan balance, unless the terms of the loan allow for such fees to be compounded, which I believe is unusual.

Are you a lender, loan consumer, or is this for a class assignment?

The answer affects just how precise you need to be.

If you are lender, you will want to follow correct practices.  The rules that you describe are not how interest for professional loans is usually calculated, as I understand it.  Of course, my understanding might be incorrect; and it might vary from lender to lender and with the practices in one country to another.

I believe that generally,  "early" payments -- i.e. payments made before the due date -- are effectively held until the due date.  So they do not reduce interest.

On the other hand, additional  payments -- i.e. payments in excess of the amount required by the amortization schedule -- do reduce the balance.  But they are figured as if the additional payments arrive on the next due date.

As for late payments, some/many/most lenders allow for a grace period.  So even if a payment is late, it might be treated as paid on the due date.  That is, it might not increase interest.

These details complicate the formulas significantly, which is why I said "ostensibly".

I am not an attorney; I don't even play one on TV.  And this is not the place to offer legal advice, which I am not doing anyway, of course ;-).  So I will bite my tongue and say only that I think you are wise to "keep things simple".

Having managed private loans with very similar terms, I can tell you what I did.  Early payments were treated as arriving on the due date.  Late payments received any time in the due period were treated as arriving on the due date for that period.  The late fee was assessed if the full payment due was not received by the late-fee deadline.  Late fees accumulated separately from the loan balance, without interest since the terms of the loan failed to say anything about that.  Any subsequent payment went first toward paying down the late-fee accumulation.

If late payments were made after the due period, I would offer the borrower the choice of a catch-up payment that would put the loan back on track, a new loan agreement (in the case of many missed periods), or pay off the loan including accrued interest and late fees.  (And a 4th choice for me:  write off the loan as bad debt.)

In any case, there was no need to compute interest on a daily basis.
