anthonyst. jean say:

Retrieve old Hotmail email messages

All of my previous hotmail email messages are missing with the new oultlook.com.  How can they be retrieved?

a. user say:

Hi,

 

Please verify if you created a new outlook.com account name or you renamed your old account to outlook.com account name. If you renamed your account, it causes your sign in name to change from your existing @hotmail.com address to your new @outlook.com address. Your existing address becomes an alias of your new account so that mail delivery is uninterrupted.  After renaming your account you will sign in with your new account to all services you previously accessed with your old account.

 

By upgrading to Outlook.com, you are only upgrading the user interface of Hotmail to the new user interface; hence all your emails should still stay in your Inbox. However, if your emails were over 270 days old, you may have lost them due to inactivity in account even before you renamed the account. If you had Self-recovery of emails turned on in your account before rename, you can try to recover some of the deleted messages yourself (Link to recover deleted messages is under DELETED folder), refer:

Deleted email messages.

 

If you chose to rename your account to @Outlook.com, a prompt would have asked you – ‘Where do you want to save your emails?’ You will have the option to create a new folder for your old account or save the emails in the Inbox of your renamed account.

 

If you chose to create a new folder, the default name of the folder will be the name of your old account and the folder will appear in the left pane under ‘Folders’. And all your old emails will be stored in this folder.

john w. vinson say:

Karl's suggestion of not moving the records but instead marking them "Archived" is probably "best practice" here.

If you DO decide to move, then you should certainly NOT assign any meaning to the autonumber; you have no control over the autonumber value other than the starting seed, and as time goes on you have no way to prevent collisions. If (and, again, it's not best practice!) you want to keep the archive in a separate table, it should have a Long Integer primary key (not an autonumber), and it should be filled using an Append query appending the autonumber value into the long integer key field to preserve its value. I'd say this is essential if you have any data in related tables.

ken sheridan say: The only cogent reason why you might  have separate tables would be if CurrentEmployees and ArchiveEmployees are sub-types of the type Employees.  This would require three tables, however, not two as you'd need an Employees table to model the (super) type as well as tables to model the two sub-types.

A sub-type is characterized but its sharing all the attributes of its (super) type, but not those of other sub-types.  So the type Employees would have attributes such as FirstName, LastName DateOfBirth etc which are common to both current and archived employees, but CurrentEmployees would have attributes which are those of current employees, but not of archived (by which I assume you mean ex) employees).  Salary might be such an attribute.  Conversely, ArchiveEmployees would have attributes relevant only to ex employees, e.g. DateQuit.

A type and sub-type are related one-to-one, so all three tables would have a primary key EmployeeID, but those of CurrentEmployees and ArchiveEmployees would also be foreign keys referencing the key of Employees.  That of Employees can be an autonumber, but not those of CurrentEmployees and ArchiveEmployees, which would each be a straightforward long integer number data type.

If there are significant differences in the attributes of current and archived employees then it might well be appropriate to model them in this way, but if not then a single table as Karl suggests would be a better option, either having a Boolean Archived column as an attribute of employees, or simply defining archived employees by virtue of their having a non-Null DateQuit attribute.
Ken Sheridan, Stafford, England
