Function MySum(R)
   Dim dblTot As Double
   Dim ws As Worksheet
   Application.Volatile
   For Each ws In ThisWorkbook.Worksheets
      Select Case ws.Name

           dblTot = dblTot + ws.Range(R)

hansv mvp say:

The keyword And must be inside the quotes. Literal date values must be formatted as mm/dd/yyyy or yyyy/mm/dd and enclosed in # characters. Finally, acDialog is the 6th argument, not the 5th. So use

 

    DoCmd.OpenForm "frmEmployeeTotalWorkHour", , , "EmployeeID =" & Me.EmployeeID & _
        " And ProjectDate = #" & Format(Me.ProjectDate, "yyyy/mm/dd") & "#", , acDialog

graham mandeno say:

Miscounting commas can pose a bit of a hazard with methods like OpenForm which have many optional arguments.  A good way to ensure you don't get arguments in the wrong place is to use named arguments.  You can see the names of the arguments in the intellisense as you type the command.  In this case, the arguments are named WhereCondition and WindowMode.


Also, you may find it convenient to include the delimiting # signs as part of the Format string:


DoCmd.OpenForm "frmEmployeeTotalWorkHour", _

    WhereCondition:="EmployeeID =" & Me.EmployeeID & _
        " And ProjectDate = " & Format(Me.ProjectDate, "\#yyyy-mm-dd\#"), _

    WindowMode:=acDialog


Best wishes,

Graham

scottgem say:
There are three problems as indicated by your responses. First your concatenation is in correct. The AND needs to be part of the literal string and enclosed in the quotes. The second is that you are using a date value which needs to be delimited by octothorpes (#). You don't, however, need to format it, if the control is formatted as a Date value. And you are missing a comma. 

So this should also work:

DoCmd.OpenForm "frmEmployeeTotalWorkHour", , , "EmployeeID =" & Me.EmployeeID & " And  ProjectDate = #" & Me.ProjectDate & "#",, acDialog

graham mandeno say:

Hi Scott


I disagree with your statement that "you don't need to format it".  If you allow VBA to automatically convert a date to a string, it is formatted according to the computer's regional settings.  If the regional date format is anything other than mm/dd/yyyy or yyyy-mm-dd then the SQL will fail or, worse, produce a valid date which gives incorrect results.


For example, if I type this into the immediate window:

    ?"ProjectDate = #" & Date() & "#"

then I get this:

    ProjectDate = #12/11/2013#


In a SQL WHERE clause, this will filter on 11 December 2013, not 12 November 2013 (today).


I find it convenient to use three functions: SQLDate, SQLTime, and SQLDateTime, each of which takes a date as an argument and formats in the appropriate way for SQL, complete with octothorpe/hash/pound signs.


My version of the OP's OpenForm call would then be:


DoCmd.OpenForm "frmEmployeeTotalWorkHour", _

    WhereCondition:="EmployeeID =" & Me.EmployeeID & _
        " and ProjectDate = " & SQLDate(Me.ProjectDate), _

    WindowMode:=acDialog


Cheers,

Graham
