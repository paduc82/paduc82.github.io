Flash memory and fragmentation

While I understand that files on flash media can physically become fragmented, I have always been under the impression that this is of no real consequence as it would be for a standard platter-based mechanical hard drive.

 

My logical reasoning is through technical knowledge of how mechanical hard drives operate, with excessive movement of the read head causing increased latency in reading the requested data, especially if the heads are jumping back and forth from inner tracks to outer ones (or in other words, files that have fragments at the beginning of the disk and near the end of the disk). Simply put, the more fragmented a file, the more movement is required by the read heads (and after a seek to the correct track, there is additional wasted time as the head must wait until the sector(s) to be read complete the remainder of their rotation, until the data needed is positioned under the read head) -- all in all, the point that I'm trying to convey is that this can cause significant delay in reading a heavily fragmented file or files.

 

On the other hand, flash media lacks these inherent flaws of mechanical, moving parts as I just described... therefore their access time to read (or the time to write) should theoretically be the same, no matter where the data is located on the media (as I understood). Therefore, ultimately, whether the data to be read is perfectly sequential and can be read in one linear swoop, -or- whether the data is scattered all across the media -- since access time for any given offset is equal, fragmentation -- although it can occur, is inconsequential on solid state media like flash drives, memory cards or solid state hard drives (SSD)... ?

 

My goal is not to make a big deal of a small sentence on a page -- I would just like to be corrected if I am wrong.
