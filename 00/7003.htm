whisperingchaos say:

My experience differs from the Volunteer Moderator's.  On Windows 7, the new Desktop version of Skype: 8.23.0.10 supports creating contact groups but it ignores/destroys existing ones.  Therefore, when upgrading to this version, existing groups disappear.  At the least, given contact groups continue to exist in the new version, upgrading to it introduces a "bug",  ignoring/deleting existing groups, that should be repaired.

Here's how to create, update, and delete a contact group with the new version:

 

To create a contact group that you'll own:

  • Select a contact you wishto include in the new group.  The message area will now reflect the current message history and allow you to chat, make calls, ...
  • Click on the icon displaying the abstract drawing of a person's head & shoulders with a plus sign (+) next to the head.  It should appear in the upper right hand corner above the message area.  If you hover on this icon it displays: "Create new group".  Once clicked, a dialogue box appears titled: "Add to Group"
  • Select at least at least one more contact.  A group must have a minimum of at least two contacts.
  • Click on "Done".  Afterwards, the message/chat area is updated to reflect the new group whose name is a comma separated list of the selected contacts.  This group is now owned by you and appears in your contact list.  Also notice, that this new group reports on one more participant (member) than initially added to it.  A little unsettling until one realizes that one has been automatically added to the group.

To modify the group name to reflect a desired one:

  • Click on the group name - top left of message area.  An edit profile dialogue box appears. 
  • Click on the pencil icon to the right of the name.  This action allows you to overwrite the previous/default, comma separated name.

To delete a group you own:

  • Click on the group name - top left of message area.  An edit profile dialogue box appears. 
  • Scroll to the bottom of this dialogue box.  A "Leave group" link should appear at the very bottom.
  • Click on "Leave group".  This seems to destroy a group you own, as it eliminates it from the contact list and can't be found using "Search".

  • Is inconsistent, as "Profiles" for non-group contacts offer a simple, explicit "Delete" operation while group contacts require inferring, through experimentation on part of the user, that deleting a group contact requires the owner to leave the group.  This inconsistency impedes a user's ability to understand the UI.  
  • "Leave group" will either destroy a group if the current user executing this operation is its owner or will preserve the group and detach the user from it when someone else owns it.  This operation is polymorphic: its semantics change based on ownership.  This polymorphic quality imparts ambiguity.  Ambiguity becomes apparent in situations involving duplicate group names, as group names aren't unique.  In this situation one can imagine a user experiencing a little wave of anxiety rippling through its synapses when a user realizes it might have just destroyed a group it owned when the user simply wanted to detach from a different group, not owned by the user, which happens to share the same name.
  • Although in abstract terms a contact is a member of a communications group, the primary operations, such as calling, sharing a desktop, chatting ..., executed by a contact are never applied to itself - they are only directed to one or more other members of the group.  Therefore, it's jarring to me to see myself as an item in the contact membership list that I own.  Older Skype versions didn't display my contact name in a group's membership list when I viewed it.  It seems an entry in the contact list referring to its owner was added to enable this new method of deleting a group, instead of just enabling "Delete" for the group's owner.

Besides what's discussed so far in this reply and the initial post by cwcsdc other significant deficiencies introduced by the re-imagined group feature exist:

  • The prior implementation of contact groups exposed each of its individual members.  Therefore, one could execute a call operation to one or more specific members of the group without involving its entire membership.  This was certainly handy both for my "Family" contact group and for each of my "Company" groups, as it's unusual for me to start a call that included all my family members and even more extraordinary to call all contacts associated to particular company at the same time.  Unfortunately the new version at least by default, doesn't expose its individual members for call selection, therefore, initiating a call rings all the members of a group.   So far I've been unable to locate a means in the new version that allows the selectivity offered by the previous version.
  • The new version's inability to display and limit the contact list to individual member names for a selected contact group when participating in some communication, like a call, decreases the information available to an end user that can be used to improve communication between the end user and other participants in the call.  This is especially true in the situation of "Company" contact groups or other ones in which, for most members, the end user has only a casual/ephemeral relationship.   For example, Steve is the primary contact for an end user when working with company X.  However, John Smith of company X will occasionally join the call with Steve and the end user.  Because of John Smith's association to Steve through company X the end user included John Smith as a contact in its company X group.  During a future conversation between the end user and Steve, Steve mentions John without referring to his last name and potentially within a context unfamiliar to the end user.  In this situation the end user isn't sure that Steve is referring to the John Smith he already knows.   In the prior version, John Smith's full name appeared in the contact list.   Therefore, with this information readily available through the prior version, the end user can easily formulate an insightful question: "Steve, are you talking about John Smith?", to clarify the identity of the "John" mentioned by Steve.
  • Since the contact list can no longer be limited to a specific contact group, it introduces "noise" by actively displaying individuals and other members that are unrelated to the current conversation.  

I'm certainly curious to understand the reasoning applied by the Skype team to re-implement the contact group feature.  From my inferences explained above, I don't see any benefits to the end user that warrant the change.  In fact, it seems apparent to me that the new implementation introduces more problems than it solves.  I'd also be surprised if the same team which initially designed and implemented contact groups proposed and encoded its re-imagined version.
