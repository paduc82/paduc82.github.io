ken sheridan say:

On what basis are you expecting the return value to be computed?  The DateDiff function returns the difference between the month of one date and the month of another, the day of the month in either case being irrelevant, e.g.

Date1 = #2015-07-31#
Date2 = #2016-02-01#

? DateDiff("m",Date1,Date2)
 7

It can be consequently be logically broken down as follows:

? (Month(Date2) - Month(Date1)) + ((Year(Date2) - Year(Date1))*12)
 7

If all that you require is the inclusive difference between the month of one date and the month of another, then you merely need to add 1 to the return value:

? DateDiff("m",Date1,Date2)+1
 8

If, on the other hand, you want the day-on-day difference in months then the following VBA function will return this:

Public Function GetAgeMonths(varDoB As Variant, Optional varAgeAt As Variant) As Variant

    Dim intmonths As Integer

    If Not IsNull(varDoB) Then
        If IsMissing(varAgeAt) Then varAgeAt = VBA.Date

        intmonths = DateDiff("m", varDoB, varAgeAt) - (GetAge(varDoB, varAgeAt) * 12)

        If Day(varDoB) > Day(varAgeAt) Then
            intmonths = intmonths - 1
        End If

        GetAgeMonths = intmonths
    End If

End Function

e.g.

Date1 = #2015-07-31#
Date2 = #2016-02-01#

? GetAgeMonths(Date1,Date2)
 6

Whereas:

Date1 = #2015-07-01#
Date2 = #2016-02-01#

? GetAgeMonths(Date1,Date2)
 7

john - project say:

Sebastian,

Yes you can do a year delay in Project but I would not recommend that type of approach for the scenario you describe.

I don't know the nature of your business but let's say that a "season" starts June 1st and ends August 31th. Then let's say the deadline for completing a task during the season is July 31st. The next task (successor) will take 2 months so there is no way it will complete by end of season. If the successor task was simply delayed by a year (365 ed), that would make it start Aug 1st of the following year and it still wouldn't finish by the end of next year's season. So you really don't want a year delay but rather you want a "delay" that will start the task at the beginning of next season.

There are various ways to delay a task but with your scenario I suggest you simply flag those tasks that must start next season and then shift the remaining tasks to start at the beginning of next season (i.e. not with a dependency link but by separating the next chain of tasks as a whole new set and resetting the start date of the first task in that new chain).

Hope this helps.

John

tom_bpc say:

Sebastian,

I'm afraid your "...then project B must wait a whole year," may be a little misleading.  Seasonal work restrictions are normally (and easily) handled using project, resource, and/or task calendars in MS Project.  Examples of weather-related non-work periods for various activities include summer thawing of ice-roads in the far north and winter-shutdowns of asphalt plants in less extreme northern climates - not to mention the myriad of regulatory restrictions for activities in environmentally sensitive areas.  I usually handle these with task calendars.  Such calendars automatically interrupt any ongoing work at the end-of-season and restart it at the beginning of the next season.  So yes, an accumulated delay of a few days in the wrong (weather-sensitive) activities can lead to project delay of several months.

If your organization prohibits starting an activity that cannot finish before end-of-season, then the overall completion will be delayed further than strictly necessary.  Modeling of such schedule constraints is more complex.  This is the scenario that John's answer seems to address.

tom_bpc say:

Sebastian,

Glad you got it sorted.

A slightly more efficient approach would be to drop the resource altogether and simply assign your 1-day-per-year calendar as the Task Calendar of the publishing task.

Caveats:

1. With either approach, the task must have a non-zero duration for the calendar to be applied.  I typically assign a duration of 1-day for such things - e.g. it takes 1 day to perform the "Publishing" task.  If insisted, you can then re-tag the 1-day task as a "milestone," but I find that's more trouble than it's worth.

2. Use default (FS) relationships for predecessors and successors.

3. Using such Task or Resource Calendars does affect the calculation of Total Slack (and Critical tasks) in ways that may be confusing.

Your situation seems pretty rare, so I would imagine any workable solution to seem "wierd" to some.  Nonetheless, both approaches seem quite straightforward to me. Either:

1. This (publishing) task shall be performed by a resource (the "publishing window" resource) that is only available on 1 specific day each year.

OR

2. This (publishing) task may only be performed (using available worktime) during 1 specific day each year.

In practice I've had to impose similar restrictions for, say, monthly steering committee meetings.  If key decisions don't get included on the agenda this month, then they won't get made until next month's meeting.  Those are the facts in some governance schemes, and using task (or resource) calendars seems to be the best way to model them in a schedule.

Good luck, tom
