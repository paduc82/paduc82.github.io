Band 2 vs. Charge HR & Suggestions to Improve the Microsoft Health App

So I've been a FitBit person for a few years now, right now I have the ChargeHR, but when the Band 2 came out it was too cool to resist not picking one up to compare against my FitBit. I've been wearing both for 4 days, and so far I feel that it is a mixed bag. I feel that the ChargeHR is a better fitness tracker (obviously the ChargeHR does not have GPS), while the Band 2 beats the pants off of the ChargeHR in other smart functions.

The biggest issue I'm having with the Band 2 is really something that could be easily improved on Microsoft's part, and pertains to the edge in terms of data presentation that FitBit has when using the website dashboard and the mobile app. Specifically, while the Band 2 does track second-to-second heart rate during exercises (like the FitBit does all day, another, separate, issue with the Band 2 - see below), it does not actually let you access that information - it simply plots it on a graph and gives you a few statistics like max heart rate, average heart rate, finishing heart rate, etc (see screenshot 1). One of the things that makes the FitBit dashboard so powerful is that you can actually track along each minute and see what your heart rate was during that minute (see screenshot 2). For me personally this is very useful, because I use this information to compare workouts afterwards on a spreadsheet. 

Other random thoughts/comparisons:

  1. "Continuous" heart rate tracking. Both FitBit and the Band 2 claim to track your heart rate continuously throughout the day, but only the FitBit actually does this. The Band takes your heart rate once ever 10 minutes, and therefore by definition going to miss things that occur during your day. This isn't the worst thing in the world, but I do have to say, it is kindof cool to be able to look back on the heart rate graph of my day on the FitBit Dashboard and see how high my heart rate got exactly when I climbed those 10 flights of stairs (See screenshot 3). Or conversely, to find out within 5 minutes what time of the day that was that I climbed those 10 flights of stairs. The reality is that when you check your heart rate once every 10 minutes instead of truly continuously, you lose a lot of this resolution (See screenshot 4).  Again, notice that you can't trace over the chart like you can on the FitBit dashboard and find your heart rate at every specific point in the day. Obviously the reason for the decreased "continuous" heart rate resolution on the Microsoft Band is for the purposes of energy conservation, to power the screen and all the other amazing smart features - I am just pointing out the trade-off. It is also worth noting that the Band does switch into true continuous heart rate tracking mode during exercise, just not during the rest of the day.
  2. The FitBit app. It is just much more refined than the Microsoft Health app for a few reasons:
    1. Truly real time information. The FitBit app, when connected to the FitBit itself, will show your steps/calories/heart rate changing in real time (see screenshot 5). Meaning, when you pull out your phone on a run, you can see your heart rate on there, and your steps and calories going up in real time. In contrast, the Microsoft Health app syncs once when you open it, and does not show real time information gathered from the band.
    2. Data density within one screen. There is much more information presented in the home screen of the FitBit app than the Microsoft Health app as per screenshots 5 and 6 below.
    3. Data presentation. I was too lazy to take many more screenshots, but when you dig deeper and click on each of the numbers in the FitBit app, everything is presented beautifully, in a slightly more refined manner than the Microsoft Band.
    4. Social connections. One of the areas that FitBit really excels is in the social aspect of fitness. The app and online dashboard enable you to friend other FitBit people, join activity groups and be ranked against them on your stats, have "competitions" of various types with up to 10 people in each. There really is no corollary within the Microsoft Health app or dashboard.
  3. Battery life. Obviously Band seeks to do more than the FitBit, but it is a major consideration that the battery life of the Band is 2 days, compared to 5 days for the ChargeHR (despite the true heart rate monitoring).
  4. Aesthetics. Obviously the Band 2 tries to accomplish more in the way of sensors and therefore requires more weight and real estate, but I think some of this is probably superfluous... personally I do not find much utility in a UV sensor and would have opted for a version without whatever bulk is added by it. I would have appreciated a more subtle outward facing clasp area, something more akin to what the FitBit has.
  5. Feel. I can hardly tell that my FitBit is on my wrist. It is light, slim, and extremely flexible. The band feels sortof like a handcuff - not even close to as flexible and natural feeling as the FitBit. 
  6. Barometer. Both the Band and the FitBit have barometers, allegedly so that you can track floors climbed. For some reason I have not been able to determine, neither lets you access the actual data from the barometer - it would be great to see calculated altitude based on air pressure as a tile.
  7. Music control. For some strange reason there is no built-in music control feature on the Band, something I assumed would be present. There are third party applications that can do this, but they are a little rough around the edges and it would be nice to see Microsoft come out with a more refined version.

All of that said, I would be willing to settle for the Microsoft Band 2 - if they would just invest some more time developing their app and dashboard experience. Very curious to see what the next few updates to the app/band firmware will bring, but unfortunately I have to make a decision on whether I'll switch from my FitBit to the Band 2 within the return period of 30 days :)

Doing a clean install and reinstalling backups

If you know that you may be having to do a clean install of Windows 10 and have a bunch of programs that you downloaded the best backup you can do is to not erase anything in the Download folder. and then copy that folder along with your data files to your backup.

Reason;  Downloaded programs do not put all of the information into Program Files.  Some of the parts of the installation, usually a .dll file, are installed in the Windows/System32 folder.  These are not usually included in a normal backup.

Instead of copying the programs to the clean install you can reinstall from the Download folder and get every thing in the right place.

graham mayor say:
You could do it with a macro in the normal template e.g. as follows, which will use the signature associated with the active e-mail account. Note that this macro uses early binding to the Outlook object library so you will need to set a reference to that library in the vba editor - tools > references - see http://www.gmayor.com/installing_macro.htm

Sub Send_As_Mail_Attachment()

' send the document as an attachment _
in an Outlook Email message
Dim bStarted As Boolean
Dim oOutlookApp As Outlook.Application
Dim oItem As Outlook.MailItem

On Error Resume Next

'Prompt the user to save the document
ActiveDocument.Save

'Get Outlook if it's running
Set oOutlookApp = GetObject(, "Outlook.Application")

'Outlook wasn't running, start it from code
If Err <> 0 Then
   Set oOutlookApp = CreateObject("Outlook.Application")
   bStarted = True
End If

'Create a new mailitem
Set oItem = oOutlookApp.CreateItem(olMailItem)

    With oItem
       ' .to = "someonerATsomewhere.com"  'the recipient (optional)
       ' .Subject = "This is the subject" 'the subject (optional)
    'Add the document as an attachment
        .Attachments.Add Source:=ActiveDocument.FullName, _
        Type:=olByValue, DisplayName:="Document as attachment"
        .Display
     End With

'Clean up
Set oItem = Nothing
Set oOutlookApp = Nothing
End Sub

By deleting the periods, you've changed the command to variable assignments. For instance, you've told Word to create a variable named "Location" and assign it the value "wdEndOfDocument." Thus, those statements no longer do what they were intended for.

But that is good, because it means that your effective code is really these three lines:

Sub Macro1()
Selection.Endnotes.Add Range:=Selection.Range
End Sub

And that is what you should be using. With the other parameters, you were telling Word to repeat a lot of stuff that you need to specify only once for the document (by selecting Insert > Footnote, defining the parameters, then Apply):

  • Treat them as endnotes, not footnotes
  • Continue numbering from previous endnote, if any
  • Number the first Endnote as "1" 
  • Use Roman numerals
  • Don't use any special reference symbol (like * or )
