al jarvi say:

Look at using a free dynamic naming service like No-IP.com or DynDNS that map a fully qualified domain name (FQDN) to your cable ISP assigned DHCP address. The way it works is you run a small updater program on one of your home PCs that contacts the No-IP.com (or DynDNS) servers on a time scheduled basis. The server then knows your current IP address and maps that to your unique FQDN. You call home using the FQDN, ie. yourFQDN.noip.com for example.

Note that many consumer grade routers support No-IP.com or DynDNS or similar services. That means you setup everything in the router instead of a PC on the LAN. Check the users manual for help or the manufacturers support site.

http://www.no-ip.com/services/managed_dns/free_dynamic_dns.html

http://www.dyndns.com/services/dns/dyndns/

As far as the RDC host computer on your home LAN you really should use a static LAN IP address. Not doing so can negate any port forwarding scheme you have through your router if the PC reboots and gets a different IP assigned.

The article Jack pointed you to contains the rest of the dirty details. Good luck and have fun...

canadian tech say:
I just yesterday worked on a client's PC with the same symptoms.  After a lot of trying, I decided that it was time to get conservative.  I removed the drive from the machine.  Then installed it in an external drive controller and connected that to my working PC.  Then I was able to copy off all the data, with only a few corrupted files, on to the working PC.  It was then that I decided to install a new hard drive and re-install Windows.  Further investigation yielded that the drive was almost 11 years old.  Very likely to be corrupted, and really not usable.  Drives just don't last that long.

wasimahmad6699 say: My Laptop is 8 years old, quite old.

canadian tech say:

Life expectancy of a laptop hard drive is about 5 years.  Yours is either failing now, or will be shortly -- statistically speaking.

If you were my client and brought me your computer, if the computer were otherwise is good condition, I would be replacing the hard drive with a new 7200rpm one at a cost of about $60.  
Then re-installing windows 7

If you want detailed step by step instructions, I will provide them.  As long as you are attentive and follow directions precisely, these are not hard to follow and most intelligent people can do it.

wasimahmad6699 say: I live in Bangladesh, I can do the installation on win 7, but the main problem is about previous settings..... I do not want to lost those, second problem is I wanna to add a second HDD to my laptop as I have a option for it, but here's the problem is about HDD bay, which may not avilable here.

wasimahmad6699 say: I want to just fix the registry without any problems.... 

canadian tech say:

First step is to ensure you have a good backup.  Statistically speaking you are likely to have a complete failure soon.

There is a great facility in Win7 that you should use.  It saves all your DATA and settings in one large file.  It  is a program designed to  facilitate the transfer to a new PC.  It is called Windows Easy Transfer.  You run it on your current system and save that big file to an external drive.  Then install Windows on a new drive and afterwards run the same program on the new system.  99% of the time, it results in a system that looks just like the old one.  Note.  This save Data and settings, not programs.  Programs must be re-installed.

The replacement drive could and probably should be a larger one.  I  use a 7200 rpm Western Digital black with 500 Gig of space.  Most OEM drives run at 5400 and this makes a notable difference in performance.
