jim.low say:

As Oberwald indicated, a lot of good definitions are out there. However, it depends on what our overall objective is.

 

For Windows 7, there are things that use of a Work Group will provide along the lines of peer-to-peer; and some things it won't.

 

Your concern about others viewing what you've been accessing on the internet from another peer device (say another Win 7 system), is warranted; though under very specific circumstances.

 

For example with two devices:

1) User1 has a profile on your computer and on his own.

2) You being User2, has allowed User1 with admin privileges.

3) If User1 maps a drive to your C: using his admin define profile, then User1 would be granted all the power of admin rights that he's been afforded.

 

On the other hand, if all you want to do is share pictures, videos, documents and that type of stuff, that's not an issue. Just post them in a normally shared folder. Most often it is named Music Library, Pictures, Videos; though you can set the Documents folder to shared as well, or as sub-folder within the Documents folder of your profile as shared. In that way, use of peer-to-peer on your local network can be more tightly controls, especially when the other device and your device is added to a HomeGroup. Everything operated "under-the-covers" that way.

 

A couple of links either in Micorsoft or a search on the net will give the proper nomenclature and setup steps to provide a seamless approach to sharing of information and what is not shared.

 

Just remember, belonging to a home group through peer-to-peer does not make any other device that is also sharing, a server. It is very unlikely that to launch Word for example from a device that is part of your shared network, where your PC is the one which holds the license won't work (or would be illegal if it did) since the installation PC where it is installed is the system of record for the installation.

 

Also, keep in mind that any shared device should remain on your local network, not one on the internet. That type of setup is much more complex, and may require third party software to permit it (even through VPN connections).

 

So, the point - you PC is as secure as you make it. If you don't allow another to have access to your system through the creation of another profile with Admin privileges, and only allow them access through the established and available methods with your OS, there should be nothing to worry about.

 

I hope this helps...

 

- Jim

elmer123 say:

scanned docs

 how do I convert scanned doc pictures to word on windows 10 ?

anthonybyrne3 say:

Microsoft Word can recognize and convert scanned PDF files into Word documents without requiring any additional software.

Right-click the PDF you want to convert, select Open with, and click Word in the resulting pop-out menuClick OK when prompted. Word will begin converting the scanned PDF into a Word document.
Enable editing for the file if needed. 
Converting scanned files into Word documents isn't always perfect. You may have to add missing words, remove excess spaces, and fix typos before your Word document is ready to go.

If your document saved as an image (e.g., a JPG or PNG file), you can use OCR to convert it instead. To use OCR:

go to https://www.newocr.com/

Click Choose file. It's a grey button at the top of the page. Doing so prompts a File Explorer.
Select the scanned file.Click Upload + OCR. You'll find this button near the bottom of the visible page. New OCR will begin extracting the readable text from your scanned image.Scroll down and click Download. This link is in the lower-left side of the page, just above the box containing the document's text. A drop-down menu will appear.

Click Microsoft Word (DOC). It's in the Download drop-down menu. This will prompt a Microsoft Word version of the scanned file to download onto your computer.
