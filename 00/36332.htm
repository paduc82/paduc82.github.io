I am recording and editing in both Blu-ray and the subset of Blu-ray known as AVCHD. A full Blu-ray movie requires special Blu-ray programs and in general will not play correctly, if at all, on WMP. CyberlinkPowerDVD, ArcSoft , and a few other players in their deluxe (expensive) editions will play full Blu-ray if you have a drive suited for Blu-ray playback. I can select between full Blu-ray and AVCHD by selecting a single button when encoding a HD file. The advantage of AVCHD is that it can be recorded on a standard DVD or DL one with an ordinary DVD burner program and will play back on most stand alone Blu-ray players as well as on a computer that will play Blu-ray. The main disadvantage of AVCHD is that it allows much shorter recording time on a DVD than does a normal Blu-ray file set recorded on a Blu-ray disc. In short , the Microsoft movie maker and editing programs and WMP are next to useless when working with Blu-ray and AVCHD files. One of the best choices for working with HD files is the Sony Vegas Pro 9.0 which also will edit and record standard video and sound files. But this program will likely cost you about as much as you paid for your camcorder, so few will buy it.

There are programs that will convert Blu-ray and AVCHD files to older standard definition formats, which can be fairly good but are far from 1080p HD that can be handled by Blu-ray and AVCHD. Such programs can be had for under US$100. You will find some Blu-ray and AVCHD files that will not work correctly on some of these programs.

The Blu-ray specifications allow recording in several video and audio formats. For video, the mpeg 4 format and another one were used early on and still some. However the AVC format using mp4 perhaps is most used now. There are compressed audio stereo and surround formats still used, but for the best audio LPCM uncompressed audio is used, sometimes in 6 channel surround, sometimes at 24 bit, and sometimes at up to 192 KHz. This high resolution may not play on many media players, including WMP, even if the audio alone is extracted from a Blu-ray movie. The old CD standard for sound is not suitable for the highest quality of audio that is possible now. A few companies that require top, demanding sound are recording Blu-rays discs with 24-bit resolution and sometimes up to 96KHz, and sometimes in surround. As a result the audio alone may require up to slightly over 10000 Kbps. Opus Arte is using such HD sound for some of their operas and ballets.

Hi, most likely caused by a failed install (not the wizard) you need to do a bit of detective work, lets try the easy fix first:-


1. Go to start, run and type regedit.

2. Navigate in the registry editor to
HKLM\software\microsoft\windows\current version\installer\inprogress.

3. Click on the inprogress key and note the name of the file listed on the
right side for databasepath (should be have a .msi extension).

4. Rename inprogress to oldinprogress.

5. Close the registry editor.

6. Open My Computer.

7. Navigate to C:\Windows\Installer (Installer folder is hidden by
default, folder Views check "show hidden files and folders" also uncheck "hide protected system files", don't forget to undo this when finished)) and rename the file noted in step 3.

8. Reboot the computer and try the install again.

You can create a stop and start script for Windows Update.

Create a Notepad file with each of the following and save them with the .bat extension.

stop-updates.bat:

sc config wuauserv start= disabled
net stop wuauserv

sc config bits start= disabled
net stop bits

sc config dosvc start= disabled
net stop dosvc

pause

start-updates.bat:

sc config wuauserv start= auto
net start wuauserv

sc config bits start= auto
net start bits

sc config dosvc start= auto
net start dosvc

pause
