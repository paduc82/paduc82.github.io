joeu2004 say: Do not confuse "end" and "beginning" of the period with "last" and "first" day of the month, even if the period is "monthly".

For example, if the monthly period starts on day 2 of a month and ends on day 1 of the following month, and payments are made "at the end", use type=0 even though the payment is on day 1 of some month.

Your financial arrangement will determine if payments are made at the end or the beginning of the period.

What you describe appears to be a savings or investment arrangement.  Usually, we model savings with an initial balance (it might be zero) and payments

To know the answer for your situation, think about what interest applies to after the first and last monthly periods.  (Note:  "monthly period", not "month").  By the way,

Two possibilities:

1. You have or you make an initial deposit at the beginning of the first period; and you make a payment at the end of each period. In the first period, interest is earned only on the initial deposit; and the last payment does

2. You have or you make an initial deposit; and you make a payment at the beginning of each period. Interest is earned on the initial balance plus the payment in the first period; and the last payment

-----

FYI, most loans are type=0 (payment at the end).  Think about it:  it would not make sense for the lender to give you $100,000, then ask you to make a payment.  In effect, that simply reduces the size of the loan.

However, most leasing arrangements are type=1 (payment at the beginning).  In that case, for example, you are receiving a car that is valued at $100,000 (smile), and the auto dealer asks you to pay for the first month immediately ("payment in advance").

joeu2004 say:

I tried to keep things simple.  But there is a third possible interpretation of your situation.  You make an initial deposit at the beginning of period 1, and you make 23 additional payments at the beginning of each subsequent period, for a total of 24 payments over 2 years.

Technically, that is not "equal" payments for each period.   But we can convert it into a model that is.  The FV formula would be:

=FV(7.5%/12, 23, -250, -5000*(1+7.5%/12), 1)

Again, we choose type=1 because we want to earn interest after each payment.

But there are only 23 equal payments.

The amount 5000*(1+7.5%/12) is the initial deposit plus the first-month interest.  It becomes the "initial" deposit for the remaining 23 periods of equal payments.

-----

PS....  Naumee wrote: ``FV [Future Value] means At the beginning of the period / At the end of the period``.

No.  FV always means "at the end of the last period".

Again, interest is always applied at the end of the period, regardless of when the payment is made.

The difference between type=0 and type=1 only has to do with when the regular and equal payment is made during the period; and in effect, whether or not interest for that period applies to the payment for that period.
