Office 365 Outlook Online Archive Problems - Microsoft Community

pawnie say:

Office 365 Outlook Online Archive Problems

Hi, I just thought I'd summarize the many problems I've had with the online archive feature of Outlook using Office 365 (likely the same for other versions of Outlook with the online archive).  Hopefully Microsoft looks at this and puts a little more thought/work into this feature.

First off, I have saved my PST files dating back to when I first started my own business (about 12 years ago).  I work in the engineering consulting business and I regularly search through old emails from several years back looking for old contacts, or maybe a problem has recently surfaced on a project I worked on years ago, etc.  I want *all* of my email available in a searchable format.  I have traditionally used Gmail for this but I only ever had my Inbox mail available (through email forwarding everything to Gmail) and I'm tired of managing multiple accounts and clients and I also want to be able to forward an old email once in a while without having it come from a gmail address.  I was delighted when I first installed Office 365, especially the Outlook connectivity, DNS options, etc.  Everything about it went so smooth that I find it perplexing that the online archive is so kludgy.  

Problems in order:

- Uploading email was excruciating.  I tried using the ridiculously complicated Azure blob data upload and mapping file method at first but the transfers kept failing with very little explanation why in the log file (typically because of a timeout - I tried all kinds of ways to fix this including limiting the number of transfer pipes but same result).  I eventually learned that you can just keep restarting the transfer and it will pick up where it left off but not until I tried the method below.  They really should tell you this (that it saves the partial upload even if it aborts part way through).  A lot of the transfer jobs also informed me that they didn't transfer completely and I watched the data size closely and indeed every single job was short by a GB or 2 without any information on what wasn't transferred. 

- Another issue with this method is it completely shuts down the Internet in our house (it overwhelms the connection to the point where Internet access is way too slow to do anything in the entire house) and of course it takes hours and hours per file.  This is weird because the methods I tried below did not do this.

- After the frustration using Azure storage method to upload, I eventually started just importing files with the desktop app directly to the online folder.  These too would fail constantly after partially transferring, leaving holes in the data all over the place.  So then I tried just opening PST files and copying large chunks at a time into the folders in the archive.  So slow and tedious and of course, lots of failed copies and aborts just like the other methods.  In case anyone thinks this is because of my Internet connection, it does the same thing if trying to copy from one data file to another on my local machine. 

- Using these last two methods, any Outlook meeting invite I've ever received would pop up like a new event.  I had to close hundreds of windows throughout the process.  I also suspect these had something to do with the aborted transfers but I'm not sure.

- After weeks of struggling with this (no joke), with spotty results, holes in the data that I knew I would have to comb through and fill in, much to my dismay, I realized Outlook had started duplicating messages all on its own, in some cases up to 5 times!  Very strange but somewhat common from what I've seen online.  The clean up tool won't work on the online archive so my choices are to live with the duplicates or manually comb through years of emails and manually deleting duplicates (not bloody likely).

- I have the Enterprise E3 edition with the unlimited auto-expanding archive.  I knew from the beginning the way it works:  when you approach the data limit, you have to wait up to 30 days for them to add an extra 10GB.  This is fine for someone whose data is just normally being auto-archived as messages expire but excruciating for me but I was willing to live with it.  I have about 150GB of email so I purposely only uploaded 90GB and planned on uploading the rest, 10GB at a time over a few months.  I see this as just not a great system.  I've also recently read that there might be a 170GB hard limit on the archive which is absolutely not as advertised (can anyone confirm this?).

- The worst part of all:  after carefully checking my archive limit constantly to make sure I stopped at just over 90GB, I stopped transferring and went to bed one night only to wake up to 120GB in my archive and it stated I was over the limit in the Exchange admin area.    I suspected this was happening earlier but now I knew for sure.  I have no idea if this is because of more duplicates, but I don't think so (see below).  I was worried about whether this would mean something would get deleted after all the work I did so I contacted support and the help desk tech told not to worry, it just auto-expands.  I knew this isn't quite how it works but I didn't want to debate it.  I also said it was still letting me transfer data even though I was over the data limit and asked if I should just continue and she said yes.  She emailed me a couple of links to how auto-expanding works (which I already had and were contrary to the advice she gave) and that was that.  At this point I was losing interest in the whole project so I shrugged and continued uploading until it finally prevented me from doing so. 

- More phantom data expansion occurred after that.  I probably have uploaded about 75GB of real data that had magically turned into 190GB on their end. 

- Then the data was automatically split into subfolders for each year of data.  I'd be OK with this if I could search for a specific message in a subfolder from the root folder but nope, doesn't work (tested thoroughly).  So I would have to know exactly what year the message I'm looking for is located or navigate through several folders and searches until I find it.

- After all of this, the data has so many holes in it and I can't upload any more to fill them in and I don't want to wait 30 or more days to find out they only give me another 10GB (leaving me still 70GB over the limit) or maybe even just start randomly deleting some of the data to bring it into compliance so I decided to just delete everything more than a couple of years old (the only data that seems complete) and start over.

- Deleting data from the online archive has been equally frustrating with lots of failures, aborted attempts, painfully slow, etc.  Also, apparently you can't delete folders from the Deleted Items folder.  You can get rid of the messages but from what I've read online, you're stuck with those folders.

- In the end, after deleting everything but the last couple of years of data (maybe 35GB max), I have an archive that's still over a 100 GB.  There are no duplicates in this data as far as I can tell so I'd sure like to know what's taking up all that space.

- Perhaps the most annoying thing of all is finding out I can't access this data from the mobile Outlook app or a mobile browser. Also, Outlook goes to great lengths to prevent me from loading the web app in "desktop" mode on either Chrome or Samsung's web browser (I did find a mobile browser that can get around this but I'm afraid to mention the name of it lest Microsoft "fix" this loophole - it's still not really a functional way to get to this data from my phone though).  Why?  I can access an eternity of email on Gmail from the mobile app and instantly/seamlessly search for a message all in one convenient folder.  

That was the final straw.  I'm currently archiving my mail with MailStore and hosting the data myself.  MailStore's web host is almost exactly what I wanted the online archive to be.  I'll be playing with the MAPI host too to see how it works so maybe I can even access all of this from the outlook mobile app (it has an add-on for the desktop Outlook app which is very good).  It's just too bad the online archive wasn't better though, I'd love to have all the functionality I need in one convenient application. 

I was thinking maybe the problem is I'm trying to use the online archive for something it's not designed for.  But then, if you can't use an "unlimited" online archive to store and search through old email, what on Earth would it be for?

Formatting a drive in Windows 8.1 is handled by the Disk Management utility. The same process applies to both hard disk drives, or HDDs, and solid state drives, or SSDs. After deleting the volumes, or partitions, on the drive, you can format the drive and create new volumes. Remember to back up any files you want to keep before starting this process. Deleting and formatting destroys all data on the drive.

  1. Tap the "Search" icon on the Windows Start screen or click start and type "format disk" in the Search field. Select "Create and Format Hard Disk Partitions." The Disk Management utility opens.
  2. Select "Create and Format Hard Disk Partitions."
  3. Click the drive that you want to format. Note that in most cases "Disk 0" is the drive with the Windows operating system on it. If this is the disk you want to format, you should reinstall Windows instead.
  4. Select the drive you want formatted. Select "Delete Volume."
  5. Click "Yes" at the warning that all data on the partition will be deleted. After a few seconds, the volume is deleted. The drive is now listed as unallocated in the Disk Management window.
  6. Disk Management warns that all data on the partition will be lost.
  7. Select the drive you are formatting if it isn't already selected. Click the "Action" menu, then "All Tasks" and "New Simple Volume." The New Simple Volume Wizard opens. Note that if the disk is part of an array, advanced users will have additional options like creating a new spanned volume, striped volume, mirrored volume or RAID-5 volume.
  8. Create a New Simple Volume. Click the "Next" button in the New Simple Volume Wizard so you can specify your preferences. The New Simple Volume Wizard opens.
  9. Specify how much space should be used in the new volume by entering a value in the text field. If you are creating only one volume or partition in the drive, this number should be the same as the maximum disk space displayed above. Click "Next."
  10. Specify the volume size. Select a drive letter that you would prefer the computer to use for this drive. If you don't want a letter assigned, click the "Do Not Assign a Drive Letter or Drive Path" option. Advanced users may also prefer to mount the drive in an empty NTFS folder. Click "Next."
  11. Specify a drive letter. Select a file system for the volume. Your options are ExFAT or NTFS. Use ExFAT if you're going to share files with a Mac computer; otherwise, NTFS is the better choice. Unlike its predecessors, ExFAT can support the same volume sizes as NTFS -- up to 256TB. However, it's not as robust as NTFS and doesn't support file system-level encryption and built-in compression. Mac computer's can't write files to an NTFS drive.
  12. Select a file system. Select the "Perform a Quick Format" option if the drive doesn't contain any private information, or if you're using the drive for your personal use. A Quick Format takes only a few seconds, but the data it leaves behind can be easily recovered by anyone with the right software. A full format takes much longer but it does erase every byte of data. Select the "Enable File and Folder Compression" option if desired. Type a name for the volume and click "Next." Change the volume label if desired.
  13. Click "Finish" after reviewing the settings that were applied to the new volume. If you want to change anything, click the "Back" button and format the drive again. Close the New Simple Volume Wizard by clicking "Finish."
  14. Exit the Disk Management utility.
