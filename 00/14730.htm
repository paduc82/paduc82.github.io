This code was created for someone who wanted to give each student a document containing the questions in a different random order for each student:

Dim i As Integer, j As Integer, k As Integer, Source As Document, Target As Document, students As Integer

Dim Message, Title, question As Range

Message = "Enter number of students"    ' Set prompt.

Title = "Randomizer"    ' Set title.

' Display message, title

students = InputBox(Message, Title)

 

For k = 1 To students

    Set Source = Documents.Open("E:\Worddocs\source1.doc")

    Set Target = Documents.Add

    For i = 100 To 0 Step -1

        j = Int((i * Rnd) + 1)

        Set question = Source.Paragraphs(j).Range

        Target.Range.InsertAfter question

        question.Delete

    Next i

'   Print and Close Document with random list of questions

    Target.PrintOut

'    Target.SaveAs ("E:\worddocs\student" & k & ".doc")

    Target.Close SaveChanges:=wdDoNotSaveChanges

    Source.Close SaveChanges:=wdDoNotSaveChanges

Next k

Leonard, I'll mention - only because John and Trevor have not (on first reading) - that use of multiple/variable calendars (whether task or resource) always makes total slack unreliable as an indicator of the critical path.  Deadlines/late-constraints and resource leveling also degrade the usefulness of total slack.  These issues are  not limited to MS Project.  Since I believe that multiple calendars as well as the other techniques are necessary for modeling the work that I'm involved with, I use other tools (add-ons) to accurately assess the critical path - and other non-critical logical paths - through the schedule.  Project's user base is largely uninterested in these issues, so the desired functionality is not available out of the box.

tom_bpc say:

Leonard, a couple random thoughts of my own:

1. Total Slack is always calculated as the lesser of Start Slack or Finish Slack, which are themselves calculated as the difference between the Late dates and the Early dates - all computed according to the task's "effective" calendar.  The backward pass in a CPM calculation doesn't keep track of which one out of any number of successor constraints might be the one that controls the Late dates - only the most urgent one matters.  As Trevor suggested, if you don't want your schedule affected by the complications of multiple deadlines with intersecting logical paths, then remove the deadlines.

2. The idea of non-zero Total Float/Slack on the "Critical Path" is anathema to many logic-based scheduling purists (which I don't claim to be).  Most serious practitioners seem to accept (and understand) negative slack as an indicator that the required completion date will not be met.  Positive Slack on the Critical Path is not nearly so accepted, however, and Microsoft Project doesn't support it's calculation in the same way that certain other tools (i.e. OPV) do.  As formally defined (and without the complications I mentioned earlier), Total Float/Slack is an artifact of the CPM/PDM calculation method that indicates the amount that an activity may slip without affecting the project's EARLY Completion date.  The difference between that EARLY project completion date and the project's contractual completion date - variously called "Project Float," "Terminal Float," "Terminal Buffer," or "Project Schedule margin" - is essentially a risk buffer for absorbing Critical Path slippage within the Contract Time.  It should not be confused with Total Float/Slack - which is precisely what your clients seem intent to see.

3. Your combination of an early-constraint and deadline is normally imposed to fix the milestone at the contract date - typically to satisfy demands from stakeholders who refuse to show or accept an earlier completion date.  This is a double-sided constraint (comparable to Finish On in OPV) that will always result in Total Float/Slack that is either zero or negative.  They are disallowed in some construction scheduling specifications because they synthetically inflate float in the predecessor chain.  Your use - to accomplish the same end - effectively hands over your project schedule buffer to every prior task in the project.  This is not a good risk management approach in the industries where I operate.
