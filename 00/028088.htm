Hi Stretch,

I'm not sure unchecking the option will get you what you need.  

Project calculates Earned Value on % complete - which is a duration measure.  If you say the task is 50% complete, Project will assume you have "used" half of the original duration and still have half of the duration left.  As you note, in a 4 week task you should be 50% complete at the end of two weeks.

Now, if you are saying that during that two weeks the resource has only completed 33% of the work - the next question is where is the work the resource did not perform being scheduled?  If the resource was initially scheduled to work 160 hours over the 4 weeks and should have completed 80 hours but only completed about 52 hours has the remaining work been moved forward and extended the duration of the task?  

For a quick experiment I created a 20 day duration task with one resource assigned with a cost of $1 per hour.  My BCWS at the end of week one is $40.  I entered 25 hours of actual work for week one and show ACWP and BCWP $25 at an assignment level. At a task level the BCWP is $36.57 . The duration of the task is now 21.88 days because the 15 hours of work the resource did not complete week 1 has been scheduled at the end of the task. The % complete is calculated at 23%

I update my status date to the end of week 2 and in week two enter 30 hours of actual work.  The duration of the task is now 23.13 days with 25 hours of work being re-scheduled.  The percent complete is 43% (you should be 50%).  The task BCWS is $80, the ACWP is $55, and the Earned Valued (BCWP) is $69.19.

All of this is done without turning off the option.

I hope this helps.

the only difference with data and wifi (aside from the speed) is the access point you're using. with data, you utilize your service provider's internet access point. hence you pay for it. with wifi, you either set up your own access point (such as your home internet with router--in which case you technically still pay for it, but to your isp provider not your cell carrier) or through a wifi hotspot. If public, you get a free connection to the internet while some other bloke pays for the actual service.

 

As for the Touch vs smartphone, I would personally go with the smartphone. the Touch technology is pretty much still in it's infancy as opposed to the smartphone's history. Both Hotmail.com and MSN.com are powered by Outlook.com. New accounts will have the outlook.com domain added after the User Name @.
