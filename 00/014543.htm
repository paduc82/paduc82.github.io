Bing is Slow or Doesn't Load with Time-Warner - Microsoft Community

jeeped say:

Bing is Slow or Doesn't Load with Time-Warner

Technical Level : Intermediate

Summary

There have been a number of complaints recently from people trying to use the Bing.com search engine and finding it slow or completely unresponsive while other search engines and sites respond promptly. This problem may not be with your computer, other device or Bing. The problem may be with your ISP's Domain Name System servers and how they are manipulating the requests that go through them.

This article will be divided into the following sections. If you simply want to get on with the repair and get back to browsing, go straight to the second topic for succinct directions on changing the DNS server for your active Internet connection. After all, the whole point of this article is to get you up and running as quickly as possible.

Details

Background - What is a DNS server and Why am I Having Problems

A Domain Name System server or DNS is like an old phone book. You may not know how to get to someone's house but if you know their name, you can look up their address by cross-referencing their name. Similarly, your computer does not know exactly where Answers.Microsoft.com resides but it can ask a DNS and the DNS will respond with an IP address like 157.56.56.109. With this information your computer can go to the Microsoft Community Answers web site. Virtually every place you want to go on the Internet needs to be resolved from its common name to an IP address and DNS servers are kept busy fulfilling these requests.

The current round of problems with ISP DNS servers stems from what they are attempting to do with each request as it is made through their servers. While PII (Personally Identifying Information) collection is rare outside of outright malware infections, the collating of geographic, demographic and sociographic data is highly valued and is commonly resold to marketing and advertising conglomerates. I have no problem with this information collection until it adversely impacts the internet connection and many ISPs have gone overboard in their data collection methods. When this happens, it is time to shut off the flow of information at the source; e.g. stop using their DNS servers.

Fast Track to Changing the Internet Connection's DNS Servers on a Single Windows 7 Computer

These directions will address a computer running the Windows 7 operating system. The specifics for other systems will vary but the principals are the same. If you cannot adequately transcribe these instructions for your own purposes, post into the Comments section below giving details of your system and I will attempt to address your individual situation. I have bypassed some of the click-this-then-click-that process as the direct route is more appropriate for other versions.

  1. Tap the Windows key+R then paste the following into the Run dialog's Open: text box,

        %SYSTEMROOT%\System32\Control.exe ncpa.cpl

      

    After pasting that in, click OK to launch the Network Connections window pane.

  2. Locate the active network connection. The active connection will not have a red .

      

    If you have more than a single active connection (e.g. a wired and a wireless connection), proceed with the first one and then repeat this procedure for any others.

  3. Right click your active connection and choose Properties. This will open the individual connection's Properties dialog.

          

    Select Internet Protocol Version 4 (TCP/IPv4) and click the Properties button.

  4. With the Internet Protocol properties dialog open, type the new IP addresses of the DNS servers into the Preferred DNS server: and Alternate DNS server: text boxes.

         

    You could also copy the DNS server addresses from the list supplied at the bottom of this article and paste them directly into the first (left-most) part of the text box. The four portions will split up and go into their respective places.

  5. Click OK and then keep clicking OK in all of the respective dialogs that you used to get here. Finally, close the Network Connections window pane.
  6. If you do not see immediate results, restart the computer to flush the DNS cache currently being retained in memory.

That's it. With your ISP's crippled DNS out of the way you should receive the Bing.com search engine in a prompt and snappy manner. You should even experience an improved browsing experience for many other sites.

Getting to Network Connections through the Control Panel


If following hte direct route to the Network Connections window through the keyboard seems daunting, here is the more conventional method of accessing it through the Windows  Control Panel.

Step 1 - Click Start or tap the Windows key and open the Control Panel from teh list down the right side of the Start menu.

Step 2 - With the Control Panel open, locate and open the Network and Sharing Center.

Step 3 - In the Network and Sharing Center pane, locate and click Change adapter settings in the upper-left corner.

You should now be at the Network Connections window pane. Proceed with the instructions in Step 2 of the details in the previous section.

Centralizing the Repair for Multiple Computers/Devices at the Router or Access Point

If you have two or more computers and/or other devices such as phones, tablets, game consoles etc. that use a central point to access the Internet you can effect the DNS change at the router and all of your devices will accept the new settings. While I cannot adequately describe the correct procedure for each and every make and model of router or access point, I can tell you that the process is virtually identical in methodology to the process in changing the DNS servers on a computer. The following is an image from a Cisco DPC3825 DOCSIS 3.0 Gateway wireless router configuration page showing the LAN 1 Static DNS 1 (aka Primary) and LAN 1 Static DNS 2 (aka Secondary) making use of the OpenDNS server settings.

First point to make is that configuring a router or access point will typically have to be performed on a wired (CAT5/6 network cable) connection. Wireless connections are generally not allowed to modify the configuration of a router/access point as a security measure and this should not be changed. Additionally, while most anyone can view a routers basic configuration, you will also likely need a password to change and save the primary and secondary DNS server configuration.

Other Benefits of Alternative Free DNS Services

Using the same functionality that can cripple your Internet connection but with good intentions, a DNS service can add productivity through subtle manipulation of the DNS requests made upon it. Some of the beneficial tasks that an altruistic DNs can perform are:

  • Correction of common spelling mistakes in domain names; e.g. typing micisoft.com could be auto-corrected to microsoft.com.
  • Increased security by minimizing cache poisoning.


Other Problems with DNS Redirection

The DNS redirection problem is not exclusive to misintentioned ISPs. Many variants of malware, adware and viruses use DNS misdirection as a way to change how and even where you connect to a web site or other Internet resource. The Conduit virus is just such an example.

Keeping your computer clean of these parasitic programs, add-ons and toolbars with periodic scan using a free anti-malware utility program like MalwareBytes is always sage advise.

History

Using alternate DNS servers to improve your Internet responsiveness and name resolution functionality is actually nothing new and the practice has been around since the Internet became a part of many households. The only sad thing about the practice is when it is needed to overcome a handicapping of a service you pay for with no regard to your Internet experience. This is the problem when marketing executives with shiny new MBAs on the wall make decisions that affect how you connect to the Internet.

If you change the DNS on your computer, that will bypass the DNS that the router passes along as part of the DHCP IP assignment. A primary DNS will always be used unless it times out or is completely unreachable. There is no alternating with the secondary, only a fail safe fallback.

There is a section in the above article on how to change your router's DNS settings. While individual makes and models of routers are different, the example for a Cisco DPC3825 DOCSIS 3.0 Gateway wireless router is fairly typical of what to expect. If you cannot change the router's settings for some reason, you are stuck with changing the computer's settings.

If you use the router's LAN IP as the secondary DNS, all you are doing is adding a 'hop'. It has to as++k the router to lookup the information from its DNS rather than asking directly the source directly. Not that the secondary would ever be asked much; only when the primary times out.
