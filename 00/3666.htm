    Ignoring the fact that dollars-and-cents values should be rounded to cents at least....

    If the net sales price is S and the commission rate is r, the gross price P = S*(1+r).  So S = P/(1+r).

    Thus, in Excel, the net sales price in C1 is =A1/(1+B1).

    However, it would be prudent to write:  =ROUND(A1/(1+B1),2).

    Caveat:  If you were to compute the commission amount in D1 as =ROUND(C1*B1,2), sometimes C1+D1 will not exactly equal A1.  So either A1 should be computed by =C1+D1, or D1 should be computed by =A1-C1.

    I agree that it is a matter of interpreation.

    IMHO, the OP presented contradictory information.  He/she wrote:  "I want to see in cell C1 what the value would be BEFORE the commission was applied to the value in A1 (where a1 includes the value of the product PLUS the amount of commission on that value)."

    On the one hand, the OP says that the commission "was applied to the value in A1" (gross sales price).  In that case, I agree that your formula is correct.

    On the other hand, the OP says that A1 "includes the value of the product PLUS the amount of commission on that value".  What value?  Based on rules of good English grammar (proper antecedent), one interpretation is:  "the value of the product" (in C1).  In that case, the formula provided by georgejabr and my explanation are correct.

    And here, I might be influenced by the word "commission".  Normally, I believe commission is based on the net sales price, not the gross sales price.  There is good reason for that:  net sales price is usually set to ensure a certain profit based on production or wholesale cost.  Additional cost of sales such as tax and commission is usually charged to the customer directly.  Otherwise, if we set the gross sales price, then deduct a commission, which might be a variable rate, we lose control over the profit margin.

    Be that as it may, it is really up to the OP to decide what he/she wants.

    If the net sales price is $23.52 and the commission on that amount is 1.75%, the gross sales price should be 23.52*(1+1.75%) = $23.93, not $23.94.  Ergo, if the gross sales price is $23.93, the net sales price would be 23.93/(1+1.75%) = $23.52.

    Alternatively, if the gross sales price should indeed be $23.94, the net sales price should be $23.53 so that 23.53*(1+1.75%) = $23.94.

    But if the gross sales price is $23.94 and 1.75% of that amount is to be allocated to commissions (i.e. 23.94*1.75%), the net sales price would be 23.94*(1-1.75%) = $23.52.

    john mcghie say:
    Hi Jorge:

    If this is happening soon after re-starting the computer, then it's definitely tracked changes and not fragmented memory.

    If you use Compare Documents, your editor will get what looks like Track Changes.  In fact, "Compare" is simply an automated way of accurately applying change tracking, the result IS tracked changes, simply automatically applied.

    Mac Word and Windows Word are not related: different factory, different designers, different programmers, different architecture, different code.  In a complex GUI application such as Word, the Operating System is doing 80% of the work.  80 per cent of the CPU time is spent in Mac OS X code, not in Word code.

    Obviously there's a bit of cross-pollination between Word PC and Word Mac, and consequently you get lumps of code that were designed to call Windows functions now calling Mac functions.  The two sets of functions are not exactly equivalent.

    And of course, Office for Windows has literally 100 times more people working on it than Mac Office has.  Yes, Windows Office is bigger, but not THAT much bigger.  It is, however, more expensive :-)
