Who cares?

Whether they truly managed to hack your camera or not, what's going to stop them from releasing the footage even if you were actually foolish enough to pay them?  And who would they actually give it to that might care?

How did you determine the email came from your own account?  The email friendly name (personal name) is easily spoofed (faked) and can be just as easily checked on most email systems by hovering over it until the email address is displayed, though even this can be faked as well.  Since you have two-factor authentication, how would they have gained access without this?

They've already tried to trick you by using at least a single password they likely got from an online database of breached websites and now they're trying to use this for extortion, so why would you ever trust anything they say?

This is the most ridiculous part of all of these many ransomware schemes, since there's simply no reason to trust a single thing that criminals say, but fear causes people to be foolish.

The only option anyone ever has in these situations is to hope for the best, but prepare for the worst, while generally the truth is somewhere between these two extremes.

The only thing you can control is what happens going forward and giving the criminals money does you no good, since they'll then know they've got a sucker and either continue to try and extort more money from you, sell your contact information to other criminals who'll do the same or most likely both to maximize their profit.

Note that the laptop camera typically only picks up your face unless you're standing in front of it.  Also, the easiest way to insure it can't be hacked is to cover it with a piece of masking tape, which can be quickly removed if you'd ever truly need it.  This is both simpler to do and impossible for anyone to bypass remotely.

People get all caught up in the scammer's stories, when the truth is always much simpler and less scary.  That's what con men have always depended on, from the ones trying to sell the Brooklyn Bridge on down to the supposedly "high tech" scams of today, only fools ever believe any of them.

You've already done the right thing by using two-factor authentication and should never use the same password on any important sites, since these are typically easy to recover today if you should forget them.  I never write any of these down for important sites like a bank, which I won't forget since I use it regularly or reset them for others which I only use rarely, since it would take just as long to look them up.

Different passwords are far more important today than their complexity, since most systems won't allow more than a few tries before locking out additional attempts and two-factor authentication makes even having the password generally useless.  Complex passwords were always a bad idea, since they were easily forgotten, while it was the inadequate system designs of the past that required these.

Don't make security difficult, keep it simple and easy to deal with and you'll always be more secure.
