Hi

It sounds like you are describing the Junctions that are present in Windows 7. Many of the default folders that were present in Windows XP have had name changes starting with Windows Vista.

The folders with the shortcut icon are either Symbolic Links or Junctions (AKA: Soft Links or Reparse Points). They are there for  compatibility  with older programs that are hard coded to look for those old folder names when the older program is installed.

 

These folders have special properties that will redirect the old installer program to the new location in Windows 7. This allows the older program to install and work in Windows 7, seamlessly.

 

A good example is the C:\Documents and Settings  folder. This folder was used in previous versions (XP) to hold all of the users files. When an older program that was written for XP is installed, the Symbolic Link called Documents and Settings will redirect that installer to the new location in Windows 7, which is the C:\Users folder.

Because they are very important, they all have the properties, Everyone = Access Denied.

 

There are many of these Soft Links in Windows 7.

 

Windows has supported the use of Soft Links for a long time, but they were not used extensively until Windows Vista. 

 

It is recommended that the user does not change any of the properties of these folders, since doing so can cause some installed programs to stop working or other behavior that can cause more serious problems.

 

Here is a link with more information.

Application Compatibility: Junction Points and Backup Applications

considerate_guy say:

Here's a user-contributed guide to Movie Maker 2011.  The concepts should be relevant to Movie Maker 2012 as well.

 

One way to think of making a movie, is to think of building a house.  For a house you start with your construction materials.  You have a blueprint that tells you how to cut and combine those materials.  The final result is a house.

 

In Movie Maker, your construction materials would be all your source files (photos, videos and music).  The project is your blueprint.  The final result is a completed movie.

 

As you are adding items into Movie Maker, you are revising your blueprint/project.   It is easy to move things around at this point because you are just changing your plan, not the final movie.  If you need to walk away from your project, just save it and return to the latest point.  The project file (.wlmp) will still contain a list of all the source materials, their order, their lengths, etc.  You can continue revising as you like.

 

Then when you are all done, you tell Movie Maker to take the final step of building your house (movie). While it might seem you have been working on a movie, you are only previewing what the final result will look like.  When you tell Movie Maker to save your movie, it does the real work of pulling everything together, following your blueprint (project) and making a single video/audio file that can be played, shared, etc.

 

Again, using the analogy of building a house, let's say you decide you want to change something about  the house.  With the blueprint, you easily know what can be changed, where things are, etc.  You could even make major changes to your blueprint and just build another house to your new specifications.  For example, let's say you wanted a basement, a larger entryway and to remove several walls.  Doing that after the house is built is pretty hard.  But if you are allowed to revise the blueprint and then build another house, it is very easy.

 

So yes, you want to keep your project around, especially if you plan to make any revisions at a point in the future.  While you could go straight to building your movie, just imagine how you'll feel if  you find a typo in a caption or that you wanted a different transition or better music?  You can't change a caption once it is embedded in the final movie, but you can, if you still have the project file. 

 

On to your other question about editing.  The most important features to learn are trimming (setting an in point and out point to a clip) and splitting.  You've probably figured out how to trim from the beginning or end of a clip, but many people overlook the ability to Split a clip.  This is key to working with clips.  For example, let's say you had a clip that was good at the beginning and end, but not the middle (GGGGBBBBGGGG).

 

Play the clip to where the undesirable portion starts and make a split (Video Tools > Edit > Split, or simply 'M' on the keyboard) --> GGGG | BBBBGGGG

 

Make another split at the end of the undesirable portion --> GGGG | BBBB | GGGG

 

You should see 3 parts.  Right-click on the middle part and choose 'Remove' --> GGGG | GGGG

 

Don't worry about splits that remain.  You can put a transition here or let the clips just cut from one to the other.  The final movie will have everything put together so don't worry about them being in parts.

 

Does that help? Is there anything else that I can help with?  Is there anything about the "classic" versions of Movie Maker that you are finding harder to do in the newer version?

Hi CherylBean,

 

1) Is this a refilled cartridge or a brand new cartridge?
2) Which is the Operating System installed on your computer?
3) Have you made any changes on your computer recently?

 

This generally occurs due to three reasons-


a) If the Cartridge head is faulty.
b) The ink and toner levels are not appropriate.
c) If the fuser on the printer is faulty.

 

You may try to remove the cartridge and seat it back in again.

 

Method 1-


You need to check the Ink and the toner levels on the cartridge.
To do so, you need to refer to the article mentioned below-


http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows7/How-do-I-change-my-ink-or-toner-levels


Note: You may use the link mentioned above just for reference if it does not apply to your Operating System.
