mouneshwar r say:

Hi Austin,

There shouldn’t be any problem by turning off Portable Device Enumerator Service. As stated earlier, this enables applications such as Windows Media Player and the Image Import Wizard to transfer and synchronize content using removable mass-storage devices.

For example, if you plugin a USB stick containing pictures, then you might not get the Image Import Wizard which upon clicking will import all your photos to your Windows Photo Gallery. So, the process is not automated and you need to manually import the photos to Windows Photo Gallery. Similarly, when you plugin a USB stick that contains music files, a wizard will pop-up that would ask you to sync the music files with Windows Media Player. You wouldn’t get that wizard and you need to manually sync the music files by opening windows media player and following the sync instruction wizard to sync music files.

If you don’t want to lose this functionality, then here is a work around that lets you disable mobsync.

1.      Click on Start menu and click on Control Panel.

2.      Click on Network and Internet.

3.      Next Click on Offline files.

4.      In the Offline Files window, click on the Disable Offline Files button and click Ok.

5.      A restart is required and after that, it should be disabled and you don’t see it in prosexp.exe

Hope this information is helpful.

3. In the General tab, change the ‘Startup Type' to Manual.
4. Click on the Stop button, and then click on OK.

Please accept this personal viewpoint from the aspect that it is offered. I have no intention of starting a flame war; just to explain the situation as you have described it and I perceive it.

The Microsoft Store price is the official MSRP possibly adjusted by any promotional discount that is currently in place. Promotional pricing structures generally have a time period that they are good for and expecting the same price days, weeks or even months after the promotion has expired is unreasonable, not the other way around. If you could not get the promotional product at the promotional price during the promotional period, Microsoft will have to be the ultimate arbiter in selling you the product at the expired promotional price. FWIW, I don't believe there is much of an existing infrastructure designed specifically for selling products at a reduced price in 'special' circumstances.

When looking at prices on Amazon, you are not buying from Amazon. You are buying from vendors using Amazon as a listing service. These vendors may or may not be authorized to officially sell Microsoft products and some have been known to sell pseudo-legitimate or outright illegal software licenses. It is possible that one or more vendors may have 'stocked up' at a promotional price that was offered earlier and is now expired. In this case, I would make very sure that the license you are purchasing can be used and activated outside of its promotional sales period. Caveat emptor.

That is my take on the subject. I'm not a Microsoft employee, have no impact on Microsoft product sales policy and volunteer my time here. You should regard my statements and advise with that in mind.
