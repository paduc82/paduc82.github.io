Same issues with the monitor not being detected when coming out of sleep, BSODs, sometimes one monitor starts flickering, etc etc. Like you, these issues were temporarily solved by restarting the computer with the dock connected and/or power cycling the dock, but almost always recurred.

- changing my system sleep preferences
- upgrading my cables (I think this helped somewhat with detection failure coming out of sleep)
- making sure my Dock was connected directly to a wall socket (I think this made it perform more consistently; it used to drop USB-A devices every now and then)

NO HICCUPS with constantly connecting and disconnecting the Dock, and the SB2 going to sleep.

- This is a theory, but I think the Surface Connector circuitry/programming to get 2 video outputs from 1 port makes things more complex by not being compatible with all monitors
- Dell monitors are probably the biggest culprits in difficulties with the Dock and you should make purchases carefully and in person. Preferably with your Dock and Surface in hand, like I did for my S3100
- the Dock is amazing when it finally works.

Don't give up! There are plenty of success stories in both individual and enterprise usage of this over-engineered Dock. Try the suggestions outlined in the 3rd paragraph above and let us know how you do.

Thank you for writing to Microsoft Community Forums.

 

We understand your concern and we truly appreciate the time and effort that you have taken to work on the same. However, we would need some more information regarding the issue.

 

  1. Could you please let us know, are you able to see hard drive in Disk Management utility?

  2. Is the hard drive connected internally or externally?

  3. If it is external hard drive, does it get detected when he connects to a different computer?

If the disk is connected internally, we would suggest you to follow the methods mentioned below and check.

Method 1: Check if the disk is well-connected.

 

Please make sure your hard drive is properly connected to your motherboard and power supply. Open your computer case, and make sure you attached a data cable to an appropriate port on the motherboard and a power cable to the power supply.

 

Method 2: Disk Initialization.

 

  1. Right click on Start menu (Windows icon) and select Disk Management.

  2. Check for the drive, if it is listed as unknown or unallocated.

  3. If yes, right click on the respective disk and select Initialize Disk.

  4. Then select GPT and click OK.

  5. Right click on stripped box across the disk and select New simple volume.

  6. Click on Next and assign a drive letter.

  7. Then choose NTFS in the drop down menu across File System.

  8. Click on Next > Finish.

Method 3: Update chipset drivers.

 

It is also possible that driver are corrupted, please visit manufacturer website and try updating chipset drivers.

 

Method 4: Check if your hard drive is displayed in BIOS.

 

Restart the computer and get in to BIOS and check if the hard drive is listed.

 

Disclaimer: Modifying BIOS/ complementary metal oxide semiconductor (CMOS) settings incorrectly can cause serious problems that may prevent your computer from booting properly. Microsoft cannot guarantee that any problems resulting from the configuring of BIOS/CMOS settings can be solved. Modifications of the settings are at your own risk. 

 

Method 5: Connect the hard drive to a different port or use a different cable.

 

  1. Try switching to a different port and check.

  2. If the issue still persists, try using different SATA cable and check. 

If the hard drive is connected externally, please refer

 

  1. Try connecting to a different port and check if it works.

  2. Create a system restore point.

  3. Then open Device Manager by searching for it in search bar.

  4. Right click on the driver under Universal serial bus controllers and select Uninstall.

  5. Restart the computer to complete the process.

If the issue still persist, please visit manufacturer website and try updating chipset drivers.
