hairyfool say:

If you have used the correct term that it will not work. Is it how it is described in the documentation?

DVI-D interfaces only have Digital signals and no analogue VGA signals

For them to both be available it needs to be a DVI-I

Strictly speaking DVI-D connections should be missing the two pairs of pins above and below the flat blade terminal. Occasionally the wrong physical connector has been used as it then allows a fully populated DVI-I cable to be used.

The monitor may be being detected because the DDC Pins are common to both standards

http://nvidia.custhelp.com/app/answers/detail/a_id/221/~/difference-between-dvi-i-and-dvi-d

Note the last paragraph.

robertn83 say:

Ah, this makes sense now. The port on the GPU only has a DVI-D port (along with the several HDMI and DP ports), not a DVI-I. I was unaware that a DVI-D would not work with a VGA cable/monitor. This makes me wonder why there are DVI-D-to-VGA adapters in the first place.

hairyfool say:

By definition you cannot have DVI-D to VGA adaptors. To be a true DVI-D plug at one end it cannot have the pins either side of the flat blade contact. As these are the analogue RGB signals there would not be anything to connect on to the VGA plug. As I said the socket on the board should have those holes filled but if they did it would prevent a number of DVI cables being used. What you have is a DVD-D in electrical terms but DVI-I mechanically. Confusing but don't worry, many others cannot follow the subtleties either.

What you can get is HDMI to VGA converters, not a dumb adapter as they actually render the VGA signal so are active devices. How they work out in terms of cost vs. getting a DVI monitor only you can decide.
