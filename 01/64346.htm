lemp say:

Andrew E's response is a bit confused, so I'll clarify things for you. 

Laptop computers have two batteries -- the main power battery and the RTC (CMOS) battery.  When the computer is turned off, the main battery does not supply any power to the computer, and in particular, it does not supply power to the internal clock. 

For almost all laptops, if the main battery is removed, the laptop will still be able to run if the external power supply/charger is plugged in.  Again, however, when the computer is turned off, no power is supplied to the internal clock.

When the computer is turned on, the internal clock receives power either from the battery or the external power supply.

When the computer is turned off, the RTC battery supplies the power necessary to keep the internal clock running.

Unlike the main battery, however, the RTC battery is not rechargeable.  Thus, after several years, the RTC battery dies and the internal clock will only run when the computer is turned on.  When the computer is turned off, the internal clock resets to some default value making it necessary to reset it properly every time you turn the computer back on.

The service manual for your Dell N5010 Inspiron can be found here:  https://downloads.dell.com/manuals/all-products/esuprt_laptop/esuprt_inspiron_laptop/inspiron-15-intel-n5010_service%20manual_en-us.pdf

Dell calls the RTC battery the "coin-cell battery."

The good news is that the battery appears to be a standard CR2032 battery that you can buy for a few dollars at most drug stores, electronics stores, and the like.

The bad news is that the battery is on the underside of the motherboard and getting to it requires considerable disassembly of the computer.

If you're not comfortable with taking your computer apart -- and that would put in in a large majority of computer users -- I suggest that you take your laptop to a local independent computer repair shop.  The tech there should be able to determine that your problem is, in fact, a dead RTC battery, and if it is, I wouldn't expect that the labor charge to replace it would be too high.

If you want to do it yourself, there are several YouTube videos in addition to the directions in the service manual.

Good luck.

It is easy to see the total physical memory and what it is being actively used along with showing what is hardware reserved. Hardware Reserved represents physical memory addresses that have been reserved by hardware (generally busses like PCI or video cards) and is not available for Windows to use. It is usually small on x64 systems (except servers that do memory mirroring) but can be several 100MB up to 1GB on x32 systems. This means a 4GB x 86 systems can have only 3 GB of accessible memory.

The usable memory may be less than the installed memory on Windows 7-based computers

It is located in your router.  Your modem may be a combination device that has a router inside it.  Or, it could be a separate device.

If you cannot find your network name or the password, you are going to have to reset it.  Contact the supplier of the device for help.

If it is a separate router, you can likely find a "pin hole" in the back that takes a straightened paper clip.  While the router is running, press the wire into it an hold for at least 20 seconds.  Then the router will have been reset to the same as it was when you took it out of the box.  You will then have to go through the installation procedure as if new.  In that procedure you will be prompted to set the network name and password.
