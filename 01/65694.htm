Network Device Network Networking Ethernet Wireless Adapter NIC

langware say: delindajohnson,

Are you saying that when someone brings their laptop to your house and tries to access the internet via your wireless router, and they enter your router's passcode, their connection request is refused?

If that is your question, then first make sure the person is accurately entering your router's passcode (upper and lower case does make a difference .... they must enter the exact pascode with the exact upper and lower case).

If you are sure that the correct passcode is being entered, then your router may have been set up with mac (Media Access Control) filtering.

If you are not familiar with mac filtering .... here is a brief explanation. Every piece of networking hardware has a unique media access control address (assigned by the hardware's manufacturer). If mac address filtering is enabled in ones router, then the router only accepts connections from devices whose mac address matches the contents of a list that is maintained in the router.

Even if a computer uses the correct passcode for the router, if that computer's wireless adapter's mac address is not found in the table of acceptable mac addresses in the router, then the connection is refused. This is a security feature ... even if someone finds out what your router's passcode is, they cannot gain access to your router unless the mac address of their computer's wireless adapter is also in your router's list. (mac addresses can be cloned, but that's a different topic).

So, if mac address filtering is enabled in your router, and your own computer(s) are able to connect to the router, then someone (you or whomever set up your router) has placed the mac addresses of your computer(s) wireless adapter(s) into your router's mac address list.

However, when your friend brings their computer to your house, and enters the correct passcode, if the mac address of your friend's wireless adapter is not in your router's mac address list, then connection will be refused (even if the correct passcode is entered).

If that is the case, then you will need to determine the mac address of your friend's computer's wireless adapter, and then enter that into the mac address list in your router.

Of course, all of the above assumes that the problem you are having is due to mac address filtering being enabled in your router. If that is not the case, please provide more information on the problem you are having.

langware say: delindajohnson,

I just thought of another possible cause for the problem you are having.

Again .... assuming that your friend is accessing your router wirelessly ....

Wireless routers implement one or more specific protocols for sending/receiving. These are collectively known as the IEEE 802.11 standards. Here's a link with explanations:

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IEEE_802.11

Over time, these standards have been improved (faster speeds, better reliability, etc).

Each specific 802.11 protocol is not compatible with a different specific 802.11 protocol. But most router and wireless card manufactures make their hardware/firmware to support the entire set (or a broad range) of 802.11 protocols.

What is the make and model of your router. and how old is it?

It is possible that if your router is older, it only supports older protocols (802.11a, 802.11b, and 802.11g), and if the wireless adapter in your friend's computer only supports the newest approved standard (802.11n), then there would be a mismatch in protocols and then your friend's computer will not be able to connect to your router.

However, as I mentioned, most new wireless adapter cards support all of the old and new standards, so a mismatch in protocols is not likely to be the issue. I only mention it for completeness.

There is another (but even less likely) possibility. There are several newer protocols that are being used in routers and adapters, but have not been finalized yet. These include 802.11ac and 802.11ac. Since these protocols have not been standardized, each router and wireless card manufacture may be implementing them in slightly different manners. Until these protocols have been finalized, I would not use them .... but if one must use them, then be sure the wireless adapter (in the computer) and the router are both made by the same manufacturer. I doubt that this is your problem, but again for completeness I wanted you to be aware.
