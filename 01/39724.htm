It works with encryption keys and encryption certificates, without the certificate the encrypted files cannot be decrypted.  The EFS certificate is dependent on your user account and your password, on stand alone machines the certificate is usually stored locally so if someone can logon to your account they will generally be able to access your encrypted files but you can export and delete the certificate thus preventing anyone from accessing the files.  In a domain environment the EFS certificate can be and is usually stored on the domain controller thus forcing users to authenticate on the domain controller before they can access the files.

The following cannot be stressed enough:  Be sure that you understand how EFS works before you encrypt your files an make sure that you save a copy of the EFS certificate in a secure location OR ELSE CHANCES ARE THAT YOU WILL END UP USING YOUR FILES!  If you do not heed this advice you will end up being another one who will be had by the Delayed Recycle Bin which is the seeming inevitability of data loss if an inexperienced user encrypts his or her files.

For those that are having the problem, this course of action may fix it

1. Start up computer

2. Hold "alt" down after the blue Windows logo disappears and release it when the Start screen appears

3. When you get to the Start screen (has Live tiles on it), type "Optional", click "Settings" in the search menu, and then click "Install optional updates"

4. Click check for updates, and install all of them

5. Now, hopefully, you can boot your computer by just turning it on, and your screen will work perfectly.

6. For safe measure, disable adaptive brightness in the power settings under "Advanced Plan Options"
