Security auditing is enabled by default in Windows 10, so you should expect to see thousands of these events, one after another, and that's completely normal. At the moment I have 32,145 of them.

By itself, the sheer number of events means nothing. They require context to be interpreted. For example, the audits are conducted against policies setup in the Group Policy Editor, so you would need to know what those policies are.

I'm not saying that what you are seeing is totally meaningless. It's just that by itself, and particularly the sheer number, you can't draw any conclusions. You would need much more analysis to understand what you are seeing

You can read about Windows security auditing on the internet, where it's all explained in detail.

What's more indicative is the "intense periodic lag" you are experiencing. If it only happens when you are playing games, that's more likely to be associated with the games, which can make substantial demands on your computer's resources, as you probably know.

What I would do in your place is to either contact the games' technical support or (even better) post your question in their own community forum.

I wouldn't say your computer is 'done for' - not yet, at least. My computer turned 9 in March and it runs Windows 10 really well.

It's tempting to blame everything on Microsoft's updates - they've certainly earned it - but in truth that's not often the case. Windows PCs are built on many layers of hardware and software that come from different places, so it's not surprising (to me) that things don't always work well. Companies like Apple and Google have it easier: Apple, because they build their own hardware; and Google, because so much of the software that runs on a Chromebook is subject to Google's control.

A wise developer once explained to me that all software has bugs, lots of them. There are some bugs a developer knows about and will fix; other bugs that they know about and won't fix - either because it's too complicated or it doesn't affect enough users; and many other bugs that they don't know about and can't even imagine until it actually affects a computer.

There is definitely a reason for the lag you are experiencing; it's only a matter of how much effort you want to spend to uncover it. You might have to experiment with dozens of (carefully considered, not random) potential fixes until you find success and you may never succeed.

When all else fails, a clean install is always a possibility. (A reset is a type of clean install.) A clean install a lot of work if you want to do it really well. But, surprisingly enough, sometimes a clean install actually makes things worse, because it eliminates legacy code that was helping a computer run better. That may be what happened in your case.
