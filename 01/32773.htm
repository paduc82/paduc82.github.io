My monitor is already set as my primary display. This does not affect the actually identity of the display. If an application launches and is configured to run on display 1 then if a different display gets assigned as display 1 that application will run there regardless of your primary display. This also includes the login page and desktop icons.

The problem is that main display is not respected in all situations like the login screen. To resolve this the os needs to communicate display identity some other way like forcing the primary display to always be 1 or allowing user preference to be set identities.

It is easy to replicate.

1. Setup 2 displays and set display 2 as the main display.
2. Turn off Display 2, Reboot and turn on display 2 after the post.
3. Problem 1: Display 1 (non main display) will display login screen. Display 2 will be blank.
4. Now shutdown and turn off display 1 and restart.
5. Display 2 will now be display 1.
6. Open a program or game on display 2 (now seen as display 1 by windows) and configure desktop icons on it.
7. Shutdown, turn original display 1 back on and restart.
8. Problem 2: desktop icons will now appear on display 1 and previous application will launch on display 1 instead of display 2 which is the main display.

This shows that display identities have some priority over "main display". Or at least something in the os or APIs peg activities to monitors based on its identity, and only use main display when a display preference has not already been set. When identites change it confuses which display stuff should launch/show on.

Either displays need a unique, permanent identity regardless of whether other displays are on, or users need a way to setup the preference of which display is identity 1 when more than one monitor is present.

Hope that describes the issue thoroughly.

Why is album art missing sometimes?
 
The Player might not download album art from the database in the following situations:

·    You aren't connected to the Internet when ripping a CD.

·    You're connected to the Internet, but the online database is missing album art (or there's no media information at all) for the CD you are ripping.

·    Your system administrator has prevented your computer from downloading items such as album art from the Internet.

·    You ripped the CD using a program other than Windows Media Player; doing so might prevent the Player from finding a match in the online database.

Finally, if the online database contains incorrect album art for a CD, the Player will download that album art.

To view album art when playing music
 

Do one of the following:

·         Begin playing the song in Now Playing mode. Right-click an open space in the Player (to the left of the Stop button, for example), point to Visualizations, and then click Album art.

If the Player is currently open and you’re in the Player Library, click the Switch to Now Playing button in the lower-right corner of the Player.

Large album art appears in the visualization area.

·         Begin playing the song in the Player Library. If the list pane is not visible, click the Play tab.

If the Player is currently open and you’re in Now Playing mode, click the Switch to Library button in the upper-right corner of the Player.

Small album art appears in the list pane.
