rob koch say:

Though it's very hard to tell, to me this mostly sounds like side effects of the Google Apps Sync for Microsoft Outlook plug-in you must have installed at some point in order to synchronize between your Google Apps and Microsoft Outlooks accounts.

The  complications that likely could have also occurred if you upgraded any of the PC or phone devices mentioned to the new Windows 10 versions while this Google to Outlook sync was also installed and operating are simply mind boggling.  I'm not surprised that you might be confused by this, since even a technical expert in these different synchronization products would likely end up lost in all the possible problems that such a multiple node mesh of competing synchronization systems might create.

The only reason I could even guess at this is because you happened to mention "turned off syncing across my Microsoft and google accounts" in your post above.  If you never mentioned this to the previous helpers here or at Microsoft, there's no way they could have known the mess you've created for yourself.

The problem is that complex programs like the one that performs cross-platform synchronization between Microsoft Outlook and Google Apps are very sensitive to not only any changes in those two back-end platforms, but also any changes to the program versions running on the local system, including upgrades to either Microsoft Outlook and even possibly the operating system.

So though I can't truly hope to help you myself, since all I know about the Google Apps Sync plug-in I've just learned in the last few minutes online, I can at least tell you that it's likely that many of the apparently unexplained access attempts are most likely due to some of these synchronization attempts by one or more of your devices.

Note also that the location technology that attempts to determine where an access may have occurred based on IP address is often badly confused by phones, which in many cases are determined to be connecting from locations tied to cell phone towers that may have been registered by their owners as being located anywhere.  Even the locations my own PC is supposedly accessing from vary over nearly the entire southern half of the state in which I'm located.

As for the access from other countries, it's possible that some of these may have been attempts to hack your accounts, that's true.  However, the question is whether these were actually successful, since for Microsoft at least, I understand that most requests outside a 300 mile radius are now blocked automatically.

As for the Russian data files and any other file confusion, these might also simply be side effects of the mixing up of synchronization between your own accounts and any others that might exist on the various servers involved in performing these operations worldwide.  We have no idea how Google performs these operations within their networks, nor really how Microsoft does theirs as well.  So if any of the proverbial "wires got crossed" somewhere, more likely by software in the digital age, your own accounts may have ended up unintentionally synced in part with someone else by mistake. 

Or maybe it's just unrelated garbage data from something totally unrelated, since at this point it would probably be virtually impossible to back-track all of the various mixed up communications which may have happened over months of this flaky interaction.

Personally, I'd give up on involving the police in this technical piece, since they are typically untrained in such technical complexity and often have no real jurisdiction to deal with the multiple country issues even if they do have some knowledge.

Your problem at this point is how to properly disconnect and completely remove all vestiges of any portions of the Google Apps Sync plug-in that exist on any of your devices, first disabling their operation and then preferably manually removing the plug-in itself.  Once that's complete. try operating your devices for a few days to see whether most of the problems disappear.  Even if some continue, they should be much less prevalent and so easier to explain.

Remember that the core operation of Windows 10 with your Microsoft Account and the cloud applications it contains also perform regular synchronization, so these will likely continue at least some of this same sort of automatic access.  The difference is that at least a knowledgeable Microsoft person should be able to recognize what these might look like, though I wouldn't depend on that if you're dealing with someone in support in India, for example.

At this point I must remind you that all of this is just an educated guess, but based on your descriptions it makes far more sense than some sort of globally sophisticated hacking attempt.  In any case you aren't likely to be able to tell the difference until you get that confusing set of cross-platform synchronization garbage out of the picture.

Good luck cleaning up the mess,

Rob
