tryx3 say:

Jennifer,

I have had these emails from time to time [normally when I am accessing mail from abroad].  It looks like the genuine email to me.

To avoid falling for a fake email of this kind, when you get one delete it then go to the Recent activity page manually [so that there can be no doubt that you are looking in the correct place].

  1. Log in to your MS Account online,
  2. Click on your account name in the upper right corner,
  3. Click on Account settings,
  4. In the long blue banner at the top, click on Security & privacy,
  5. In the upper left, under the heading Account security, click on See my recent activity,
  6. You will normally be made to sign in again at this stage and may also be required to go through additional account verification [a text to your mobile phone or an email to one of your previously declared additional email addresses].

All this will get you to the page that the link in the email would have taken you to.  But this route is not prone to misuse by hackers who send out fake emails.

In your case, as you have not used the account for months, I expect that you will find false logins.  You can report them as fake [This was not me] using the links that appear on the Recent activity page.  You should then also change your password to something that hackers will find harder to break into [and make sure you write down the new password as, if it difficult for hackers to crack, it might also be difficult for you to remember].

See the other replies you've gotten, and let me add the following:

 

RAID 1 (mirroring) is *not* a backup solution. RAID 1 uses two or  more drives, each a duplicate of the others, to provide redundancy, not backup. It's used in situations (almost always within corporations, not in homes) where any downtown can't be tolerated, because the way it works is that if one drive fails the other takes over seamlessly. Although some people thing of RAID 1 as a backup technique, that is *not* what it is, since it's subject to simultaneous loss of the original and the mirror to many of the most common dangers threatening your data--severe power glitches, nearby lightning strikes, virus attacks, theft of the computer, etc. Most companies that use RAID 1 also have a strong external backup plan in place.
