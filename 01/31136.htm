vinod n say:

Hello reg614, 

You can't format Unallocated space. You need to create a partition ( New Simple Volume ) first.  Or extend the partition immediately on the left to incorporate it, using Windows Disk Management. If you want to add the space to the C partition - you need a 3rd party partition manager.  

You can extend the 200 MB partition to the left of the unallocated space to combine it into the 200 MB partition, or create a New Simple Volume with the unallocated space so it will be a separate partition. 

To extend a partition: 

1.    Like in most administrative tasks, you must logged on as an Administrator.

2.    In Windows 7, go to Control Panel --> System and Maintenance > Administrative Tools, or on the My Computer context menu. Select Computer Management. In Vista, if you are prompted for an administrator password or confirmation, type the password or provide confirmation.

3.    Next, scroll down till you get to the Storage section, and in it go to the Disk Management console.

4.    Select the disk you want to extend, for example Drive C.

Note: Extending any volume is fine as long as you have additional unallocated free space on that same physical hard disk . Note that you can also extend the partition beyond the physical disk it is located on.

5.    Right-click the disk and select Extend Volume.

6.    Click Next.

7.    Windows 7  tell you how much space can be added to the existing partition. You can manually enter the amount of space you want to add by changing the "Select the amount of space in MB" values. However note that you cannot enter a higher value than the value already present. As noted above, if you have additional physical hard disks that have unallocated free space, you can add that space up and further extend the partition to span multiple disks. Again, be warned, this configuration is NOT fault tolerant.

8.    The partition or volume has now been extended into the assigned unallocted space.

9.    The process will finish quite quickly, and a reboot is NOT required. 

Coming to Xbox 360 as a media extender, you are right.

Media Center Extenders, such as the Xbox 360, do not work under a dynamic disk configuration. You have to use basic disks. 

Please refer the below article for more details: 

While attempting to connect your Xbox 360 console as a Media Center Extender to a computer, you receive the error message: "Session Terminated" 

http://support.microsoft.com/kb/911123

Savan – Microsoft Support.

The contacts list you see depends on your signed-in status, as shown by the right-most button on the Home tab of the ribbon. 

  • If you are signed in, then you will see the contacts list belonging to the account you used to sign in with.
  • If you're not signed in, you'll see the default contacts list.
  • When you import contacts, it depends on your signed-in status which contacts list they will be imported into.

None of this has the remotest influence on downloading, synchronizing, sending or reading mail messages. Any change in what messages you see is caused by something else.

Your @ntlworld account was set up to use POP3. Unless you configure recent mode, each message will only be downloaded once. When your messages vanished from the inbox, they couldn't be downloaded again because of this idiosyncrasy of Google-powered accounts.  

The chances are that the messages that vanished are still there, possibly having been recovered. Press Ctrl-Y to reveal the entire folder tree, then look for the missing messages in all the folders you see.

Your post was posted in the Outlook.com forum, where it was off-topic.  I moved your post to the Windows "Performance and Maintenance" forum where things like defragmentation are appropriate topics.  Always state your Windows version when you post.

 

Why do you think you need to delete "contaguous" files?  I think the word you want is "contiguous".  After you defragment a disk, you SHOULD see contiguous files.  "Contiguous" means that the files's data are all located in the same area of the disk.  This is the whole point of defragmenting your disk - to make all of your files as contiguous as possible.  I hope you WOULD see a lot of contiguous files following a defragmentation.

 

Note, however, that most modern operating systems handle disk allocation fairly well and the need to use defragmentation tools is nearly non-existent.  You don't worry much about fragmentation any more.
