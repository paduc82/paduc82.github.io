As usual, LemP has provided some good instruction.  However, from your other thread, you indicate you've tried some of this with no success.  So as a contingency, I can offer a backup suggestion...

 

Your router is assigning you IP address 192.168.1.2.  On the assumption that the conflicting device on your network also has this same IP address, here is another way you can get around it without locating that device.  Since I don't know the make/model of your router, I am making a lot of assumptions, but most routers I've worked with allow this...

 

First, connect to your router with a web browser.  Connect to:

   http://192.168.1.1

It likely will ask for login credentials.  Unless you've changed the password, most routers either accept "admin" or nothing for the user name and "admin" or "password" for the password.  Connect to your router manufacturer's website and download the manual for your router to determine the default logon.  Once you connect, there will be a lot of configuration options.  Either in the "LAN" section or in the "DHCP" section, there will be a setting that will specify the range of IP addresses that the DHCP server is allowed to give out.  Usually it looks like:

    192.168.1.____  thru  192.168.1. _____

where you insert numbers in the blank areas.  Apparently, your DHCP server starts at ".2".  What you need to do is change that configuration to start at "192.168.1.100" thru "192.168.1.199".  Click on the "SAVE" button after making the change.  After that, unplug the router for half a minute, plug it back in, and reboot your computer.  After that, if you do another "ipconfig /all" on your computer, you should see that you have an IP address of 192.168.1.100 or thereabouts.  This should "map out" the conflict with the device that appears to be sitting on the 192.168.1.2 address.

 

HTH,

  JW
