Actually - the damage the heat causes can be just as random (more so actually) than drive damage.
 
With drive damage - you would need to access a given part of the damaged drive to cause the freeze - and that would be indicated (most of the time) by you doing some similar action or some similar action taking place in the background.  Also - being Windows 7 - there would be indications (most likely) over time in the event logs [could easily check the event logs for any failures detected.]  Exceptions might be damage to the circuitry of the drive as opposed to the platters - but that damage would have either shown up early in the computers life OR been caused by some external force such as power surge or heat.
 
Heat damage (note the wording - not just over-heating) could happen anytime.  It could be caused by slight overheating of some components over time and could be anything from bursting capacitors as the glue weakens on their tops to a CPU getting overheated as a background process the user doesn't even think about is occurring.  The video card could also be the source - not an uncommon device to overheat and if its fan is also not working to par (and most people may have no sign of this other than the resultant freezes.)  The power supply could even be the source - as it heats up slightly more than it should because of an assortment of issues it may not be able to supply the necessary power to the motherboard and the result may well look like freezing to the end-user.  Even memory is not immune to the damage meticulous over-heating can cause.
 
Of course - drivers can be at fault - but it did not sound like that in this case (although I even suggested looking further into those in my last post) since the trouble was seemingly averted for a short period with a video card driver update.
 
Corruption in files can certainly cause this - and that COULD be attributed to all sorts of things (including hard drive issues) - and perhaps a SFC /SCANNOW could detect and repair such trouble.  The OP is more than welcome to try this solution: http://www.sevenforums.com/tutorials/1538-sfc-scannow-command-system-file-checker.html
 
However, having said all that, running a diagnostics on the drive is a good idea - make sure backups are completed before you do.  Just get the drive manufacturer's diagnostics from their web page and run it - but do it carefully as some features found in these utilities can be damaging to your data.

srfreeman say:

The use of password authentication is not inherently insecure because of anything concerning the number combinations required in guessing the password.

Password authentication is inherently insecure because the password is a shared secret. And, as everyone knows, a secret that is shared is no longer a secret.

Passwords can be given away, stolen or used for access to multiple external systems. Once the password is had, it can be used, by anyone, from anywhere in the world, for access to any external system you have access to. Username/Password combinations, when used as access credentials must be stored by the external systems being accessed. None of this is true concerning Public Key Authentication or certificate authentication, as provided in Windows 10.

You may want to go beyond Wikipedia for a more complete explanation of the TPM. You will need to check with the manufacturer of your device about on board inclusion of the TPM in older kit, the TPM will be included in every PC delivered with a full, genuine edition of Windows 10 as part of the hardware certification program.

Microsoft, your corporate Active Directory or any other Certified Trusted Third Party Authority (see the FIDO Alliance) can and will certify the public keys and/or certificates generated by any Windows 10 device enrolled with them as world unique identifiers of the device that generated them. Again, you may want to delve deeper into this subject, it is beyond the scope of a simple forum and retyping of already provided documentation serves no good purpose.

If not
Right click the current display adapter
Click Uninstall
Check the box that says, "Delete the driver software for this device.”
Exit Device Manager
Restart

Go to Start -> Settings -> Update & security, then Check for updates and install any available updates.

You might need to update the display driver from the manufacturer of your computers website.

If you have an nVidia, AMD ATI video card or Intel HD graphics installed, you can download the latest drivers from their website.

First, determine what type of graphics you have installed.

Press Windows key + X
Click Device Manager
Expand Display adapters
