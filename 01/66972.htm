Hi Vasi K,


Let me answer your questions:

1. In general, if you purchase a pre-made computer or laptop, Windows 10 would usually be bundled together. You will not need to purchase a separate license for it. 
2. There is a way to put Windows 10 on a USB or DVD via the Media Creation Tool, however, if you installed it without owning a Windows 10 license, it will not be activated. Certain features will be disabled and security risks are higher in an un-activated operating system.
3. Since you already have Windows 10 on a different computer, the license for it depends on how it was initially installed. An OEM license is something pre-installed on your computer. Like my answer in number 1, it's packaged together, but you will have no capabilities of transferring it to another computer. A Retail license is what you purchase separately, and will have the capability of being able to be transferred to a different device.

'rickt say:

Windows 10 OEM license keys are tied irrevocably to the hardware they're first installed on.  So, if your Win7 was pre-installed on the old-machine you can't move the license over.

Of course, theoretically if the Editions (Home, Pro, N, Retail, OEM, whatever) are exactly the same on the two machines' keys, then you should be able to do what you want how you want: unactivate the system on the original machine, move the image over, activate using the license that's for the new machine, and carry on.  And whoever gets your old system can run it as well with your old key.

But that's theoretical.

In practice, unless the two laptops are identical models, you'll have a **** of a time trying to get your image install to work right - all the drivers will be different, for starters; it will crash the first time you boot it up, and you'l be spending quite a bit of time in Safe Mode, until Windows figures itself out.  And, of course, unless the activation key types are identical, you'll be stuck in Pirate mode.

For probably the same amount of work, it will be easier to do a fresh install of Windows 10 on the new-machine, activate that (with the new-machine's existing license), then install your programs and move over data.  Some software companies may even be able to tell you the best way to reinstall their programs and move data and configurations over.

Decide if you want to spend your time preparing, or spend your time repairing.

Either way you really need to talk to M$ Support first (it's free), to figure out the Activation issues.

Let us know how it's going, either way, though.  Sounds interesting.
