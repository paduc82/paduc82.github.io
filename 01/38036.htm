When you boot in Safe Mode Windows load a minimal set of drivers and startup programs.

If you can operate in Safe Mode but not in Normal Mode it sounds like something that is being loaded in Normal mode is causing the problem(s) and you can figure out what that is by performing a Clean Boot. 

There can be lots of variables (Services and startup items) in the process of a normal boot that are not part of Safe Mode. 

A Clean Boot is a process of elimination either disabling every non Microsoft Service and startup item and seeing if you can boot normally and if you can, start enabling other Services and startup items one at a time (or in small groups) until you can no longer boot normally, then one of the last things enabled is where to start looking for the problem.  It may take several reboots to find the culprit and you may want to take some notes as you go along to help you keep track of things.

Here is an article that will tell you more:

https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/kb/331796

Just me personally I would think it might be Kaspersky since sometimes when people install it they enable all the options and protections thinking that more protection must be better but sometimes it causes conflicts since it can be a very complicated application if it is not tweaked just right.  Depending on what Kasperky might be doing when it loads and runs, it might be running some major scan or may be afflicted in some way that is consuming all your system resources.  Kaspersky won't load in Safe Mode so maybe that's why things work better in Safe Mode.

If it was me, I would start by temporarily uninstalling Kaspersky (you can always reinstall it again, right?) being sure to use their special removal tool/procedure (don't use Add/Remove Programs) that you can get from here:

http://support.kaspersky.com/common/service.aspx?el=1464#block1

If you don't uninstall it completely, using the Clean Boot you can at least disable all the parts and pieces associated with it (if you can find them) to get it out of the equation with many variables.

  • Check for other wireless interference Active interference
    Active interference is caused when other electronic devices are broadcasting either a wireless signal or some other form of electromagnetic interference. Common causes of active interference are microwaves, ovens, air conditioners, CRT televisions, wireless headsets, baby monitors, other wireless networks, cordless phone systems, and wireless speakers.

Note As a best practice to minimize potential wireless interference, make sure all the cables on the back of your console are gathered away from and are not touching the console.

  • Passive interference
    Passive interference occurs when a wireless signal passes through objects. This causes the wireless signal to be weakened and refracted. Materials that cause passive interference include concrete, glass, lead and copper piping, insulation, mirrors, gun safes, filing cabinets, tile, and plaster. The wireless signal also degrades over distance. Interference can also occur when the console is too close to the wireless router. The ideal distance from the wireless router for the Xbox One console is between 5 and 50 feet.
  • Line of sight
    When dealing with active or passive interference, a critical component to consider is line of sight. Draw an imaginary straight line from your router to your Xbox One console, and try to eliminate as many points of interference as you can along that line. You can do this by turning off or moving any devices along that line that could cause active interference. Or, you can move the console or router to minimize passive interference. Moving the devices outside of entertainment cabinets or desks can often help improve wireless signals.
