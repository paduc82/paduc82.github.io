    I had the exact problem that you're having.  The key issue is the Three partitions on the external hard drives: the UEFI partition, the normal partition and the unallocated space at the end of the drive.

    I struggled with the driver install/upgrade/replacement and eventually removed the VIA usb 3 driver and from add/remove programs uninstalled some other VIA software, then went into device manager and uninstalled the VIA xHCI driver and under hidden devices removed all hidden USB devices. If you have a xHCI hub device, uninstall it as well.  I could not delete the VIA driver, I wasn't given that option.

    This is a desktop PC, but you should do the same with a laptop to ensure Windows installs the correct USB 3 xHCI driver from Microsoft, not the manufacturer.

    On a laptop before doing any of the previous operations, remove the battery so when you power down after removing the dubious drivers it's running on mains power only.

    This is very important!

    Unplug all your external USB drives

    Now step through the procedure to remove the drivers.

    after that you must shut down the machine

    *** Remove the power cord and wait for at least one minute ***

    switching off the power on the PC does not remove power from the motherboard completely, think about how wake on lan works!

    Plug the machine into the power and turn it on again

    go into device manager (Windows key + X)

    expand USB and check the USB 3 xHCI driver.  It should be the Microsoft one 

    Plug your external drives back in.

    At this point what I did was to go into Disk management and make sure the external drives were visible.

    If they are but still show the 3 partitions, don't despair

    There are two things you can try. What I did was delete the main partition on the external drive, back up your data on another computer before you do this if you must keep it. 

    I don't remember quite how I got rid of the UEFI first partition, but I think I deleted it as well.

    Now format your drive as a basic disk make sure it is NTFS.

    Now you will be able to map a drive letter.

    Second option I didn't think about, because the data on my drive was easily replaceable, use a partition management tool like Minitool (google it, it's free),

    delete the UEFI partition, and expand the main partition to the beginning and end of the disk so it occupies the full volume.

    I haven't tried this, but I'm pretty sure it will work, because I'm sure the presence of the UEFI partition is what is preventing you from mounting your drive.

    Again before changing the partition back up your data to another machine.

    I hope this helps other people having this problem
