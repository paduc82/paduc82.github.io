The Rosewill AC1200PCE adapter is not really a Rosewill product, per se.  What Rosewill did was source some adapters that allow minipcie cards to be run out of a desktop PC's PCI-e slot.  Then they picked whatever minipcie network cards were cheap at the time and stuck them in the adapters.  They screwed on a Rosewill heatsink, included an admittedly-nice dual-band antenna, and sold the unit as a single device.

There are multiple batches of these cards.  Not all of them use the same minipcie network card, meaning that the underlying chipset is different from card to card.  Mine is a Realtek rtl8812ae which will not work *at all* in Windows 10, no matter what drivers I try.  Some of the cards shipped with Atheros ath10k chipsets.  I believe those are the ones that may be providing limited connectivity under Win10, though I am not so sure about that.

Anyway, for me (and anyone else like me with the rtl8812ae version) the underlying issue is that MS has no drivers available for this extremely-common wireless ac chipset that came out back in 2013.  That is absurd.  Realtek has also dropped the ball by not offering any drivers, either.  The latest driver I can find for rtl8812ae is supplied by Asus for one of their notebooks, and it is a win7/8/8.1 driver.  It does not work in compatibility mode or anything.  This is a huge problem for MS and Realtek, since there are millions of these little 8812ae cards in notebooks all over the world.  Anyone updating to Win10 on a machine with this chipset in it is gonna get burned, badly.

RGV RAGE, I'd be genuinely interested in knowing what chipset your Rosewill product uses.  If you DO have the rtl8812ae version like me and you can get the OS to at least recognize the adapter, you're further ahead than I.  Win10 won't even recognize the card at all.  It only calls it "network adapter" in device manager, and it can not find a driver for it anywhere (Windows Update or otherwise).
