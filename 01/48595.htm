lemp say:
A few points.

I did understand -- and agree -- that it's ridiculous that Windows users have to resort to 3rd party apps to do things that Windows ought to do on its own -- but that's often the only answer.

Don't hold your breath waiting for a MS Support Engineer to provide you with useful information.  You'll turn blue first.

As I said, it's cumbersome to get special characters this way on a laptop because there isn't a separate numeric keypad.  Thus, as you explained, you have to press yet another key (or keys) to get the alternate meaning of the key (e.g., to get a number 1 from pressing the J key).

Although you can type some special characters by pressing the ALT key and two digits on the numeric keypad (e.g., ALT+21 gives ¬ß), the "proper" way to do this is to use 4 digits, including a leading zero or zeros if necessary).  The 4-digit code for the section sign is 0167.  Thus, pressing ALT+0167 gives ¬ß.  The cent sign is ALT+0162 -- ¬¢ 

You can find the correct 4-digit code from the Character Map applet.  Just select a desired character -- in whatever font you like, it doesn't matter for most normal fonts -- and look at the Status Bar at the bottom of the window.  On the left is the Unicode for the character, along with its name (Unicode Table).  On the right -- for some but not all characters -- is the ALT 4-digit code. 

From a quick look, the easily-found characters that can be generated this way are between ALT+0160 (non-breaking space, just before the "inverted exclamation point") through ALT+0255 (small letter y with diaresis), but there are more if one cares to hunt and peck for a while.
