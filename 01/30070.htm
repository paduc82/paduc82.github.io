Kinecting your Toddler

When my wife bought me an Xbox 360 with Kinect bundle for my birthday a year ago, I didn't really think our then eight month old toddler will be able for use it, at least for years to come.

Since he started walking, at around fifteen months (are boys that much slower to walk than girls?) he wanted to be picked up when we played. Even while he was still sitting down and crawling, he started waving at the TV, and my wife claims this is where he learned to wave.

Since then he gets quite upset when left out. Although the Kinect gets confused if you hold an infant in your arms, it did recognise his hands, and with a bit of luck saw us as one person. He noticed that flailing his arms around, did make stuff move, all be it at random.

We tried out more and more games, mostly games where I didn't need to use both hands. Jumping was also obviously quite cumbersome. At this stage the Kinect was mounted on top of the TV, which is on top of a cabinet, at about 7.2 feet/2.2 meter high. According to Wilson Rothman (2010), the sensor should be about six feet high, playing at around six to eight feet from the sensor. He also states that this makes tracking people of different heights easier, if the sensor is looking down onto the playing area.

I started with one-handed games like Kinect Sports table tennis, and bowling, where I didn't have to move around much. Kinect adventures also worked but requires a lot more physical activity. I thought The Gunstringer would also work well as it allowed playing while sitting down, but my little one wasn't happy with that. Once Upon a Monster held his attention, but due to the different activities needed for each level, didn't really work well either.

Of course, fooling the Kinect into thinking we are one person, and actually having my kid control the Kinect, are not quite the same thing.

As he started standing and walking with more stability, I eventually made him stand upright on an ottoman, while sitting behind him on the couch, guarding him from diving off. As the minimum player height is around 40 inches (Rothman 2010), we had to make him a bit taller.

According to Deborah Percy (2011), the Kinect setup should be done again, for small children. I just left the Kinect at being calibrated for me, as getting him not to move while recalibrating, was not an option. Although the Kinect recognising him improved when standing on the ottoman, his arms were still too short to really actually play games.

We carried on trying different activities, and decided to focus on Kinect Sports bowling, which is the most simple Kinect activity on my shelf. Sitting behind him I still had to pick up the bowling ball for him, and helped him by moving his arm up and down, for the actual bowling. Our bowling effects were only properly recognised 25% of the time, and both of us got a bit frustrated.

Recently I decided to move the Kinect to below the TV, on the cabinet, at 4.72 feet / 1.44 meter, instead of high up in the air, to see if it made a difference. My little one is about 19 months now. To my surprise moving the Kinect made a significant difference, and by now my little one has learned the motions of picking up the ball, and bowling. The Kinect's new position enabled it to actually recognise enough of his arms, to detect when he's picking up the ball. I still had to help with the bowling, but after a few sessions, he got the hang of it, and was bowling without my help, 75% off the time.

What I realised was that the Kinect was still noticing me sitting behind him on the couch. I decided to, for the first time, let him handle it on his own. To my surprise the accuracy improved, and now I only intervene when pressing buttons, as he doesn't quite have the coordination for this yet. Although he doens't really understand how to control the direction of the ball, he has been able to bowl a number of strikes as well. When he gets tired of playing standing upright, he even gets it to work while sitting down on the ottoman. In fact, playing while standing on the floor also now works, although we had to tape a marker for him to stand on, otherwise he will just stand anywhere.

Although playing with the plastic bowling set we got him is still a disaster, we will try Kinect Sports golf, tennis and table tennis next. With golf, he's getting the swinging right, but he still doesn't quite understand that he has to do it sideways, and not facing the TV. With table tennis and tennis, he also doesn't quite yet grasp the concept of action and reaction when there's no physical object involved, like when the ball comes towards him, and having to hit it at the right moment.

All in all, experimenting with the Kinect and toddlers is great fun. It requires fine tuning the physical setup and picking activities that toddlers will pick up easily. It still can't replace a ball kicking session on the lawn, or a tyre swing, but it certainly beats watching TV in terms of teaching them coordination and interactivity. We currently stay in a sectional title unit on the first floor, and on a rainy day like today we can't really walk down to the recreation area either.

Now where did I put my virtual nine iron?!

Make certain your computer is not busy.  Do that by bringing up Task Manager.  If the %  CPU in use isn't around 10%, it's busy doing something.  It could well be that it is still trying to run Windows Update in the background.

Start Windows update and change the setting to NEVER, and by the way, keep it that way for the foreseeable future.  Close Windows Update.

Type services in the text box above the start globe.  Select Services.  Find Windows Update in the list.  Right-click and choose Stop.

go to this web site:  http://wu.krelay.de/en/2016-07.htm There you will find a set of 5 updates.  Download and store each of them in a folder on your desktop.  Close your browser

Now, install each of them.  It could well be that any or many of them will be reported as already installed.  That's OK.  After installing each of these manual updates, restart the computer.

After the restart, start Windows Update.  When the list comes up 5 minutes later, choose the updates you want and proceed to update.

Once you have followed the procedure below, and set Windows update to NEVER, once a month, start Windows Update (WU) manually, check for updates.  When you get the list of “important” updates, right-click and choose hide on EVERY update in the Windows grouping that does not describe itself as Security.  Allow the Malicious software removal tool or any C++ updates.  Do not use ANY optional updates or place a checkmark in any unchecked updates.  Proceed to update.
