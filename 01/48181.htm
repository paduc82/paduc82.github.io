lemp say:
I doubt that the status of the integrated adapter on you mobo is affecting things, but sure, go ahead and disable it in the BIOS.

Similarly, I doubt that installing the beta driver would help, but you can install it and if it doesn't work out use the "driver rollback" feature.

I do think, however, that your ISP is correct: tuning the TCP/IP stack is necessary to get maximum speed. 

If the utility provided by your ISP doesn't help and if the information at DSLReports doesn't help (look at all 3 items in the dropdown list: Tweaktester, Receive window, and more info), then keep after your ISP tech support until you either get something nearer to the speed you're paying for or you decide to stop paying for it.

The problem is that without a second computer connected to your router, you can't tell whether you have a problem in your own equipment (modem/router, cables, Ethernet interface, motherboard, Windows XP) or if there is a problem between you and the ISP's server.  This is a significant problem and if I were you, I'd make an effort to borrow another computer (that has a gigabit Ethernet adapter) and test your LAN.  Otherwise, it's all too easy for the ISP to say, "Our equipment and connection is working just fine; your lack of speed is your fault."

lemp say:
The fact that your Ethernet adapter is theoretically capable of 1000 Mbps doesn't mean that everything else in the path between your computer and the fiber cable (which is what your ISP is basing its "speed" spec on) is similarly capable.

For example: 

  • what sort of cable are you using between the computer and the modem/router ... and how long is it?  Did you make the cable yourself or did you purchase it with the connectors already attached?  You need at least Cat 5e cables, and attaching the connectors yourself is not recommended unless you have a fair amount of experience doing so.
  • what is the make/model/version of your modem/router?  Does it have the latest firmware installed?

Do you have a second computer that also has a gigabit network adapter?  If so, check the transfer speed from computer A through the router to computer B (i.e., what is the transfer speed of your LAN, completely apart from the transfer speed between you and your ISP).  Either use a stopwatch and a large file or use a utility such as LAN Speed Test.

What version TG-3269 do you have?  The current one is V3.

Assuming that you have v.3, you don't appear to have the latest driver (although I doubt that is the problem).  The package labeled TG-3269_XP_beta has version 5.719.0325.2009 of the XP driver.  http://www.tp-link.com/en/support/download/?model=TG-3269&version=V3#tbl_a

Finally, you may want to think about tuning your connection, which is more important for XP than for later versions of Windows: http://www.dslreports.com/tweaks and http://www.psc.edu/index.php/networking/641-tcp-tune
