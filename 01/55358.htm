Now when I continued from here, I didn't get any helpful answers, but this did make me start thinking about the jacks/connectors themselves, rather than the drivers or anything else.

So I discovered that my audio interface (HD VDeck) that installed with my motherboard audio drivers, does not have the options to either disable the front panel jacks, nor the ability to re-configure jacks how you want them. If you want your speaker sound to be coming out of the same jack that it was before, you MUST be able to reconfigure your jacks. Otherwise, the default jack for your speakers, according to your manufacturer's drivers, is in fact not the green jack I was used to, but one of the other ones instead. This other jack, or perhaps even just the front panel headphone or microphone jack will work instead.

So to summarize:

If you have done a clean install of Windows 7 (or a similar version), and you have installed your sound drivers, and re-configured your Windows Sound settings (speakers as default, etc.), then you have done everything up until this point correctly. At this point, you MUST have an audio program such as Realtek, that allows you to re-configure or disable jacks. Otherwise, your speaker sound, by default, is no longer coming from the green audio jack we are used to, but instead another of the rear jacks. You MUST use the other rear jack (most likely the FRONT SPEAKER jack), or one of your front panel jacks, to plug your speakers in instead.

It might look funny, but this workaround will work, unless you get an audio program to re-assign the normal green audio jack.

Error 0x80071AC3: Volume is Dirty - Microsoft Community

damjan josipoviä‡ say:

Error 0x80071AC3: Volume is Dirty

hi, i have posed the question to win 7 forum but never got reply

so i am getting Error 0x80071AC3: Volume is Dirty message. i have tried below cmd prompt but the volume is exfat not ntfs

can you please come up with the solution quickly

Could be the Dirty Bit.

Sometimes when a drive is disconnected without safely removing a file becomes corrupted and Windows flags the drive as "unreliable" (so to speak) by setting a single hex value for the drive as "dirty". Thus "the dirty bit".

To check if this is so, open an elevated Command Prompt and then type " fsutil dirty query m: " (with m being the drive letter for the external drive. This will tell you if the drive is marked dirty.

If it is, then do this: open the Command Prompt and type: " CHKNTFS /X m: " . This will tell Windows NOT to check the drive on the next restart. Then manually restart the computer (keep the external connected).

You should get into Windows without an messages about the drive.

Once in Windows, now run chkdsk on the drive. At the Command Prompt type: " Chkdsk /f /r m: " . Chkdsk should go through all 5 stages of it's test.

When it's done then check the disk by running " fsutil dirty query m: " again. If all went well the Windows will confirm that the dirty bit is not set on that drive.

Sometimes chkdsk can not reset the bit. In than case you can go nuclear and copy all the files off the drive and then format it (that will definitely reset the bit).

steven_b say:

Greetings Guggu74,

To be able to tether your internet connection, or do a File transfer via Bluetooth, you'll need to pair your iPhone with your Surface. 

On your Surface Pro, swipe from the right, click on Settings, then on Change PC Settings. Click on PC and devices, click on Bluetooth and pair your iPhone to your Surface.

Once the pairing has finished, it will go into a Disconnected state (offline or not connected) as it is not currently being used. Close the PC and Devices screen, and return to the desktop.

Swipe from the right, click on Settings, then click Control Panel. Once Control Panel is open, click on Devices and Printers. Inside Devices and Printers, your iPhone should be shown. Click on it, and then a new option on the ribbon will appear Connect using and select Access Point.

This should enable the Bluetooth Internet tethering.
