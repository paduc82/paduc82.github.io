Posting URL Shortcuts on the Desktop =- NOT the Start screen - Microsoft Community

bobgeezer say:

Posting URL Shortcuts on the Desktop =- NOT the Start screen

I finally figured out how to put URL shortcuts - not .exe shortcuts - on the Desktop screen on my Surface Pro 3.

Go to "This PC" and click on "Desktops"  Be sure to open the window all the way.  Then find "New Item"  and select "Shortcut" from the menu.

Then a regular old Create Shortcut dialog appears, and you can type in the "http:// S...etc. URL address and give it a name "EBay", for example, or whatever.

I recommend you add a New Folder first, give it a name, like "Web Sites" or something, then open that folder and put as many shortcuts in there as you like.

When you close "This PC", it will be on your Desktop (unfortunately not where you want it, as I've found no way to arrange the real estate on the desktop screen . . .it's the old "Arrange along the left side" paradigm.  You. Can then double click the Folder, and there will be your shortcuts.

If you don't put them into a folder, they will  just be lined up on the desktop: I have a few of them, with most in a couple of folders.  One reason I recommend this is that if you put a shortcut on the desktop and want to change it or move it or something, it will stop working and YOU CAN'T GET RID OF IT - Win 8.1 refuses to put it into the Recycle Bin, or delete it or modify it.  You have to go back and reconstruct it wherever it was in Desktop - spelled exactly the same way, until it works from the Desktop, THEN you can recycle it or drag and drop it into a folder.  Frustrating: I hope this will save you lots of time if you want to use the Desktop for some things, especially URL shortcuts.

These are 7 things you should already know about a hacked account.

1-Recover your account (if you can)
Important: If you cannot recover access to your account, then it is now someone else's account. It is now the hacker's account. Unless you've backed up, everything in it is gone forever and you can skip the next two items. You'll need to set up a new account from scratch.

2-Change your password NOW
One you regain access to your account, or if you never lost it, you should immediately change your password.

3-Change your security information
While the hacker had access to your account, they may elect to leave your password alone. That way, chances are you won't notice that the account has been hacked for a while longer.

But whether they changed your password or not, they may very well have gone in and changed the recovery information.

The reason is simple: when you finally do get around to changing your password, the hacker can follow the "I forgot my password" steps and reset the password out from underneath you using the recovery information that he collected or set.

4-Check related accounts
5-Let your contacts know

6-Start backing up
One of the common reactions to my recommending you let your contacts know is: "But my contacts are gone! The hacker erased them all and all of my email as well!"

Yes. That happens sometimes. It's often part of a hacker not wanting to leave a trail ‚Äì they delete everything in the account: everything they've done along with everything you've done.

If you're like most people, you've not been backing up your online email. All I can suggest at this point is to see if your email service will restore it for you. In general, they will not. Because the deletion was not their doing, but rather the doing of someone logged into the account, they may simply claim it's your responsibility.

Hard as it is to hear, they're right.

Start backing up your email now. Start backing up your contacts now.


7-Learn from your experience.
Aside from "I should have been backing up," one of the most important lessons to learn from the experience is to consider all of the ways that your account could have been hacked, and then take appropriate steps to protect yourself from a repeat occurrence in the future.
