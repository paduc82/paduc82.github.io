tech-geek say:

By portable I mean the scanner does not install. You simply place the file on your computer or on a form of removable media such as a disc or flash/thumb drive then click to run it. If Avast anti-virus is not already installed on the computer a prompt to download the Avast definitions will be offered after clicking on scan.

Avast does have a rescue cd which can be purchased for around $10. It really is worth the money. Unlike most bootable rescue disc Avast rescue disc boots in Windows PE allowing it to gain full access to the Windows installation. Most other rescue disc boot into some form of Linux. As a result, their ability to detect malware can be somewhat limited. They can still be an effective way to get a non-booting computer to boot.

Another word on aswMBR, that is a very powerful tool and should be used under the supervision of someone with experience with the tool. It is the only rootkit scanner I am aware of that provides the option to re-write the MBR prior to rebooting when a detection in the MBR is made. This can be most useful when dealing with rootkits since some attack the MBR. When removed with other scanners you can be left with a non-booting computer. The "Fix MBR" option in aswMBR eliminates this worry.

 

While malware can prevent the installation and or functions of malware scanners, there are very effective ways to deal with it. It's just a matter of knowing how to do it which really isn't so hard. The key is to stop the process of the malware with a tool such as Rkill or the new Chameleon tool in Malwarebytes. If the malware isn't running it cannot affect the installation or scanning process. Malware is are like any other executable file. They only work when running.

 

 Edit: I would still recommend you do a clean install. Never, as previously stated, do a repair install.
