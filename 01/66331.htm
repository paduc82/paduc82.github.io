arminatorx say:

There is no way to specifically see when an account was used for extending a screen time limit. 

You can however see the general sign in activity of your account if you sign in on https://account.microsoft.com and then in the top menu click on "Security", and on the Security website, click on the activity tile to see your account's sign in activity. 

As for your problem, I wouldn't let the kid access your account. 

Please change your account's pin on the console, and also go to https://account.live.com/Proofs/Manage and change the password of your account. 

While on the account security proofs managing page, also enable Two Step Verification and set up the free Authenticator App on your smartphone if you have one. 

That way, whenever someone tries to sign in with your account on a new device that you haven't set as a trusted device yet, they are asked for a secret code number that only the authenticator app on your phone can provide, or depending on your phone, you might get a request to allow the sign in process. 

That way you keep your kid locked out of your account, and they can't override their play time limit. 

If the kid has your account password or access to your account, it's like putting a lock on the cookie jar, and giving your kid the key to the lock, telling them to not to try to take cookies and to not use the key, because you locked the jar. 

By giving your kids access to your account, you are defeating the purpose of the family settings and child restrictions mechanisms on Xbox Live.

linney say:

Any Custom (or ordinary) System Image that you create (via those earlier links) will remain it the Folder that you place it in until you physically Delete it.  Best not to have this folder stored on a Windows drive in case you ever want to Format the Windows drive.

If you don't run a Dual Boot then System Restore points should remain for a fair old while but not forever.  Dual Boot will scrap all restore points whenever you change your operating system and boot into another operating system.  Otherwise System Restore uses a first in, first out, way of dumping restore points as you run out of the allocated and set size of the restore points folder.  Mind you in this day and age of Terabyte Drives you might have allocated a restore point folder of hundreds of GB in size.


How much disk space does System Restore require?
http://windows.microsoft.com/en-au/windows7/how-much-disk-space-does-system-restore-require

If you create a Recovery Drive via Control Panel\All Control Panel Items\Recovery\Create a recovery drive,  one of the options includes the option to "Copy the recovery partition from the PC to the recovery drive.  Otherwise the Recovery Drive you might create is only useful if Windows fails to start and you want to access the Advanced Startup Repair Options by booting from the created Recovery Drive.
