rob koch say:

The problem with phishing scams has nothing to do with the addressing, it's entirely dependent on the technical education of the user..

That's why I simply stated that I'd never trust anything coming from other than the Microsoft domain, since that's the only known trustworthy domain.

However, that doesn't really matter in the case of consumers and even some corporate users, since few are capable of properly confirming the address field anyway, regardless of what that contains, let alone might take the time to verify this against some list if that existed somewhere.

Why do I say this?  Because I was professionally involved in performing phishing test attacks against organizations ranging from small financial to international business including defense contractors.  Even the executives of some of these could be tricked into providing their company username and password or other internal information to messages sent from what you or I would consider obviously invalid email addresses such as free Gmail or Outlook accounts.

It's possible that this has improved over time, but so have the quality of the attacks.  At the time I was performing phishing attacks, I could typically acquire "clicks" on a URL or attachment included in an email from between 20 to 50% of any organization from a well designed email.  Only rare smaller organizations with highly tuned anti-phishing protection systems and/or individualized training typically did better.

In my various positions in administration and security I learned that the best practice is to simplify, with basic rules like "don't respond to surveys or click links in messages you didn't expect to receive".

Thus my statement to assume that only Microsoft.com addresses are valid sources of such requests from Microsoft.  In my own communications with Microsoft as a past MVP, all internal employees including contract and most other messages came from an internal Microsoft.com address and for those few that didn't, the MVP team mentioned in advance to expect these coming from an external address.
