Sometimes a bad DNS entry will be cached and you will need to either flush the DNS cache to get rid of it, or wait up to 24 hours for it to be dropped from the cache automatically. Given below is a comprehensive step-by-step process to flush the DNS.

I understand your concern. Some PCs may need a long time to download Windows 10, depending on the quality of the internet connection, the amount of data to move and the computer‚Äôs speed.  In some cases, it may take as long as 24 hours, so please be patient.  If your hard drive is still flashing, there‚Äôs a good chance it is still making progress.

 

There are a few points in particular where the progress bar may slow down significantly enough that you may believe that progress has stopped:

  • At the black screen with a blue circle at the 30-39% range, while Windows is downloading all dynamic updates for Windows 10

  • Again at 96%, when Windows is busy backing up your data

  • And at the message ‚ÄúIt's taking a bit longer than usual to set up your device, but it should be ready soon‚Äù

If the upgrade seems sluggish, try disconnecting any USB devices and extended display cables that may be connected.  This has been seen to speed up the download process significantly.

 

Please contact support or start a new thread if it‚Äôs taking longer than 24 hours on the same screen or percentage.  When you do, make note of the CPU‚Äôs make and model, the country you are located in, what screen you are stuck on and the percentage shown.  The agent may ask if they can collect the setup logs to investigate the issue.

shenan stanley say:

If you want to keep Windows 10 in any fashion: not really.  Windows 10 is how it is.  You could likely use some third party products to change the look/feel somewhat - but even that would be limited.

 

What do you specifically 'dislike' about the Windows 10 desktop?  Perhaps there are things you can do - but saying you, " . . . dislike the desktop in Windows 10 . . . " is fairly a broad brush to paint with and making any suggestions would likely miss the mark you are trying to hit.

 

As for going back to another OS...

 

If you upgraded a computer from Windows 7 or Windows 8.1, you can go back to using the OS you were using before.

 

Best method is, of course, to restore from a full system image backup that you might have made prior to changing out the operating system with another.

 

If it has been less than 30 days and you haven't done anything that might have messed with the rollback feature - you could do that.

 

Beyond that, you could get/utilize installation media for the previous OS and use your product key to activate it after a CLEAN (wipe and start fresh) installation.

 

If you got a computer that came pre-installed with Windows 10 - you need to contact those you purchased it from and see if they have anything to offer and you *may* end up returning said system if it really is a deal-breaker for you.
