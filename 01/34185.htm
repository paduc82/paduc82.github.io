a. user say:

Scheduled task?  I doubt it but it could be.

Your system of unspecified make and model is probably receiving a "magic packet" over the Ethernet connection that causes it to spring back into life if your system supports the WOL (Wake On Lan) feature.  It is not an XP "feature".

If you get a magic packet, it doesn't mean that somebody is trying to get into your system - depending on your Internet Service Provider, they may send out magic packets frequently - you just have to tell your system not to pay attention to them.

This will get you started:

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wake-on-LAN

What you can do is before 3AM, unplug your Ethernet cable and wait to see if the system comes back to life as usual or does it not.  If it does not, that is probably what is happening and you can repeat this test as often as you care to wait to figure it out.  That may take a few days.

The WOL feature could be part of your system motherboard, somewhere in the BIOS, somewhere in the network card properites, etc. so you may have to look around for this feature and disable it and wait...

Here are some other general notes that may help you get started:


I can see that you are running XP Pro, SP3, but the Wake on Lan feature is something that depends on if your Ethernet/Network interface is built into your motherboard, you will need to look in the system BIOS setup of the motherboard to see if it is enabled or not.  It doesn't matter what your XP version and Service Pack are or what your CPU is.

On my motherboard in the system BIOS settings under Power Management is a section called "Wake Up Setup Event" and under that are the various ways that one could configure the system to wake up from some event.  Under that section are things like keyboard, mouse and one is "Resume by Onboard LAN" which if I enable that, and the correct "magic packet" comes into the built in Ethernet connection, my system would startup by itself.

If you are using a separate network interface card, the configuration could also be part of the configuration for the card and still be in the system BIOS on the motherboard.

Try not to think of it as an XP thing you need to change - it is more of a hardware thing that has to do with your motherboard and the way you connect to the Internet.

Here is some information I copied from somewhere a long time ago that might help you understand it a little better:

Wake on LAN (WoL) support is implemented in the motherboard of the computer. Most modern motherboards with an embedded Ethernet controller support WoL.

Wake on LAN must be enabled in the Power Management section of the motherboard's BIOS. It may also be necessary to configure the computer to reserve power for the network card when the system is shut down.

In addition, in order to get WoL to work it is sometimes required to enable this feature on the card. This can be done in Windows from the properties of the network card in the device manager, on the "Power Management" tab. Check "Allow this device to bring the computer out of standby" and then "Only allow management stations to bring the computer out of standby" to make sure it does not wake up on every single network activity that occurs.
