lemp say:
You don't need a router for that.

Without a router, you can create a wireless ad-hoc network between the laptop and the printer.  See, for example, http://h10025.www1.hp.com/ewfrf/wc/document?cc=us&lc=en&dlc=en&docname=c00205926

Many more Google hits

The security issue is that XP only supports WEP encryption (easily hacked) in wireless ad hoc mode. On the other hand, nobody is going to get very far attempting to log into your printer. Just make sure that you don't leave the laptop with the wireless ad hoc network active when you're not printing.

If you get an inexpensive wireless router, you can connect the laptop to the router either by an Ethernet cable or wireless.  In this configuration, you'll be able to use the most secure wireless security (WPA2 or WPA) that the weakest link (laptop or printer) supports.

In either case, don't install (or disable) File and Printer Sharing for Microsoft Networks in the Properties of the laptop's network adapter.

Of course, of the three devices, only the laptop is likely to be able to operate without 110vac available, so you'll have to buy some sort of power supply to convert the 12vdc from the truck.  Make sure that you add up all of the power requirements of the devices you likely will use in order to get an inverter/converter of sufficient capacity.  I don't have any specific recommendations, but I do suggest that you research this a bit and don't go for the cheapest product.

Addressing Common Windows 10 Complaints

It seems like every time I turn around, I'm seeing someone else on this board complain about something in Windows 10, and how awful it is because it doesn't work their way.  This is a friendly word of warning to anyone who feels the need to post something like that:

Windows 10, bluntly, is a product of market forces; yes, it's being shaped by feedback from the Windows Insider community and the broader Windows 10 userbase, but it's still a product for the modern OS market.  It's come a long way since December 2014, when I first started using it, and I attribute many of those gains (and some bug fixes too) to feedback from Insiders like me.

But folks, the Windows 10 paradigm is here to stay.  It's remained fairly consistent the entire time I've used it, especially since the XAML Start menu was introduced, and it's not going to change based on the opinions of a small minority of you, no matter how shrill and annoying you want to be.

Don't like the XAML Start menu?  You have Start10 and many other third-party apps to change it.

Don't like the live tiles in the Start menu?  It's the work of about 60 seconds to turn them all off.

Don't like the Metro/Universal apps?  You can uninstall them.  For built-in apps, there's a Powershell command you can use.

Don't like Cortana?  You can turn her off.

Don't like the design aesthetic?  There's an Aero Glass app out there that restores the full and complete Aero Glass experience.  Yours for just a few dollars.

Don't like Windows 10?  Nobody's forcing you to use it.  Downgrade to 7 or 8, or whatever OS you prefer, and stop complaining.

The purpose of the Windows Insider Program is for tech-saavy users to try out advance builds, and provide feedback to help make the continuing development of Windows better.  It is not a platform for you to spew attacks against Microsoft because you dislike aspects of Windows 10.  Threads like "they're not giving me what I want, so it sucks" are highly inappropriate and have no place on this board.

Microsoft, like any other smart business, is listening to all the feedback it gets from its users.  (I assure you, all our voices are being heard.)  The suggestions that they implement are the ones that resonate with the greatest shares of the market, and which pass the "common sense" test.

Why, after everything they've done to develop Cortana for the desktop platform, would they turn around and remove it?

Why, after restoring Aero to the GUI, would they turn around and remove it again?  Or, the inverse, why would they restore the old version from Windows 7, which would slow the OS down and rob Windows 10 of one of its best attributes--being lightweight and fast?

Some of you seem to think that just because they're not doing what you want, they don't care about what any users want.  It's complete and utter narcissism to take that worldview, and I'm sick and tired of seeing it.

For the sake of argument, let's take four of the most common complaints that I've been seeing, and think about what might happen if the changes were made that the posters seem to want:

1)  Remove Cortana.  Forgetting the question of why MSFT would just reverse course and pull out this major function that's integrated across the OS, and that they spent countless dollars and man-hours perfecting for us, what would be the result?  Microsoft will have removed a function that, by any objective measure, has seen heavy use (there's an article that lists the number of questions people have asked, if I can find it I'll link to it--the number is quite large), and make it harder to access for users that like it.  No sane business will ever do a thing like that.

Now clean up the attitudes and let's remember why we're here.

Microsoft is developing a product for us (and by "us" I mean consumers, enterprises, everyone).  They want us to tell them what works and what doesn't, so they can make changes.  Based on my experience using it these last 15 months, I can tell you with 100% certainty they're listening to us.  Features we users have asked for have since materialized in the OS, after all--we have Aero back, and now people like me can have our 4th column of Start menu tiles if we want.

However, logically there's no way they can implement every suggestion they get.  Even a company like MSFT has limited money and manpower available--so they have to prioritize based on a couple of different factors:

  • Is a suggested change practical to implement?
  • Does the requested change represent a significant enough portion of all requests for us to consider?  (Put another way, would the cost of implementing it yield equal or greater benefits?)
  • Would implementing the change cause unintended secondary effects elsewhere in the OS, for which we'd have to test and correct?

This is one reason why I get frustrated sometimes when people demand that all Metro apps be removed, or that Cortana be jettisoned.  Not only are both of those (likely) impractical, I doubt whether there's enough demand for Microsoft to even consider it (not enough people are asking for it).  Implementing those changes would almost certainly also wreak havoc on other parts of the OS, which would require their own testing and development efforts.

They've only got so many resources they can put towards implementing user feedback.  If I were a smart business owner, I'd prioritize the most critical fixes first (those things that cause crashes, or those blasted "video driver stopped responding and has recovered" errors), and mix in the most popular user-requested changes as I went.  That's clearly what Microsoft is doing, and I think that's the right approach.

We should be grateful they're allowing us to continue to have input on the direction Windows 10 takes.  When we provide rational, reasoned feedback, it helps improve the product--but when we start attacking Microsoft like some people are wont to do, saying things are awful and that they suck and so forth, they stop listening (and again, are right to do it).  I know that if I were developing Windows, I'd strike inputs from any user who behaved the way some people around here have behaved; it's a matter of prioritizing inputs from people who want to make improvements, versus those who aren't going to be happy no matter what we do.
