Recovery backup - Windows 10 - Microsoft Community

a. user say:

Recovery backup - Windows 10

I want to create a recovery back-up file  ( system image ? )  on an external hard drive in case recovery is needed at some future time.  How do I do this ?

a. user say:

As with many things in Windows, there's more than one way to do this. I'll give you what I think is the best way, but other people may have different ideas which you should also consider.

The best way to recover from a serious software problem that you cannot fix is to restore a system image. A system image is an exact copy of your C: drive. It's also known as a disk image or simply an 'image.' The C: drive includes Windows and all your software. It also includes all your personal data, unless you've created an additional partition for your personal data.

You can't make a copy of your C: drive by simply copying all the files and folders, because you'll miss the part of your C: drive that enables your computer to boot. Windows can't access that part of the C: drive. Also, as a practical matter, copying everything on your C: drive would take a very long time while it takes just a few minutes to make an image.

Windows 10 has its own image utility but it's in the process of being discontinued and Microsoft recommends the use of third-party image utilities.

When you restore an image, your computer goes back in time to the moment the image was made. It's a fabulous way to backup.

Once you learn how, it's easy to make an image and it takes just minutes. And you can schedule the image to occur automatically, while you sleep, so it's easy to make an image every day. Imagine having a complete copy of your computer that's never more then 24 hours old. That's the beauty of making daily images. Restoring an image also takes just a few minutes.

The image application I use is called Macrium Reflect but there are lots of other imaging applications that work very well. You can read about them on the web and check out the free trials.

a. user say:

Hi Jean,

 

It is not recommended to delete Appdata\Roaming folder as it typically contains settings, temporary and cache files for many of your installed applications. In fact, once you look for the sub-folders under the name, you will find other folders related to different application installed on the computer.

 

If the folder is consuming too much space on the hard drive, it could be due to some of the files related to certain application installed on the computer which you may not know about. You might browse around a bit and find out which one it is in the folder. If any of the file name that looks or related to any application which you do not use or require on the computer, you may go ahead and uninstall the application from Control Panel.

bossdweeb say: "What the system did, it made a copy of the entire roaming folder to the new location but left the old one at the same place."

Is this current folder the 'real' Roaming folder, or the shortcuts in the 'Library' ?
The 'real' folder should have been moved, while a 'Library' would be created in the process.

You could try an experiment (assuming you are not referring to the Libraries).
Either rename the  Roaming folder, or create a new sub-folder in the Roaming folder and

- If you have problems with the software that uses this data then move the files back into the

You wouldn't gain a lot of HDD space just moving the Roaming folder.
I believe most people move the whole User folder, or the music, video and photos folders if they have
a lot of these files, when needing more disk space.
