chriso747 say:

To answer your questions:

1.   It is preferable to install FSX to the root of the drive you would use for it, (e.g. C:\FSX).   The reason for this is because other folders in Program Files (x86) can inhibit FSX accessing its own files more quickly and efficiently.

2.   After you install FSX, you need to start the Sim and take at least one flight, then exit FSX completely.   Then proceed to install SP1 and do as above and exit.   Then install SP2.   Service Pack 1 was the first major 'update' for FSX (you can find out what was involved in that SP here):  

https://flyawaysimulation.com/downloads/files/1950/microsoft-flight-simulator-x-service-pack-1/

Service Pack 2 is also generally installed, but could be optional if you choose.   It mainly deals with Directx10 preview and Multiplayer issues. You can read about it here:

https://flyawaysimulation.com/downloads/files/2796/flight-simulator-x-service-pack-2/

And an important insertion into your fsx.cfg (which the ACES Team forgot to add when developing FSX), is to add the following:  Under [Graphics], add HIGHMEMFIX=1 after the last entry in that section.   This will stop parts of aircraft and some scenery from separating from each other.   There are of course other tweaks, but this is the main (necessary) inclusion.

3.   I can't speak about using a Laptop for FSX, as generally they don't perform well unless they are highly spec'd.   In reference to screen resolution and zoom, I would think you would go into your FSX Settings > Customize > Graphics and look in the Full Screen Resolution box.   Normally, the setting would be that of your Monitor/LT screen, but you can change the setting in FSX Settings to find the one most suitable for you (re:  size, graphics quality, better FPS, etc.).   For example, on my 24 inch monitor, I run 1920x1080x32 as my resolution, which is correct.   However, if I was having trouble with it being harder on my frames, etc., then I could consider going down a couple of settings as a test.   So FSX screen settings (to answer your question) will go as high as FSX will allow (i.e. the screen size options listed in FSX Settings) and remember, it is best to align both your screen settings and FSX settings at the same values.

4.   If you're happy to fly default aircraft (e.g. B738), then you can improve the quality of the engine sounds by downloading (often, free) other more realistic ones than supplied by FSX.   You can also change aircraft behaviour and some aerodynamic features in the aircraft configuration file, but don't touch this unless you know what needs to be done and you are familiar with the four forces that act upon an aircraft and their effect.
