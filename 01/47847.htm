This is very easy. In fact, you don't even need to be fully awake. That's how easy. So don't worry about it.

considerate_guy say:

Look further down in the menu.  There's a Save option which saves your *project*.  A little lower down there is a Save Movie option that saves your *movie*.

 

The project is just like a recipe with all the individual ingredients listed, with all the individual parts remembered.  That's saved in a .wlmp file.  It's essentially a text file and doesn't contain any video, photos or music, just names of the locations on your disk.

 

The movie, on the other hand, is the rendered version of your movie where everything is pulled together into a single movie file (.mp4 or .wmv in your version).

 

So just follow the steps I outlined above about choose the Save Movie option.

stoopsolo say:

The problems seems to have been around for a while and some fairly drastic solutions have been suggested (converting and reconverting the audio data). The problem, however, seems to lie in the ID3 data itself. I have discovered a simple and non-destructive workaround. I use the free program foobar to strip the ID3 data. To do this (without losing the data) add the affected files to your foobar playlist. Select them all and then right-click on them. A menu pops up: choose the option Tagging and then the sub-option MP3 tag types. Select APEv2 and deselect ID3v1 and ID3v2. Then press Update. This strips out the ID3 information but saves it as APE information. Restore the ID3 info by reversing the process. Go back to WMP and you will find you can now add the album art via drag and drop.

Sounds complicated but isn't! Takes very little time and works!

ken blake say:

I can't help with your problem, but I wanted to mention two things:

1. The Del takes you to the BIOS setup screen, where you can edit the BIOS settings. It has nothing to do with upgrading the BIOS.

2. You say you "need to upgrade [your] BIOS," but what ,makes you think you need to? It's very rare that anyone needs to. Certainly don't do it just because it's X years old and there are upgrades available. Although a BIOS upgrade normally goes well, it is not without danger. If something goes wrong while the upgrade is an progress (for example, a lapse in power) you can be left with no BIOS at all, and an unbootable computer.

Do a BIOS upgrade if you need to--because you are having a problem that the new version is known to fix--not just because one is available.

suat.cini say:

It seems that your BIOS post screen timeout is set too low. If this is the case, you can manually reset BIOS using the jumper bridge on the motherboard. Look into the M/B manual to find the location of reset jumper. After resetting, you will have enough time to press the <esc> key or <F2> key or whatever.

Conversely, Asus must have BIOS update programs that work on Windows. You can use that. Asus EZ update is the program you should use, I guess.

Hope this helps.

suat.cini say:

Further to my post above, there is another way to get into BIOS for motherboards which are UEFI-compatible.

Right-click Windows menu button on the Taskbar and click Run on the menu that opens.

In the Run dialog box, type in

shutdown -r -o -t 10

click O.K.

Note: There are blank spaces before the dashes and it is the letter O after the dash.

This will take you to the Advanced Options menu upon restart. if you have a motherboard which is UEFI compatible, then you will find a way to get into the BIOS..

Now click Troubleshoot and then Advanced options and then UEFI Firmware Settings. Your computer will restart and take you to the unreachable BIOS. Note: My translation into English may not be perfect. And your screens may read slightly different.

Hope this helps.
