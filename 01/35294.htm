You might be experiencing this issue because Recycle Bin is corrupted. If your Recycle Bin is corrupted, you can face various problems when using it such as this behavior. Let's repair Recycle Bin by using the System File Checker tool. This tool scans for corruptions in Windows system files and restores corrupted files.

If the issue persists, we suggest that you manually delete the Recycle Bin folder on your computer. Each drive on Windows has a hidden and protected system folder named $Recycle.bin. When you delete files or folders, they are moved to the Recycle Bin folder for holding. If you delete the Recycle Bin folder, Windows will automatically create a new $Recycle.bin folder. However, all previous contents of Recycle Bin will be lost. Make sure to only do this step if you don't have any valuable data in Recycle Bin. Here are the steps:

  1. Click Start.
  2. On the search bar, type Command Prompt, right-click Command Prompt from the results, and select Run as administrator.
  3. Type rd /s /q C:\$Recycle.bin, and press Enter.
  4. If you are using multiple drives or partition, you will have to do this for every partition on your hard disk by replacing C with the corresponding drive letter.
  5. Restart your computer, and Windows will recreate a new Recycle Bin for you.

Basic things to reduce input lag would be running the Xbox 360 (or any capable device connected to your TV) at your TV's native resolution (could be 1080p or 1360x768 for a "720P TV"). This allows the Xbox 360 (which is most of the time FASTER at scaling an image than your TV is) to scale the game's rendering output to match your TV's native resolution so your TV can shut off its internal scaler.

-

Also, turning off "enhancements" that are named features such as "Dynamic Contrast/DC", "DNR/Digital Noise Reduction/Noise Reduction", "Dynamic Color/Live Color", etc. (Usually they are located in your Picture settings under "Advanced settings" or "Expert Control"). These settings are good for enhancing Standard def broadcast signals, but for all HD content that can be produced digitally, it does more harm than good. In this case, causing input delay.

-

Make sure to keep Display Discovery ON unless you see things not working with different apps on the Xbox 360 and in that case, you can experiment with it OFF. If your TV has a BLACK LEVEL setting, for PC-related (Yes the Xbox 360 is a type of PC) content, set to HIGH or NORMAL black level. You then set your Xbox 360's REFERENCE LEVEL to Expanded (Expanded also means 0-255 contrast range, Intermediate is about 8-248 which you should never use, Standard is 16-235). If your TV does NOT have a black level setting, Set it to Standard as it is the SAFE setting for contrast range since your TV might be expecting 16-235 already.

-

The benefit of having a black level setting on an HDTV is that if you have a device capable of outputting the 0-255 full range rather than your HDTV processing the extra 0-16 and 236-255 levels, it can reduce input lag IMMENSELY. Alternatively, sending your TV a "PC" signal (1280x720p) instead of an HDTV signal (720p) will most likely make your TV set for that 0-255 full range, which for that case, you can use Expanded on the 360. You cannot force 720p to be a "PC" signal on the Xbox 360 or any other computer, but you can force it on your TV (look below for input label trick) if your TV supports it.

-

Sharpness and backlight settings are minimal for helping a bit towards input lag if set a bit lower than midpoint, but those "enhancement", black level, and native resolution tips are the most major solution to reducing input delay. Renaming your HDMI input "label" from HDMI 1 to "PC" could also act as a "Game mode" if your TV has such a feature.

-

I know this is a lot to comprehend, but doing these exact things over and over again for different HDTV's helped me figure out the general steps toward reducing input delay.
