cmdrkeene say:

Well it's more dependant on the type of service, IMAP or POP based delivery.  If it's IMAP (or EAS), then your mail is stored on the remote server and the local client only caches copies of it for fast reading (or offline reading).  The benefit to this is that if your computer is broken, stolen, or burns in a fire: none of your email is lost.  You just use another computer, sign into the email program, and 100% of your mail is instantly right back where it was. Same folders and all.  (This is why the Windows 8.1 built-in email app only supports EAS and IMAP: it's built for total resiliency of a mobile device. You'd note that iPhone/Android/Windows Phones do the same. No one wants to lose their lifetime of email if they drop their phone in a puddle).

If you use POP based delivery, such as from an ISP, then your email is not stored on the server.  It is downloaded, stored on your hard drive, and erased from the server (some exceptions: Google allows -- although obviously discourages -- POP delivery, and has options to continue keeping the server copy even when it is downloaded to an email client).

In the case of POP delivery, you need your own backup.

Windows Live Mail stores your email in this folder:

C:\Users\Logon\AppData\Local\Microsoft\Windows Live Mail (where logon is your profile name)

You have several options for backup.  You could schedule a command or batch file to make a copy of that folder.  It could copy it to another hard drive, or to perhaps your OneDrive (or Drop Box/Google Drive/etc) folder for online backup in addition to the local copy).

a. user say:

Separating your software and your personal data (Documents, Pictures, etc.) on separate drives is a very smart idea. That way, they can be backed up on separate schedules, and restoring one doesn't automatically restore the other.

Using an SSD for your software is also smart because your personal data is less likely to benefit from the extra speed of an SSD. You can store your personal data on a HDD.

There are many ways to accomplish what you want to do. You can't do this from within Windows. The easiest way is to make a disk image of your existing installation and restore it to the SSD. Then format the HDD. Now move your personal folders from the SSD to the HDD, and configure Windows to make the HDD the default location for data folders.

It's actually not that simple. There are lots of fine details. For example, your existing HDD is probably the C: drive, and you want your new SSD to be the C: drive. And you don't want to have two bootable partitions in your computer at the same time. Because of all the details involved, you should (as I did) do lots of research about this on the internet before you start. Have a complete plan in front of you. I highly recommend disk imaging software. That way, if something goes wrong you can easily put it back together.
