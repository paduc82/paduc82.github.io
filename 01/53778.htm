a. user say:

That looks very much like a 'translucent selection rectangle' - which is the correct terminology. It shows you that the icon has been selected for whatever command you want to apply to it.

For example, if you were to press the Delete key, the icon would be deleted. If you were to press F2, you could rename the icon. To remove the translucent selection rectangle, just click on any unoccupied place on your desktop.

To create a translucent selection rectangle, click your left mouse button on an unoccupied place on your desktop and hold the button down; now drag the mouse and you'll see the rectangle form.

A translucent selection rectangle is handy when you want to apply the same action to several icons on your desktop as a group. For example, say you want to move a group of icons from the left side of your monitor to the right side: Just enclose them all in a translucent selection triangle so they're all selected. Now you can drag any one of the icons and they'll all move together.

Sometimes it's hard to see the rectangle, especially if the image on your monitor is a light color. You can select a whole bunch of icons and not even realize it! That's why Windows lets you make the rectangle darker, so it will be easier to see. Here's how to do that:

Control Panel > System > Advanced system settings > enter the Administators password > Advanced tab > Performance settings button > Visual effects > Show translucent selection rectangle

I don't mean to frighten RunningLady, but this answer is not quite correct.

Any computer connected to the Internet will have a unique public IP address. 

If you connect directly (for example using a cable modem with no router), your computer itself has the public IP address.

If a computer is connected through a router (either at home or in an Internet cafe, library, or a wifi hotspot), your computer will be assigned a private IP address by the router (and many, many computers will have the same private IP address), but the router's public IP address will be available to any website to which you connect.  And the general location of that IP address can easily be obtained. 

That's how you can communicate with a website:  you connect to a website, it responds to your public IP address, and the router cleverly forwards the response to the private IP address that the router had assigned to your computer.  When several computers are connected to a single router, the router has to keep track of which computer is communicating with which website so that it can properly direct (route) the responses from the websites.

To test this from any computer, do the following:
  1. Open a Command Prompt window (in Windows 7, type cmd.exe in the Start > search box and then press Enter)
  2. In the black Command Prompt window, type
    ipconfig
    and press Enter
  3. In one of the sections of the output will be a line similar to this:
     IPv4 Address. . . . . . . . . . . : 192.168.1.101
    That's the IP address assigned by your router
  4. Now open any web browser and go to this location:
    http://www.whatismyip.com/
  5. You'll see a page with your public IP address and your general physical location

All that said, that information is typically only available to websites that you connect to (notwithstanding shows like NCIS and others where they "trace the IP" of the bad guy).  So unless you connect to a website operated by the person you're attempting to avoid, it's highly unlikely that your IP address will be exposed.  Although ancestry.com (for example) will likely have logs that show the public IP address associated with logons by your userid, that information is not available outside ancestry.com unless you (a) hack into the company's servers or (b) are a law enforcement agency who can obtain that information from the company either by asking (sometimes) or by obtaining a warrant.


For extra security, you can connect to the Internet using a VPN service such as

http://www.strongvpn.com/ or http://www.hidemyass.com/ .  These are readily available and not very expensive.


With respect to email, any received email includes information that shows where it came from.  So unless RL sends email directly to her ex or he has access to a computer of someone else to whom she has sent email, he won't have that information. 


Depending on how the computer is connected to the Internet and what sort of email service is used, the IP address information in the email may or may not reveal a physical location that is at least geographically near the actual sender.  If the email is sent using a web-based service like gmail, the address will belong to the service (e.g., Google).  A "web-based service" means that you connect to a website to send and receive email using a browser rather than using an email program installed on your computer.
