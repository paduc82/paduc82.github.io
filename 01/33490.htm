It turned out to be a permissions issue with my Synology NAS.  The default for shares is to only let the owner of files change ACL Settings.  This was causing lots of problems for me in addition to this problem.

I don't disagree with what James already replied above (depending on the game, it might be treated like a mouse or not) but do want to caution you regarding digitizer scaling.

 

The pen works with a digitizer layer in the screen: unlike a mouse which is tracking 'where you are' on the screen with respect to the desktop resolution settings when you click (e.g. "the pointer is currently this many pixels in width and height from a top corner"), the digitizer tells the Surface "the user clicked *here*" in physical x,y coordinates on the screen.  The digitizer resolution and position is independent of the screen resolution.  Result?  Touches with the pen might be in a totally different location than the game understands!  Newer programs designed for Touch understand that whatever the digitizer or touch overlay grid is indicating may not align with a pixel display resolution, and they know to do that translation.  Older games for which 'touch' wasn't even on the horizon, just never even had to consider such things.

 

I've seen this on older games which I scaled to say 1024x768 resolution, that resolution stretched to fill the screen on the Surface as much as possible (leaving some black, since the Surface has a 16:9 display aspect ratio).  Hovering with the pen results in a pointer moving around in a totally wrong location, whereas with a mouse I (mentally) compensate just fine and put the pointer where I want.  You can also see this on plain old legacy programs running in the Desktop mode of Windows 8 with the 1.5 or 1.25 'scaling' applied, if the application compatibility settings aren't right.

 

As James indicates the Surface does have a USB port (3.0 for a mouse, and the bluetooth installed also works with a wireless mouse.  I play with one of the Surface-edition Wedge mice.  Depending on the game, sometimes I like a very high resolution like the one the actual screen can display (space simulations, Homeworld or Jupiter Incident), sometimes I don't (certain older RTS's like Bullfrog's Dungeon Keeper 2, "roguelikes" like FTL etc.).  Using the mouse means I have the flexibility to set the desktop to whatever resolution is most useful given things like UI element size and the like.
